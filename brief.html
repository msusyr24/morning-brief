Commitment and Consistency

Once we commit publicly, we distort reality to stay consistent. Cults exploit this with small initial commitments (a meeting, a donation) that escalate. Munger's rule: be very careful what you commit to publicly. Once you've said it, your brain will defend it against evidence.

Thomas DeLauer

Two solid, mechanism-forward videos. On inflammation: DeLauer builds a three-compound stack — ginger (blocks NF-κB gene expression AND COX/LOX enzymes simultaneously, unlike NSAIDs which only hit one pathway), curcumin with piperine (boosts glutathione and superoxide dismutase, targeting oxidative damage), and EPA-dominant omega-3s (directly lowers circulating IL-6, TNF, and CRP). He notes the stack needs 6–8 weeks to fully work, unlike the acute relief of NSAIDs. On fasting: a detailed timeline breakdown — 16:8 sits in the early AMPK-activation / fat-oxidation zone and produced meaningful results in a 90-person RCT (3.6% waist reduction, lower LDL/triglycerides, raised HDL, and a 23% improvement in memory strategy scores vs. calorie restriction alone). The big claim: the structural remodeling benefits (collagen, ECM, connective tissue) don't meaningfully kick in until 36–48+ hours, which 16:8 simply can't touch — though you can close the gap on the other four mechanisms with smart protocol tweaks.

Anthony Chaffee MD

Short but pointed: fat-soluble statins cross the blood-brain barrier and suppress the brain's own cholesterol synthesis — a separate system entirely from serum cholesterol, which cannot cross back out. He cites FDA-mandated cognitive warning labels and a 2012 withdrawal/re-challenge study (n=18 Alzheimer's patients) where stopping statins improved cognition scores, restarting them dropped scores by the same magnitude — a reversible effect. His broader claim: B12, D3, vitamin A, choline, creatine, carnitine, DHA, saturated fat, and cholesterol are structural requirements for myelin, synapses, and mitochondrial function — not optional additions.

Japan's Parliament Reclassifies Bitcoin as a Financial Asset

Japan passed legislation formally reclassifying Bitcoin and crypto as financial assets — a meaningful regulatory shift that aligns digital assets with traditional finance frameworks rather than treating them as speculative commodities or payment instruments. This is a real structural development: it expands the universe of institutions that can legally hold and custody Bitcoin in Japan.

🧬 Health & Science

Is the Correlation Between Hypertrophy and Strength Gains Stronger Than We Realized?

Stronger By Science digs into whether muscle growth causes more strength gain than previously estimated. The finding: hypertrophy likely contributes more to strength in novice lifters than older studies suggested — but the relationship is nowhere near as clean as it superficially appears, partly due to methodological artifacts. Practically, this doesn't overturn training advice, but it complicates the common claim that "strength gains in beginners are mostly neural."

💡 Worth Reading

Stoicism is not unemotional

Donald Robertson pushes back against the popular caricature of Stoicism as cold detachment — the Stoics weren't aiming to be "made of oak or stone," but to experience emotions without being enslaved by them. The Stoics distinguished between passions (irrational, overwhelming) and good emotions (eupatheiai) — things like joy, caution, and wishing — which they actively cultivated. Worth reading as a corrective if you've absorbed the "suppress everything" misreading.

Donald Robertson / Substack

New In-Depth Interview: Stoicism and the Art of Living

Robertson joins Andre Duqum for a wide-ranging conversation covering Stoicism, modern psychology, emotional resilience, and practical wisdom. Robertson bridges ancient philosophy and CBT/REBT more directly than most commentators — if you want the Stoic material applied to real emotional regulation rather than abstract virtue talk, this is his wheelhouse.

Donald Robertson / Substack

The Hard Problem is a Category Mistake

Massimo Pigliucci argues that the "hard problem of consciousness" — why there is subjective experience at all — is not a genuine metaphysical mystery but a category mistake, similar to how vitalists once asked why matter was "alive." His comparison to vital force is pointed: once you explain all the functions, there's no residual fact left to explain. Provocative if you've found Chalmers compelling; worth at least one read-through as a counterweight.

Figs in Winter (Pigliucci) / Substack

Toward a Theory of Uni-Context

Tyler Cowen links to a Derek Thompson / Agnes Callard dialogue with an idea worth sitting with: goodness is more context-dependent than badness — there's nothing that's good for everyone in every context, but some things are bad across nearly all contexts. This asymmetry has real implications for how we think about virtue, moral advice, and what it means to live well.
