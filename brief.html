Peter Attia (with Layne Norton)

The conversation drills into whether the industrial processing of seed oils — not linoleic acid itself — is the real harm vector. Norton walks through the hexane extraction process in detail: hexane is a nonpolar solvent used to extract crude oil, then evaporated off via steam at low temperatures; residual hexane in finished oils is typically 0.05–0.5 parts per million, well below any documented toxicity threshold, and hexane danger is from inhalation not ingestion. Norton's bottom line: you'd need to consume roughly 11,000+ kg of oil to reach mild side effect territory from hexane alone. The more important variable he flags is the historical shift in linoleic acid's share of total food availability — from under 3% a century ago to roughly 10% today — which he frames as the better question for chronic disease risk than processing artifacts.

🧬 Health & Science

Seed Oils: The Processing Question (Attia / Norton)

Norton makes a specific, quantified case that hexane residue in seed oils is almost certainly not the harm mechanism: finished oils contain 0.05–0.5 ppm hexane, the danger from hexane is inhalation not ingestion, there are essentially no documented cases of hexane poisoning from food, and you'd need ~11,000 kg of oil to approach mild side effects. His more important point: linoleic acid's share of the food supply has grown from under 3% to roughly 10% over the last century — that population-level dietary shift is the better research target than processing byproducts. The honest takeaway is that "industrial processing = harm" is plausible but currently unproven, while the LA dose question remains genuinely open.

Hair Graying: Three Mechanisms and Partial Reversibility (DeLauer)

A reasonably well-sourced mechanistic breakdown: (1) hydrogen peroxide accumulates in hair follicles while catalase activity drops, poisoning melanocytes; (2) melanocyte stem cell pools deplete under oxidative/inflammatory stress, removing the ability to replenish pigment across hair cycles; (3) mitochondrial dysfunction (low NAD, poor electron transport) is upstream of both, generating ROS that drives the whole cascade. A study in eLife mapped pigment density along individual hairs against self-reported stress timelines and found reversals mid-shaft aligned with stress reduction — suggesting the system isn't fully one-way. Practical interventions with some mechanistic grounding: sleep quality, NAD precursors (NR + CoQ10), sulforaphane (raw/lightly blanched broccoli), and avoiding overtraining without recovery. The C15 fatty acid plug is sponsored but C15 does have emerging longevity research behind it.

Prostate Cancer Screening: PSA Guidelines Are Failing Men (Peter Attia, Ep. 388)

Attia's guest argues the medical establishment overcorrected against PSA overdiagnosis in the 2010s and created a mortality problem on the other side — men presenting with late-stage, non-curable prostate cancer who would have been catchable earlier. The thesis: detection timing is not a minor variable, it is often the entire ballgame, and modern tools (beyond standard PSA thresholds) can meaningfully improve early detection. Worth listening to if you or anyone you care about is in the relevant age window.
