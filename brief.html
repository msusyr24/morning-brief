Availability Bias

What's easily recalled feels more common. Plane crashes dominate the news, so flying feels more dangerous than driving — despite driving being ~100x deadlier per mile. Munger's defense: when estimating frequency, write down actual numbers, don't trust your gut's sense of 'how often this happens.'

🧬 Health & Science

Hold Still: What Does the Evidence Actually Say About Isometric Training for Strength and Hypertrophy?

Stronger by Science does a full evidence review of isometric training — the longstanding assumption that it's inferior to dynamic full-ROM training for strength and hypertrophy doesn't hold up well under scrutiny. The piece examines where isometrics produce joint-angle-specific strength gains versus generalized transfer, and when they may be a viable or even advantageous tool (e.g., injury rehab, tendon loading). Specific mechanisms and study data are cited throughout — worth reading if you program any accessory work or deal with pain-limited ROM.

Stronger by Science

UCLA Discovers First Stroke Rehabilitation Drug to Repair Brain Damage

UCLA researchers report the first drug candidate shown to repair brain damage after stroke rather than just preventing further injury — a meaningful mechanistic distinction. The drug appears to promote neural repair in post-acute phases, when current medicine essentially has nothing to offer. Still early-stage, but the category of "repair rather than prevent" is a genuinely new frontier in neurology.

Bear in Mind the Social Consequences of Your Actions — Epictetus Handbook #36

Donald Robertson's ongoing commentary on the Enchiridion reaches #36, where Epictetus argues that before acting you should consider not just your own role but the ripple effects on your relationships and community. Robertson contextualizes this in the Stoic idea that we are fundamentally social animals whose virtue is expressed through our roles — a useful check against purely self-focused decision-making frameworks.

Donald Robertson / Substack

Physicalism and Consciousness — Parts I & II

Massimo Pigliucci (Figs in Winter) runs a two-part series on what physicalism actually claims about consciousness, why the evidence supports it, and why intelligent people still resist it. Part II addresses the hard problem of consciousness and the most common pushbacks. Not Stoicism directly, but directly relevant to Pigliucci's broader project of grounding philosophy in science — and it's genuinely rigorous without being academic.

Using AI Agents to Build Economic Datasets (DRIL methodology)

Researchers propose "Deep Research on a Loop" (DRIL) — using AI agents to assemble empirical datasets from primary sources by applying a fixed research instrument across a mapped unit space. Constructing datasets from scratch is one of the most expensive tasks in empirical economics; this is a concrete, specific use case where AI meaningfully changes what research is feasible. Complements the separate Marginal Revolution note about using AI swarms to "fight over a literature" until convergence.
