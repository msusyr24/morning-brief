🧬 Health & Science

Cold Water Immersion Meta-Analysis: Performance Recovery Claims Collapse

A new meta-analysis (30 RCTs, 527 participants, 2008–2025) found that cold water immersion — tested across temperatures of 5–20°C, durations of 6–25 minutes, and sports from jiu-jitsu to half marathons — does NOT meaningfully restore strength or jump performance post-exercise. The subjective soreness reduction was real but the creatine kinase reduction (molecular damage marker) largely disappeared after publication bias correction. Critically, partial/lower-body immersion worked "just about as well" as full-body immersion across outcomes — which, as Dr. Mike noted, means "just about as well as nothing." The known downside (reduced muscle protein synthesis/hypertrophy stimulus within ~1 hour post-training) remains confirmed. The only remaining legitimate use case: feeling less sore during multi-game tournament days where performance recovery is less critical than psychological comfort.

Via Renaissance Periodization

Hyperinsulinemia and Bone Quality: The Hidden Fracture Risk

Dr. Isabella Cooper's core research claim: bone density and bone strength are distinct, and hyperinsulinemia (too much circulating insulin, even with normal blood glucose — what she terms ICE: Insulin Compensated Euglycemia) degrades bone collagen quality and ductility even while potentially increasing mineral density. The mechanism is disruption of collagen matrix organization, producing dense but brittle bone (she used the White Cliffs of Dover analogy: highly calcified, easily smashed). The ACCORD trial data is the smoking gun: 10,000+ participants, aggressive insulin therapy to lower HbA1c showed 26.6% more deaths in the higher-intervention group — the intervention being more insulin, not lower blood sugar per se. Her recommendation aligns with the low-carb/carnivore framework: dietary reduction of insulin load is the lever, not pharmaceutical management of glucose numbers.
