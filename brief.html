🎥 From People I Follow

Worth your time:

Nick Norwitz

Norwitz documents his 7-year experiment running LDL cholesterol near 600 and total cholesterol over 700 on a therapeutic ketogenic diet (for IBD remission) — then gets an advanced coronary CT with AI plaque quantification. Result: zero measurable plaque, calcified or soft. He frames this as a challenge to the dogma that "high LDL is always dangerous," arguing instead that context is everything: the same marker can mean broken genetics, poor metabolic health, or an adaptively fat-fueled metabolism. His key claim is the "lipid energy model" — that on a fat-burning metabolism, LDL elevation is a metabolic adaptation, not a pathological one. He also published a peer-reviewed case study on this. Important caveat he makes himself: this does not mean everyone with high LDL is fine — it means LDL without metabolic context is insufficient information.

Video: I Bet My Life Against Cholesterol Dogma (And Won)

GTO Wizard

First-ever GTO Wizard livestream, with Tombos21 and Joey Ingram walking through the newly released PLO solver tool. Key insights surfaced: (1) PLO ranges call far more and 3-bet far less than NLH, because equities run much closer preflop and playing in position matters more than pot inflation; (2) in a 4-bet pot, roughly 86% of the 4-betting range is pocket aces, which means hands like KK are correct folds — a major mindset shift from NLH; (3) the fundamental PLO heuristic isn't "what's my hand strength now" but "what will my hand strength be by the river," with backdoor draws and future blocker utility being the key factors; (4) the NLH heuristic of "check to preflop aggressor" doesn't apply the same way in PLO — there's a much wider leading range on many boards.

Isometric Training: The Evidence Is More Nuanced Than You Think

Stronger by Science's deep dive challenges the common coaching assumption that isometrics are categorically inferior to dynamic resistance training for strength and hypertrophy. The piece reviews the mechanistic and comparative literature, finding that the evidence doesn't clearly support that hierarchy — particularly for specific use cases in programming. Worth reading if you've been reflexively dismissing isometrics or if you're programming around injury constraints.

Stronger by Science

Nick Norwitz: Zero Coronary Plaque After 7 Years of LDL near 600

Norwitz (MD/PhD) documents his own case: ketogenic diet for IBD remission drove LDL to ~600 and total cholesterol over 700 for 7 years. Advanced coronary CT with AI plaque quantification returned zero measurable plaque — calcified or soft. He published a peer-reviewed case study on this and frames the mechanism via the "lipid energy model": in a fat-adapted metabolism, LDL elevation reflects adaptive lipoprotein trafficking, not atherogenic pathology. His key methodological point is that LDL is a marker that exists in individual context — the same number can mean familial hypercholesterolemia (dangerous), poor metabolic health (dangerous), or therapeutic ketosis (apparently not dangerous in this case). He's explicit that this does not generalize to everyone with high LDL.

YouTube — Nick Norwitz

Post-50 Visceral Fat: The Hormonal Mechanisms

Dr. Sten Ekberg's breakdown is mechanistically clean: in men, the aromatase loop is the key — visceral fat increases aromatase, which converts testosterone to estrogen, which further reduces testosterone, which increases visceral fat. Supplementing testosterone without addressing insulin resistance and visceral fat often just feeds more substrate into this conversion cycle. In women, estrogen loss at menopause removes the "directional" signal that routes fat to subcutaneous depots (hips/thighs), so by default fat accumulates as visceral. The cortisol angle is underappreciated: high-intensity cardio (140–170 bpm) spikes cortisol, which preferentially deposits visceral fat — the opposite of the intended goal. The intervention hierarchy he argues for: reduce dietary insulin load first (refined carbs/sugar), compress eating window, then resistance training for insulin-independent glucose uptake during muscle contraction.

YouTube — Dr. Sten Ekberg

💡 Worth Reading

Conscious Introspection Leads to More Self-Deception?

Marginal Revolution flags a finding that internal monologue — the "narration-style" inner voice — is actually a signal you're more likely to be lying to yourself, not reasoning clearly. The claim is that verbal self-narration is more reconstruction than observation. This cuts against the intuitive idea that deliberate introspection produces more accurate self-knowledge, and has obvious implications for how you evaluate your own thinking under pressure.
