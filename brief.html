Feynman Technique

Richard Feynman's test for understanding: explain the concept in simple language, as if to a child. Gaps in your explanation reveal gaps in your understanding. If you can't do it simply, you don't know it. Apply to anything you claim to understand — and watch your knowledge recalibrate.

His five specific tools: nattokinase (10,000+ FU/day) — a Japanese fermented-food enzyme shown in a 1,000+ person study to reduce carotid intima-media thickness by 22% and coronary plaque surface area by 36%; TUDCA/UDCA — a gut hormone that calms arterial wall inflammation by suppressing foam cell formation, and which notably can reverse statin-induced insulin resistance and GLP-1 suppression (published in Cell Metabolism, a finding Norwitz calls scandalously under-followed-up); heart rate variability as a predictor of cardiovascular death, improvable via Zone 2 cardio and omega-3s; berberine, which lowers LDL/apoB by upregulating LDL receptors on the liver ("sucky straws") rather than via statin pathways; and a sex-specific statin note — women have a higher-dose gene for DHA synthesis that statins specifically suppress, making fish oil particularly important for women on statins. Dense, mechanistic, worth a full watch.

GTO Lab

Heavy MTT content this week across multiple clips. Key strategic points: (1) In soft MTT fields, prefer calling over check-raising when EV is equal — soft opponents won't no-equity bluff you, so you want to reach the turn with a stronger, more aggressive range rather than blow equity with a check-raise that lets bad players off the hook; (2) Size up on ace-high boards (A-T-9 rainbow) — population calls the same range at 30% vs 40% because they're inelastic to price and won't fold a pair regardless, so the extra sizing is a free exploit; (3) Post-bubble at shallow depth, you're playing for chips again, not survival — defend wide (including K-2o) when in position and shallow because raw equity realization improves; (4) Kayhan Mokri's heads-up coaching debut: at 40bb, A4s is a pure call vs. a 2bb open — jamming is an overplay, flatting keeps in dominated hands and plays well postflop; (5) KQs at 30bb from the small blind is better as a call than a jam — it "keeps in all the hands we dominate" and plays well postflop, while jamming wastes its blocker value and domination equity. Renaissance Periodization (Dr. Mike Israetel)

Several clips with real training substance. Most important: rows are not lat exercises — the lat never reaches its halfway stretch point in any row variation, and since lengthened-position stimulus drives disproportionate hypertrophy, rows are primarily a back-thickening (spinal erectors, mid-back) exercise. Overhead work (pulldowns, pull-ups, straight-arm variations) is where lat training actually happens. On exercise critique from the "grade this workout" video: when training twice a week, take everything to failure since fatigue management is a non-issue; weighted pull-ups should be underhand to hit biceps; for bench with limited equipment, narrow grip + elbow tuck hits both chest and triceps. Thomas DeLauer

Two videos worth pulling from. On sardines as a whole-food supplement stack: sardines deliver EPA/DHA in phospholipid-bound triglyceride form (~30% more bioavailable than standard fish oil capsules); CoQ10 is embedded directly in fatty muscle tissue (food form absorbs better than capsules, which are highly variable); and critically, sardines contain dietary nucleotides — structural units of DNA/RNA that gut lining cells (which replace every 3-5 days) cannot efficiently synthesize themselves and cannot be purchased as a supplement. Two tins per week is his protocol. On kiwi and sleep: a study of 24 subjects found two green kiwis 1 hour before bed improved sleep onset by 35%, waking time after sleep onset by 29%, total sleep time by 13% (no control group caveat); a separate RCT (74 students, 4 weeks) confirmed improved subjective sleep quality vs. pear. Caveat: if metabolically unhealthy, the glucose spike can cause a 2-3am wake. His protocol: push to 2 hours before bed, buffer with cottage cheese, stack with tart cherry juice (34 extra minutes of sleep in one RCT) and magnesium glycinate.

ModernWisdom (Chris Williamson)

The Alex Hormozi clip on push vs. pivot decisions is the most substantive: the trigger for pivoting is when a fundamental assumption you began with has been falsified by feedback — not when you feel bad, not when progress is slow. Feeling bad alone is not a signal to change course; it's only a signal to update if the core hypothesis is disproven. Hormozi's framing of description vs. explanation is sharp — "Johnny stole because he's dishonest" is circular (dishonest = person who steals); the actual explanation is behavioral reinforcement history. Most people's mental models of others are descriptive labels, not explanatory mechanisms, which is why reality keeps surprising them. The Steve Stewart-Williams clip on sex differences is well-structured — gamete size (sperm vs. egg) is definitional across virtually all sexually reproducing species, making biological sex binary not by assertion but by definition; individual sex differences are modest and overlapping but stack into a "multivariate" picture. He argues science disproves old sexist claims rather than vindicating them. The "Every Man Needs a Quest" clip is a clean formulation: hopelessness = perceived lack of options; anxiety = many options, no priorities; a quest resolves both.

Peter Attia

🧬 Health & Science

Statins, UDCA, and the GLP-1 Connection (Nick Norwitz)

Norwitz highlights a finding published in Cell Metabolism: statins reduce levels of UDCA (a bile acid gut hormone), and UDCA reduction is one mechanism by which statins cause insulin resistance and suppress GLP-1 — the same hormone pathway targeted by Ozempic and Wegovy. The implication: supplementing UDCA (starting at 250mg/day, titrating up) may reverse a significant portion of statin-induced metabolic dysfunction. He frames this as a scandalously under-replicated finding given that statins are among the most prescribed drugs in the world. Women may be particularly affected due to a sex-chromosome-linked gene that amplifies statin-omega-3 interactions. (Full video above)

Dietary Nucleotides: The Unsupplementable Gut Nutrient (Thomas DeLauer)

Gut epithelial cells replace themselves every 3-5 days and have limited capacity for de novo nucleotide synthesis — yet no nucleotide supplement exists. Whole small fish like sardines (eaten with bones and organs intact) are among the few concentrated dietary sources because the nucleotides are embedded in intact cellular structure, not just muscle tissue. A Journal of Gut foundational study found that supplying dietary nucleotides improved liver regrowth, immune responsiveness, and gut morphology — all contexts of high cellular turnover. This is distinct from fiber or probiotic benefits and essentially unavailable from processed food. (Full video above)

Spider Venom Kills Varroa Mites Without Harming Honeybees

Varroa mites are the leading cause of honeybee colony collapse globally, and current chemical treatments also harm bees. Researchers found a specific spider venom compound that is lethal to the mites but benign to bees — a potentially significant agricultural biocontrol breakthrough. The specificity of mechanism (targeting something in mite biology not shared with bees) is the interesting science here.

💡 Worth Reading

Beyond the Inner Citadel — Massimo Pigliucci

Pigliucci engages Ludwig Edelstein's critique of Stoicism: the "inner citadel" (the idea that your judgment is always fully within your control) may be an overreach — a philosophical comfort that can become a form of self-help escapism rather than genuine ethical engagement with the world. Worth sitting with as a counterweight to the "control what you can control" mantra that gets repeated without friction.

Figs in Winter

Stoicism Is Not Unemotional — Donald Robertson

Robertson pushes back on the pop-Stoicism reading of Marcus Aurelius as stone-faced and affectless. The Stoics distinguished between passions (irrational, destructive emotional reactions) and good emotions (eupatheiai — rational, well-grounded feelings like joy, caution, and wishing). Marcus himself writes with grief, warmth, frustration, and longing. The goal isn't emotional suppression — it's cultivating the right kind of emotional response. A useful corrective for anyone who's turned Stoicism into emotional numbness.
