Mental Model · Physics/Economics

Equilibrium

Systems settle into balances where forces cancel. Markets find price equilibrium; ecosystems find predator-prey balance; workplaces find culture equilibrium. Shifting equilibrium requires changing underlying forces, not fighting symptoms. Munger applies this to regulation: treat symptoms and you'll return to equilibrium; change incentives and the new equilibrium shifts.

🎥 From People I Follow

Worth your time:

Nick Norwitz

Norwitz, who carries two copies of the APOE4 gene (elevating his Alzheimer's risk 10–15x), makes the case that DHA is the single most important nutrient for brain health. The key mechanism: APOE4 carriers burn through DHA 77% faster than non-carriers, depleting the fatty acid that makes up 40% of the brain's gray matter. Counterintuitively, younger APOE4 carriers actually show increased DHA uptake into the brain — a compensatory superpower that disappears with age, which is why DHA trials in people who already have Alzheimer's show no benefit (too late). His practical thesis: APOE4 carriers must maintain very high DHA intake throughout life to offset accelerated degradation, and should avoid industrial seed oils (which shift the brain toward pro-inflammatory cPLA2 pathways) while prioritizing EPA/DHA-rich whole foods. He monitors his omega-6:3 ratio via OmegaQuant.

Renaissance Periodization (Dr. Mike)

A new meta-analysis (by Menno Henselmans et al.) of 11 RCTs found no statistically significant effect of carbs vs. fats on muscle growth when calories and protein are held equal — directly challenging the longstanding bodybuilding dogma of maximizing carbs. The likely reason: resistance training rarely depletes glycogen below the ~40% threshold where anabolic signaling actually diminishes, so you don't need "infinity carbs" to stay above that floor. Dr. Mike's practical takeaway is that mixed diets (roughly equal carb/fat split) work just as well as high-carb approaches, and individuals should experiment in monthly increments to find their personal sweet spot using strength, pumps, workout energy, sleep quality, and sex drive as proxies. He also confirms creatine's status as the most consistently validated supplement — it drives muscle growth, speeds set-to-set recovery, and appears to benefit brain and nervous system health.

Jared Alderman — Brian Hastings

Alderman interviews six-bracelet winner Brian Hastings, who brings a thoughtful GTO-vs-exploit debate to the fore. Hastings argues for RNG-ing early-game-tree decisions (like AK facing an UTG open at 90bb) where he's genuinely uncertain which action has an EV edge, but agrees that river decisions are almost always better made deterministically. The sharpest exchange: Hastings explains why pure check-jamming a hand like K10 on the river creates an over-bluffing problem a good opponent can exploit with a pure call, whereas mixing keeps the opponent uncertain. Alderman pushes back with a "be unbalanced in unpredictable directions" theory — that forcing opponents to guess whether you're bluffing or shutting down is equally hard to play against as balanced play. Both agree the limiting factor is execution: achieving true balance in-game is nearly impossible, so the real question is which deviation from balance your skillset best exploits.

GTO Wizard

GTO Wizard's first-ever live stream launches their new PLO solver tool with Tombos21 and Joey Ingram. The core theoretical insight surfaced: in PLO, calling pre-flop dominates three-betting because equities run much closer with four cards, and playing out of position in inflated pots is far more costly. In four-bet pots, ~86% of the time the four-bettor holds aces, which makes broadway hands like KK near-automatic folds — a jarring shift for NLHE players who think KK is the nuts. Post-flop, the emphasis shifts entirely from current hand strength to river-nut potential and blocker utility: having backdoor draws and future blockers to the nuts matters far more than flopped pairs or sets.

🧬 Health & Science

Isometric Training for Strength and Hypertrophy — What the Evidence Actually Says

Stronger by Science takes on the conventional wisdom that isometrics are inferior to dynamic training for strength and size. The article argues the evidence doesn't support that claim — isometrics can produce meaningful strength and hypertrophy gains, with the tradeoff being joint-angle specificity (strength gains are most pronounced near the trained angle). Practically relevant for injury rehab contexts or as a complement to dynamic training when range of motion is limited.

Stronger by Science

Growth Hormone for Musculoskeletal Repair — Peter Attia

Despite compelling mechanistic reasons to expect GH to accelerate adult tissue repair, Attia concludes the clinical evidence hasn't delivered on that promise. The piece is worth reading as a case study in mechanism-vs-outcome thinking: a plausible biological pathway doesn't guarantee a useful clinical intervention.

Peter Attia MD

Stress Disrupts Hippocampal Memory Integration

A study in Science Advances finds that stress specifically impairs the hippocampus's ability to integrate overlapping events and draw inferences across memories — meaning stress doesn't just make you feel bad, it degrades a specific cognitive function critical for learning and decision-making. This is a mechanistic finding with real implications: high-stress states actively impair the kind of pattern-recognition and flexible reasoning that skilled performance depends on.
