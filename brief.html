🎥 From People I Follow

Worth your time:

Huberman Lab

Dr. Eddie Chang (UCSF neurosurgeon) breaks down the speech-language distinction clearly: speech is the mechanical audio signal produced by the vocal tract; language is the broader cognitive system extracting meaning, grammar, and syntax. The most striking segment covers Chang's BRAVO clinical trial, in which a man paralyzed for 15 years — reduced to pecking out letters with a stick on his baseball cap — had electrode arrays implanted over his speech cortex. A machine-learning decoder trained on 50 words, combined with autocorrect-style language modeling, allowed him to produce words and sentences directly from brain activity for the first time. Chang notes vocabulary is expanding and the 50-word ceiling is an engineering constraint, not a fundamental limit. A secondary finding worth noting: primitive vocalizations like laughing and crying are controlled by evolutionarily older, separate brain areas — patients with speech-area damage can still laugh and moan.

Video: Essentials: The Science of Learning & Speaking Languages | Dr. Eddie Chang

Two Bills to Codify a Strategic Bitcoin Reserve Draw Bipartisan Support

Rep. Nick Begich (R-AK) and Rep. Matt Van Epps (R-TN) separately introduced legislation to permanently establish a U.S. Strategic Bitcoin Reserve — moving it from executive order to statute. Bipartisan co-sponsors signal this is no longer a fringe position. If passed, it would lock in federal BTC accumulation policy regardless of which administration is in office, a structurally significant shift in how the U.S. government treats Bitcoin as a reserve asset.

🧬 Health & Science

Hold Still: What Does the Evidence Actually Say About Isometric Training for Strength and Hypertrophy?

Stronger by Science takes a serious look at the claim that isometric (static contraction) training is inferior to dynamic training for strength and hypertrophy. The article argues current evidence does not support that assumption — isometrics produce meaningful strength and size gains, particularly at the trained joint angle, and may have underappreciated applications in rehab, tendon loading, and as a complement to dynamic work. The piece goes into mechanisms: time under tension, motor unit recruitment patterns, and the specificity principle (strength gains are angle-specific, which is both a limitation and a potential tool). Worth reading if you program accessory work or deal with joint issues that limit range of motion.

💡 Worth Reading

New Paradigms Won't Save You — Astral Codex Ten

Scott Alexander's essay pushes back on the idea that adopting a new intellectual framework — a new paradigm, a new theory of everything — is the fix for whatever epistemic problem you're facing. The core argument (based on the title and ACX's typical structure) is that the bottleneck is usually not your framework but your willingness to apply it rigorously and sit with uncertainty. Directly relevant to the mental models / clear thinking cluster of interests — a useful corrective to the "one more framework" habit.

Astral Codex Ten

The Evolution of Consciousness — Massimo Pigliucci (Figs in Winter)

Pigliucci — a philosopher of science with a biology background — takes on the question of whether consciousness is a biological phenomenon and, if so, how and why it evolved. This sits at the intersection of Stoic philosophy (which had a sophisticated theory of the rational soul) and modern neuroscience, and Pigliucci is one of the few people credentialed to handle both sides. Worth reading alongside the Chang/Huberman piece above on brain-machine interface, which raises similar questions about what "mind" actually is.

Figs in Winter (Substack)

How General Semantics Was Used to Treat WWII Veterans — Donald Robertson

Robertson profiles Douglas Kelley, a WWII psychiatrist (also featured in the recent Nuremberg documentary) who applied General Semantics — a precursor to CBT — to treat traumatized veterans. General Semantics, developed by Alfred Korzybski, is a framework for distinguishing language from reality and reducing the emotional charge of irrational self-talk. Robertson draws a direct line from this approach to modern rational-emotive therapy and Stoic practice. Concrete historical grounding for the "philosophy as therapy" idea that runs through Stoicism.

Donald Robertson (Substack)

Alexander Grothendieck Revolutionized 20th-Century Mathematics

Quanta profiles Grothendieck, arguably the most radical mathematical mind of the 20th century — a man who rebuilt the foundations of algebraic geometry from scratch using pure abstraction, then walked away from mathematics entirely and lived as a recluse. Beyond the math, the biographical arc is striking: extreme intellectual power, total commitment, eventual rejection of the entire enterprise. A good long read if you're drawn to first-principles thinkers who go all the way down.

🤖 AI Capability Watch

Virgin Atlantic Used OpenAI's Codex to Ship a Mobile App Rewrite on a Hard Holiday Deadline

Virgin Atlantic used Codex — OpenAI's autonomous coding agent — to complete a full mobile app overhaul on a fixed holiday travel deadline, achieving near-total unit test coverage and zero P1 defects at launch. This is one of the first well-documented cases of an AI coding agent being used for a high-stakes, deadline-bound production software project at a major company, not just developer productivity experiments. The "zero P1 defects" claim in particular is worth scrutinizing — if it holds up under examination, it shifts the conversation about where AI coding agents can be trusted.
