Renaissance Periodization (Dr. Mike Israetel)

Dr. Mike makes the strongest case I've seen him make for high training volume, citing a landmark study showing that up to 52 working sets per muscle per week produced better hypertrophy than 5, 10, or 20 sets — a finding that upended his own earlier "maximum recoverable volume" framework. The key nuance: systemic fatigue limits real-world application (training your whole body, not just one muscle in isolation), time constraints matter, and individual muscle groups vary enormously in their volume response. His practical guidance: if a muscle group fails to get pumped or sore from your current volume, it almost certainly needs more sets, not less. He also covers shoulder training specifically — pressing builds front delts and triceps but side delts require dedicated lateral work (upright rows, cables, dumbbells) at high volume and frequency (2–4 sessions/week, 5–7 sets per session). On the Magnus Midtbø analysis: Magnus rows as much as world's strongest men at 165 lbs bodyweight, explained by superior nervous system efficiency for vertical pulling, potentially favorable tendon insertion points further from the joint (mechanical advantage), and unusually large brachial artery diameter (4.4mm) enabling faster blood flow and waste clearance — which also explains why he's not jacked despite being freakishly strong.

Ken Berry MD (with ICU Dr. Roger Seheult)

ICU physician Dr. Seheult provides a calm, evidence-based breakdown of the Andes hantavirus cruise ship outbreak: the Andes strain is the only hantavirus documented for human-to-human transmission, and even then requires close-quarters prolonged exposure — not the airborne efficiency of COVID or measles. The cruise ship created an anomalous pressure-cooker environment (cold weather, recirculated air, confined space) that enabled spread from a single sporadic infection, which is not how this virus normally behaves. Key mechanistic point: hantavirus specifically suppresses the interferon response (your innate immune system's first weapon), similar to SARS-CoV-2's MAC-1 gene — which is why feeling sick (the interferon side effects) is actually a sign your immune system is fighting back. As of May 8, every confirmed case traced to the ship, with no secondary spread outside that population.
