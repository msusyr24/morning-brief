🧬 Health & Science

UVB Light, Testosterone, and Mate Attractiveness (Nick Norwitz)

The mechanism is cleaner than the headline suggests: skin functions as an endocrine organ, releasing hormones into the bloodstream when hit with UVB, which then reach the hypothalamus and trigger a downstream sex hormone cascade (GnRH → FSH → LH → testosterone/estrogen). In rodent studies, UVB increased testosterone substantially in males and enlarged ovaries in females; in a controlled choice experiment, males consistently preferred UVB-exposed females with no visible differences — suggesting pheromone-level signaling. Human phototherapy trials (UVB 2-3x/week for ~10 sessions) significantly increased passionate love scale scores in both men and women. Practical upshot: maximize skin-exposed outdoor time in summer months, when solar angle gives UVB the most direct atmospheric path.

Coffee's Protective Effect Against Sedentary Mortality (Thomas DeLauer)

A 13-year NHANES study of 10,000+ adults found that in coffee drinkers, the excess mortality risk from sitting 6-8 hours/day was essentially neutralized — not just reduced, but statistically eliminated — compared to non-coffee-drinkers at the same sedentary level. The active mechanism appears to be chlorogenic acid (not caffeine): it improves endothelial nitric oxide availability, blunts postprandial glucose absorption by ~7%, and preserves insulin sensitivity over time. A 12-week RCT comparing chlorogenic acid-enriched vs. regular coffee found 5.4 kg vs. 1.7 kg weight loss — a striking difference for a single dietary variable. The implication for desk workers: coffee quality (high-polyphenol, less heavily roasted) matters more than most people assume.

Isometric Training: The Evidence Is Less Clear-Cut Than You Think (Stronger by Science)

A full literature review concludes that the conventional wisdom — isometrics are clearly inferior to dynamic training for strength and hypertrophy — is not well supported by current evidence. Strength gains are joint-angle specific but not exclusively so, and hypertrophy signals from sustained isometric contractions are more comparable to dynamic work than typically assumed. The practical case for isometrics is strongest in injury rehab contexts and for adding training variety without cumulative joint stress.

Seed Oil Processing: Hexane Is Probably Not the Mechanism (Peter Attia / Layne Norton)

Norton walks through the hexane extraction process in detail: boiling point is 69°C, residual levels in finished oil are 0.05–0.5 ppm, hexane toxicity is primarily via inhalation not ingestion, and the calculated oral dose required for even mild effects is over 11,000 kg of oil. His conclusion: hexane is almost certainly not the mechanistic villain in seed oil harm. The more defensible concern remains the linoleic acid content and the century-scale shift from ~3% to ~10% of total food availability as LA — a change that predates hexane extraction becoming standard practice.
