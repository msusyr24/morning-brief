🎥 From People I Follow

Worth your time:

GTO Wizard

Tom Boss and Joey Ingram work through the unified math behind pot odds, MDF, and alpha — making the case that these are two sides of the same coin rather than separate concepts. The core insight: on the river, the percentage of time you bluff should equal the pot odds you're laying your opponent, and tiny deviations (just a few combos) cause optimal bluff-catchers to swing from always-call to always-fold. They demonstrate this with a node-locked GTO Wizard sim on a 5-4-3-3-4 board where villain bets 150% pot — showing how 37% bluff frequency makes every intermediate hand exactly indifferent, and how shifting that frequency by even 2–3% collapses the entire calling range. Blocker effects (preferring hands that block villain's bluffing range) are shown to matter at the margin but not be the primary consideration.

Videos: The Math Behind Every Profitable Poker Decision

Jared Alderman

A sharp conversation about why macro exploits backfire and what real balance actually looks like in practice. The key argument: "balanced" doesn't mean moderate on every decision — it means being genuinely capable of going either direction (range call or range fold) at any moment, so opponents never know which version they're facing. The analogy used is political moderates who hold extreme-in-both-directions views that average out, vs. people who are mildly middle-of-the-road on everything. Strong players are patient — they play mostly well-executed hands and only pull the trigger on an exploit when something actually strikes them as strange, rather than forcing reads. The trap identified: players who are hyper-aware that information exists and feel pressure to act on it every hand, which makes them easier to beat than someone who simply waits.

Ice Water Drowning Survival — JACC Case Study

A 2025 case report in the Journal of the American College of Cardiology documents survival of a young patient from ice water drowning — notable because the extreme hypothermia that causes death in warm-water drowning actually provides neuroprotection in cold water, extending the window for resuscitation. The clinical implication is that cold water drowning victims should receive more aggressive and prolonged resuscitation attempts than warm water victims.

Reason Without Virtue Is Just Calculation

Massimo Pigliucci argues that the Enlightenment project of separating rational inquiry from ethics was a category error — reason without virtue produces optimization, not wisdom. The Stoic framework is that logos (reason) and arete (virtue) are inseparable: you cannot reason well about what to do without having cultivated the character to recognize what matters. A compact and well-argued piece relevant to anyone thinking about AI alignment or the limits of purely technical decision-making.
