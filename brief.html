GTO Lab

Brian Kim analyzed a short-stack big blind spot (~15bb) where the player holds a full house on a paired, flushed board. The key decision point: facing action when the opponent's range is weighted toward overpairs and sets — hands that beat a full house — while also containing some flushes and air that might hero-call a shove. Kim's conclusion is that shoving is clearly correct, not close: the full house isn't strong enough to trap, checking and calling is equivalent in risk, and the only realistic equity comes from getting looked up by flushes or ace-high hero calls. The framing — "you're just going to get stacked here" so you might as well be the aggressor — is a useful heuristic for marginal-but-committed hands in short-stack spots.

🧬 Health & Science

Isometric Training: The Evidence Is Better Than You Think

Stronger by Science published a thorough review challenging the widespread assumption that isometric (static contraction) training is inferior for building strength and muscle. The actual literature doesn't support that claim — isometrics produce meaningful hypertrophy and strength gains, with specific advantages in joint-angle-specific strength development and lower joint stress (relevant for injury rehab or maintenance training). The piece breaks down what the evidence actually shows about mechanisms, where isometrics do and don't transfer, and how to program them. Worth reading if you've dismissed iso work as accessory filler.

Ezetimibe and Brain Health: A Misclassified Drug?

Nick Norwitz argues ezetimibe (Zetia) is primarily understood as a gut-based cholesterol drug but actually crosses the blood-brain barrier and may interrupt protein misfolding pathways implicated in Alzheimer's and neurodegeneration. A case-review study found eightfold lower rates of Alzheimer's and related dementias in ezetimibe users — not RCT-level evidence, but a mechanistic hypothesis with enough signal to warrant attention. His personal n=1: LDL dropped 288 mg/dL (56%) as a side effect while the primary goal was neuroprotection. This is a drug most people associate only with cholesterol management; the brain angle is underreported.

Nick Norwitz

Mango vs. Granola Bar: A Controlled Trial on Metabolic Effects

A 24-week RCT in pre-diabetic adults (published in Foods) randomized participants to 300g fresh mango daily vs. a calorie-matched granola bar. The mango group had fasting glucose 18.5 mg/dL lower, stable HbA1c vs. increased in controls, improved HOMA-IR and insulin sensitivity, and lost 1.4% body fat — despite no caloric deficit being the goal. Proposed mechanisms: mango polyphenols (mangiferin, quercetin, gallotannins) + fiber reshape the gut microbiome toward butyrate-producing bacteria (reducing inflammation and improving insulin signaling), and mangiferin specifically appears to activate AMPK and upregulate pyruvate dehydrogenase — mimicking effects of AMPK activators, shifting cells toward glucose oxidation and away from fat storage. The food matrix — not just calories or macros — drove the difference. DeLauer's practical recommendation: time mango in the evening with protein (not fat), post-workout if possible, to stack AMPK activation from exercise with the polyphenol effect.
