Chesterton's Fence

Before removing a rule or system you don't understand, figure out why it was put there. Every rule was someone's solution to a problem. If you can't articulate the problem, you might be about to recreate it. Munger applies this to business reorganizations: understand the old structure first.

GTO Wizard (Tom Boss21 & Joey Ingram)

A deep Poker Math 101 walkthrough that actually earns its runtime. The core insight: pot odds and bluff frequency are two sides of the same coin — on the river, the percentage you should bluff exactly equals the pot odds you're laying your opponent. They demonstrate this live with GTO Wizard node-locking: shift a range to be even slightly too value-heavy and every bluff-catcher folds; shift it slightly too bluff-heavy and every bluff-catcher calls. The line is that sensitive. They also walk through how MDF (minimum defense frequency) is just alpha inverted — alpha asks "how often must villain fold for my bluff to profit?", MDF asks "how often must I defend to deny that profit?" The blocker section at the end is worth isolating: scarcity of bluff combos, not just raw blocker strength, determines which hands you should hero-call with.

Nick Norwitz

Solid deep dive on Heart Rate Variability as a practical health marker. Key data points: in an 808-patient post-MI study, low HRV predicted a 5.3x higher risk of death over follow-up years; a separate study found each one-standard-deviation drop in HRV associated with a 47% increased risk of a first cardiac event. HRV reflects vagal (parasympathetic) tone — high HRV means your "brakes" are responsive, low HRV means you're flying with broken brakes. Practical improvement levers with the strongest evidence: zone 2 cardio (most reliable baseline builder), HIIT dosed correctly (~30% of aerobic volume), and omega-3s (EPA+DHA at 3,000–4,000mg/day, shown in RCTs to boost HRV via membrane fluidity and vagal tone through brain-stem accumulation). Caffeine acutely suppresses HRV by pushing sympathetic tone — don't measure HRV post-coffee. Norwitz also recommends measuring HRV immediately upon waking, before phone or caffeine, for the cleanest baseline signal.

Thomas DeLauer

Two substantive videos this period. On fasting: the "10 commandments" framework has some genuinely mechanistic claims worth noting — black coffee (and even decaf) triggers autophagy via AMPK/mTOR inhibition within 1–4 hours per a Cell Cycle study; salt satisfies the same nucleus of the solitary tract circuitry as sugar (Journal of Clinical Hypertension data showing inverse sodium/sugar relationship); and the metabolic threshold for meaningful fasting doesn't begin until 16 hours, when ketone concentrations can more than double between hours 15–20 per a Metabolism study. Andrew Huberman (ft. Andy Stumpf)

The centerpiece tool from Stumpf's book Drown Proof is the Concern vs. Influence exercise: draw a line down a page, list everything occupying your waking hours on the left (concern), then try to fill the right side (influence). Almost everything lands left; the only thing you can honestly put right is yourself — your thoughts, responses, time management. Stumpf does it monthly now; Huberman does it weekly and calls it a "game changer" for cutting rumination. The deeper point: the exercise forces the recognition that you have no control over what happens to you, only over how you respond — a clean Stoic frame delivered practically. Stumpf also makes a strong case for phone screen time under 1 hour/day (he got to 30 min in January) and notes that forcing Instagram to the laptop makes it sticky enough to close quickly.

🧬 Health & Science

Peter Attia: Breast Cancer Screening Framework

Attia's core argument: the problem isn't lack of tools, it's a failure to connect known high-risk women to better screening. At least 9% of women meet criteria for breast MRI, yet actual utilization is 0.4% — a pure execution failure, not a scientific gap. His practical framework: every woman should have a formal risk assessment by age 25 (not because imaging starts then, but to determine whether you're truly average risk), annual mammography beginning at 40 for average-risk women, and MRI added for high-risk women. The key underappreciated risk factors he flags: breast density (reported on mammogram but not discoverable until first imaging — though ~60–70% heritable, so ask your mother), dense breasts both increase baseline risk AND reduce mammogram sensitivity, and family history captures more than just BRCA mutations — it reflects lower-penetrant variants and shared environmental factors no single gene test covers. He explicitly pushes back on USPSTF's biennial-only recommendation, noting its population-efficiency framing doesn't optimize for individual outcome.

Copper Drug Restores Memory and Clears Alzheimer's Proteins (Monash)

Researchers at Monash University report a copper-transport drug that restores memory and clears toxic Alzheimer's-associated proteins in preclinical models. The mechanism targets copper dyshomeostasis — an underexplored pathway in neurodegeneration distinct from the amyloid-clearance approaches that have repeatedly failed in trials. Early stage, but the mechanism novelty is worth tracking given the long history of failed Alzheimer's drug development.
