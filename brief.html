Thomas DeLauer

Creatine absorption depends on the SLC6A8 sodium-dependent transporter — without adequate sodium, creatine pools in extracellular space, causing water retention with minimal intramuscular or cognitive benefit. The fix is simple: take creatine with electrolytes. On dosing: 7g is the general sweet spot for muscle and fat loss; upper body may respond better to a short loading phase (10–20g for a few days) due to higher transporter density there, while lower body responds better to a steady 5–7g. For cognitive benefit (especially under sleep deprivation), 20g/day for 7 days has the most evidence — one study restored executive function in sleep-deprived subjects. For immune/recovery effects, 10g+ is the relevant range. Creatine monohydrate remains the gold standard; HCl and ethyl ester show no meaningful absorption advantage once sodium is addressed.

🧬 Health & Science

Prostate cancer screening: why current PSA guidelines are failing men

Peter Attia's core argument: the timing of detection is the determining factor between life and death in prostate cancer, but current guidelines systematically delay screening or discourage it entirely in younger men — the window when catching it actually changes outcomes. The episode covers why PSA alone is an imperfect tool (sensitivity/specificity issues), and what modern adjuncts (MRI-guided biopsy, 4Kscore, phi test) can do to reduce both overdiagnosis and missed high-grade cancers. Practical upshot: don't wait for your GP to offer this at 55; advocate for baseline PSA earlier and push for the modern diagnostic stack.

Peter Attia

Vitamin C may slow biological aging via iron-sequestration mechanism

Nick Norwitz covers new data (published in a top-tier journal) showing that aging cells accumulate iron, which activates a molecular "aging catalyst switch" — and that vitamin C binds to and inhibits this switch. In a primate study, 30 mg/kg of vitamin C slowed biological aging and reduced brain shrinkage on MRI. The human equivalent dose is ~2.1g/day, achievable cheaply via supplementation. This is a mechanistic finding, not just an association — worth watching for follow-up replication.

Nick Norwitz

Is Industrial Processing the Real Problem With Seed Oils? | Layne Norton & Peter Attia

Layne Norton walks through the hexane extraction process in detail: hexane is a nonpolar solvent with a low boiling point (~69°C), removed via steam in minutes, leaving residuals typically under 0.5 ppm — and hexane toxicity in humans comes from inhalation, not ingestion. His calculation: you'd need to consume ~11,340 kg of oil at once to approach even mild side effects from hexane. The more substantive concern he raises isn't hexane but the dramatic increase in linoleic acid as a share of total food availability — from under 3% a century ago to roughly 10% today — and what chronic high-temperature reuse of these oils (restaurant fryers) might do to oxidation products, which is a different and more open question than pure linoleic acid.

💡 Worth Reading

Scientific datasets are riddled with copy-paste errors

A systematic look at how copy-paste errors propagate through scientific datasets — not fabrication, just human error at the spreadsheet level — and how rarely they're caught before publication or downstream citation. Relevant to anyone who relies on meta-analyses or population-level health data, since the errors compound when datasets are combined. A good reason to weight mechanistic evidence and well-controlled RCTs over large observational datasets.
