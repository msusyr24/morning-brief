GTO Wizard

Tom Boss and Joey Ingram work through the unified math underlying pot odds, MDF, and alpha — demonstrating that these are two sides of the same coin rather than separate concepts. The core insight is that on the river, the bluffing frequency you choose is the pot odds you lay to your opponent, and that equilibrium is razor-thin: shifting just a handful of combos from bluffs to value (or vice versa) causes the entire bluff-catching range to swing from "always call" to "always fold." They node-lock GTO Wizard to show this live — adding a few boats and removing a few bluffs makes every bluff-catcher indifferent at 35% rather than 37%, causing them all to fold. The second-meal implication for players: in practice, humans almost certainly can't detect 35% vs. 41% bluff frequencies, so the edge comes from knowing your own frequencies are correct, not from trying to read opponents at that resolution.

Peter Attia

Attia and Tom Dayspring do a deep dive on brain lipidology, leading with a fact most people miss: the brain contains 20–25g of cholesterol — roughly 20x more than the liver — and operates an entirely separate lipoprotein system from the periphery. There is no exchange of cholesterol between plasma LDL/HDL and the brain; the blood-brain barrier blocks it completely. This directly refutes the common fear that lowering LDL with statins or other agents "starves the brain of cholesterol." The brain synthesizes all its own cholesterol locally, primarily via astrocytes, and the primary cholesterol carrier in the brain is ApoE — the same gene whose variants (ApoE2/3/4) are the dominant genetic risk factor for Alzheimer's. The conversation then pivots to how ApoE4 impairs cholesterol recycling within the brain, which appears to be a central mechanism in neurodegeneration.

🧬 Health & Science

Retatrutide: Triple Agonist Pharmacology and Real-World Data (Nick Norwitz self-experiment)

The mechanism that makes retatrutide distinct from tirzepatide is its addition of glucagon receptor activation, which increases energy expenditure rather than just suppressing appetite. This explains a paradox seen in anecdotes: some users get hungrier on reta because the metabolic rate increase hits before appetite suppression, leading to a net caloric deficit driven by thermogenesis rather than reduced intake. Norwitz lost 12 lbs over a month eating ~3,000 cal/day with no activity change. The published body composition data at higher doses (8–12mg) shows ~38% of weight loss is lean mass — comparable to first-gen GLP-1s — directly contradicting the "muscle-sparing" narrative circulating in fitness media. The resting heart rate increase (from ~42 to 65–70 BPM) and HRV drop are real physiological effects whose long-term cardiovascular implications remain unknown; Norwitz is appropriately cautious rather than alarmed, noting that exogenously induced HR elevation may not carry the same risk as the same elevation from underlying dysfunction.

Brain Cholesterol Is Metabolically Isolated from Plasma Cholesterol (Peter Attia / Tom Dayspring)

The blood-brain barrier prevents plasma lipoproteins (LDL, HDL) from entering the brain — the brain synthesizes 100% of its own cholesterol locally, primarily in astrocytes, and the key transport protein is ApoE rather than ApoB. The brain is the most cholesterol-dense organ in the body (20–25g vs. 3–5g in the liver). The clinical implication: the fear that aggressively lowering LDL will harm the brain is not mechanistically supported; peripheral LDL levels have essentially no direct relationship to brain cholesterol homeostasis. ApoE4 appears to impair the recycling of cholesterol within the CNS — neurons can't efficiently accept cholesterol from ApoE4-carrying astrocytes — and this cholesterol trafficking failure is increasingly seen as a central upstream mechanism in Alzheimer's pathology.

Isometric Training: Evidence Does Not Support Inferiority to Dynamic Training (Stronger by Science)

A comprehensive review finds the standard dismissal of isometrics ("inferior for strength and hypertrophy") lacks robust support in the literature. Isometrics produce joint-angle-specific strength gains that may be undervalued in general programming, show meaningful effects in tendon health and pain management (patellar tendinopathy, for example), and some protocols (yielding isometrics at longer muscle lengths) produce hypertrophy comparable to dynamic training. The practical case for their inclusion: they allow high-force production without the eccentric damage load, making them useful in high-frequency programs and early-stage rehab.

Resistant Starch: Mechanism, Quantification, and Gut Microbiome Effects (Dr. Sten Ekberg)

The butyrate produced from resistant starch fermentation is the primary fuel for colonocytes and goblet cells, which produce the mucin layer protecting gut integrity. Feeding Faecalibacterium prausnitzii and Roseburia intestinalis via resistant starch indirectly supports Akkermansia muciniphila — chronically low in people with insulin resistance and obesity. The "second meal effect" is real and measurable: propionate from RS fermentation signals the liver to reduce gluconeogenesis, lowering the glucose response to the next meal by 10–20 mg/dL in metabolically healthy people. Critical math often missed: cooking and cooling 100g of rice does not yield 100g of resistant starch — only a small percentage converts, so treating it as a free pass to eat more refined carbs is counterproductive. The research-supported dose for metabolic benefit is 20–30g/day of RS for 8–12 weeks, typically delivered as raw potato starch (not cooked).

Noise infusion banned from statistical products published by Census Bureau

The Census Bureau has banned "noise infusion" — a differential privacy technique used to obscure individual data in aggregate statistics — from its published products. This is a significant methodological reversal: noise infusion was adopted as a privacy-preserving tool, but critics argued it degraded data quality in ways that harmed researchers and policymakers. The debate cuts to fundamental tensions between privacy engineering and statistical utility in government data.
