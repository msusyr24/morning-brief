Mental Model · Economics/Productivity

Pareto Principle (80/20)

Roughly 80% of effects come from 20% of causes, across almost every domain. 80% of sales from 20% of customers, 80% of crime from 20% of criminals, 80% of your happiness from 20% of your activities. The skill: identifying the 20% before wasting effort on the 80%.

GTO Wizard

The main video is a dense, practical PLO study guide built around what the presenter calls the "SIP method." The core argument: memorizing solver frequencies is the wrong approach — you need heuristics and patterns, not statistics. Specific insights worth noting: (1) Button opening range is basically "when in doubt, open," while early position is "when in doubt, fold" — and cutoff range serves as an anchor for multiple other spots. (2) Don't mix when the EV difference is tiny; only obsess over spots where the mistake is 20 BB/100 or larger. (3) For flop sizing, divide all boards into "locked" (paired, monotone, straight) where you use small sizes (1/3 or 1/4 pot) and "regular" boards where half-pot is the default. (4) In group analysis, find the threshold hand where strategy changes — everything stronger continues, everything weaker folds — and ignore the rest. The short on polarized betting reinforces that a checking range + large polar bet often outperforms a merged small-bet strategy on certain boards.

Jared Alderman

Kevin Rabichow makes a nuanced distinction: betting for information is different from checking for information. His argument is that deviating from GTO by betting thinner than you normally would (to "find out if you can get away with it") is poor logic — because when it works, the opponent just folds, which tells you little. The more defensible form of information-gathering is through checks, where the opponent's action reveals range. The specific hand discussed: J6cc in a single-raised pot, middle pair no draw on a paired board — GTO default is check, but Rabichow bets against a specific opponent archetype expecting fold equity from a front-loaded aggression profile.

🧬 Health & Science

Nick Norwitz on Longevity Peptides (Epitalon, Thymic Peptides, Pinealon)

The most substantive health content this cycle. Norwitz traces the science from a 2003 double-blind human trial (Neuroendocrinology Letters) where adults over 60 treated with thymus and pineal gland extracts for 6 years showed a reported 4x reduction in mortality vs. controls, plus improvements in blood pressure and physical functioning. The mechanistic paradox he works through: epitalon activates telomerase (which theoretically raises cancer risk), yet trial participants showed *lower* cancer incidence — his proposed resolution is that thymic peptides simultaneously restore adaptive immune surveillance, enabling the body to catch aberrant cell growth that telomerase activation might otherwise accelerate. Pinealon, a small orally bioavailable peptide from the pineal gland, appears to improve sleep architecture and REM — with anecdotal reports (including from surgeons and Andrew Huberman) of doubling REM sleep. Norwitz tried it himself and reports notably more restorative sleep. The underlying science on thymic aging is also worth noting: CT-measurable thymic involution in adults is an independent predictor of cardiovascular disease, cancer, and all-cause mortality — the thymus is not the vestigial organ it was thought to be.

Stronger by Science: Hypertrophy–Strength Correlation Revisited

New analysis suggests hypertrophy contributes more to strength gains in novice lifters than prior research indicated — but the relationship is still far from deterministic and becomes much weaker as lifters advance. The practical implication: for beginners, building muscle and building strength are more coupled than previously thought, which supports prioritizing hypertrophy-oriented training early. For intermediate/advanced lifters, the correlation weakens considerably, meaning strength gains require more skill-specific practice rather than just adding muscle.

An Entire Herculaneum Scroll Has Been Read for the First Time

The Vesuvius Challenge has achieved what many considered impossible: a complete first read of a carbonized scroll buried by the eruption of Vesuvius in 79 AD. These scrolls were unrollable without destruction, and the project used AI-assisted X-ray tomography to virtually "unroll" and read the text. The scale of the cultural recovery potential here is enormous — there are hundreds of scrolls still unread from the Villa of the Papyri, potentially including lost works of Epicurus, Stoic philosophers, and other ancient thinkers.
