Mental Model · Psychology

Availability Bias

What's easily recalled feels more common. Plane crashes dominate the news, so flying feels more dangerous than driving — despite driving being ~100x deadlier per mile. Munger's defense: when estimating frequency, write down actual numbers, don't trust your gut's sense of 'how often this happens.'

🎥 From People I Follow

Worth your time:

Thomas DeLauer

DeLauer's sugar video has a genuinely useful mechanism: refining strips polyphenols that naturally inhibit alpha-glucosidase (the gut enzyme that converts carbs into absorbable glucose) — the same pathway pharmaceutical diabetes drugs like acarbose target. He cites a Nutrition Journal study showing dates (66% sugar by weight) produced a GI of 43–55 even in insulin-resistant subjects, comparable to healthy controls — a striking finding, since refined sugar predictably spikes IR people harder. Coconut sugar contains 4.7g of inulin per 100g (a prebiotic fiber), and a European Journal of Nutrition crossover study showed replacing 20–30% of sucrose with oligofructose/inulin produced a measurably reduced postprandial glucose and insulin response. Monk fruit mogrosides, by contrast, aren't recognized as carbohydrates at all — five RCTs showed 12–22% reductions in insulin response vs. sugar controls, plus an anti-inflammatory effect (inflammatory cytokines down ~25%). On cortisol and sleep: DeLauer argues the morning cortisol peak (CAR — cortisol awakening response) shapes your ability to sleep 16 hours later, and that modern indoor living blunts the morning spike while artificial light keeps the evening trough too high — a flattened curve in both directions. Practical fixes he covers with citations: cold water on forehead/eye sockets activates the mammalian dive reflex via the trigeminal nerve → vagus nerve → suppresses HPA axis (randomized 28-person study, Scientific Reports showed lower cortisol and better HRV recovery); 5 minutes of sustained humming improved high-frequency HRV in a 36-person study (Int'l Journal of Yoga); sauna 2–3 hours before bed (BMC Medicine, ~1,700 people over 15 years) works via hypothalamic parasympathetic cooling response on exit; and room lighting below 200 lux suppressed melatonin in 99% of subjects vs. dim light — shortening the body's "internal night" by 90 minutes.

Andrew Huberman

The memory episode with UCLA's Dr. Alan Castel makes a clean, actionable point: passive re-exposure is nearly useless for memory consolidation — people who've seen the Apple logo thousands of times can't reliably recall which side the bite is on. What works is "errorful retrieval" — attempting recall before you've looked, failing, then correcting. The failure itself drives deeper encoding than successful re-exposure. This is distinct from simple repetition and maps onto the principle that retrieval practice (not review) is what consolidates memory. Castel also flags metacognition as the crux: most people systematically overestimate how well they know something they've merely seen repeatedly — what he calls the "fluency illusion." The cortisol implications for aging: beliefs about aging itself are one of the stronger predictors of cognitive decline rate — not just physiology.

Dorian Yates (via DeLauer)

The Yates interview is worth noting separately for the mental resilience thread. His core point: the gym is a microcosm of life — every time you face a challenge and push through it, you build a reference experience that generalizes to non-gym adversity. He's explicit that this transfer is the real value of hard training, not aesthetics. On PEDs: at his Mr. Olympia peak, his total weekly dose was ~1,500mg across three to four compounds — and today's recreational lifters routinely report running more than that, which he finds both reckless and philosophically confused. His advice for non-competitors is unambiguous: the yo-yo of cycling on and off offers no lasting physical benefit and meaningful health risk, so unless you're earning from it, it's not worth it. His framing of hard work is Stoic in flavor — he grew up watching men work 10-hour shifts in coal mines; the gym is not hard work by comparison, and treating it as such is a failure of perspective.

June CPI came in below expectations, boosting Fed rate-cut expectations and pushing Bitcoin above $64K. The nuance: core inflation and oil prices remain elevated, so this isn't a clean "inflation solved" signal — more a market reading softer headline numbers as permission to price in cuts. The Bitcoin Softfork That Tried to Police "Junk Data" — And Why It's Already Failing

BIP-110 attempted to restrict non-financial data use of Bitcoin's block space (think Ordinals/inscriptions), and the backlash is revealing a genuine philosophical tension: Bitcoin's censorship resistance means anyone willing to pay fees can write to the ledger — including "junk." Trying to police content at the protocol layer requires changing that property, which many Bitcoiners see as a non-starter. Worth watching as a proxy for how Bitcoin's core social contract gets stress-tested as blockspace demand diversifies.

🧬 Health & Science

Stronger by Science: Is the Hypertrophy–Strength Correlation Stronger Than We Thought?

New analysis from Stronger by Science revisits how much muscle growth actually drives strength gains — finding that prior studies likely underestimated the contribution, particularly in novice lifters. The catch: the relationship still isn't strong enough to use hypertrophy as a reliable proxy for strength development in programming decisions. Practically, this reinforces that early-stage training benefits heavily from size adaptations, but experienced lifters need to train specificity separately from volume. Worth reading for anyone programming around a size-then-strength periodization model.

412 points. Armin Ronacher (creator of Flask) argues that the shared language of a software project — the implicit knowledge of what concepts mean, where boundaries are, which invariants matter — is rarely written down and lives in people's heads. As AI-generated code accelerates output, this unwritten institutional understanding is being outpaced, creating systems that are legible line-by-line but incoherent at the architectural level. A mental model worth having for anyone thinking about knowledge, institutions, or how expertise actually gets stored and transmitted.

HN Discussion

Stoicism Is Not Unemotional — Donald Robertson

Robertson pushes back on the popular caricature of Stoics as emotionally suppressed or cold. The Stoics distinguished between destructive passions (caused by false judgments) and "good emotions" (eupatheiai) — including joy, caution, and wishing well — which arise from correct reasoning. The goal isn't to feel nothing; it's to not be enslaved by feelings rooted in error. Practically relevant for anyone working on emotional regulation: suppression isn't the model, re-evaluation is.

Solving 20 Erdős Problems with 20 Codex Accounts Running in Parallel

Someone ran 20 parallel AI instances at unsolved combinatorics problems from Paul Erdős's open problem list and reportedly made progress on several. The significance isn't the tooling — it's the question of whether AI can now contribute at the frontier of mathematics, not just reproduce known results. Erdős problems are notoriously hard and have resisted human effort for decades; any verifiable progress would be a genuine capability signal.

HN Discussion

The Hard Problem of Consciousness Is a Category Mistake — Massimo Pigliucci

Pigliucci argues that the "hard problem of consciousness" (why there is subjective experience at all) is not an unsolvable mystery but a category error — similar to how "vital force" was once posited as a special substance explaining life, before biology dissolved the question by explaining mechanism. The claim: consciousness will be dissolved, not solved, as we understand neural mechanisms better. This is a direct challenge to Chalmers-style dualism and worth engaging if you think about philosophy of mind or what makes a good explanation.
