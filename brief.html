🎥 From People I Follow

Worth your time:

GTO Wizard (ft. Tombos21 & Joey Ingram)

GTO Wizard launched its first-ever PLO solver and ran a live walkthrough with Tombos21 and Joey Ingram. The session surfaced a counterintuitive but important calibration for hold'em players: in PLO, calling dominates three-betting across almost all positions because equities run much closer preflop — and inflating the pot out of position is actively punished. Tombos demonstrated that when facing a four-bet, solvers show pocket kings folding ~90%+ of the time because roughly 86% of the four-betting range is aces, and one pair simply doesn't win at showdown. The deeper conceptual shift: hand strength at the current moment barely matters — what matters is nut potential by the river, plus blockers that enable future bluffs on later streets. The discussion of leading frequency (PLO has a much wider leading range vs. the "check to the preflop aggressor" heuristic from NLH) is worth attention for anyone bridging theory between the two games.

Video: PLO in GTO Wizard ft. Tombos21 & Joey Ingram

Jared Alderman (ft. Brian Hastings)

Brian Hastings — six-time WSOP bracelet winner, famous for winning ~$4M off Isildur1 in a single session — joined Jared to discuss pre-flop RNG decisions and river strategy. Hastings's core position: early in the game tree (e.g., AK facing an UTG open at 90bb), he RNGs because he's genuinely uncertain which action is higher EV, but as you approach the river, one action is almost always better and RNGing becomes "lazy." The more interesting exchange was on balance vs. exploitative bluffing frequencies: Hastings argued that if a villain can't tell whether you view a spot as "worth bluffing" or not, you create the same indifference as GTO balance — you're unbalanced in different directions across spots, but unpredictably so. He also noted the practical failure mode of balance: if your king-ten mixed on a prior street at a frequency you didn't match, your actual river check-jam range is already off from solver equilibrium, so pure-jamming all of them likely over-bluffs. The Isildur1 sessions came up briefly — he credits deep preparation on 6-tabling HU and understanding Isildur's tendencies rather than anything mystical.

GTO Lab

Short but genuinely useful pre-tournament mental prep tip: before a WSOP event, do light reps in the trainer — not studying, not trying to learn anything new, just playing a few orbits. The explicit claim is that the calm, low-pressure repetitive state it induces becomes a reference state you can return to when anxiety spikes mid-tournament. There's also the mechanical benefit of warming up math and risk-premium estimation subconsciously before the first hand is dealt.

Are We Undermeasuring Inflation for Lower Earners?

New research documents a systematic bias: when upstream input costs rise, varieties within a product category tend to increase by similar absolute dollar amounts — but the same absolute increase is a larger percentage change for cheaper products. Since lower earners buy cheaper product variants, their effective inflation rate is structurally higher than CPI captures. This has implications for real wage calculations, Fed policy calibration, and the political economy of why inflation hits differently across income groups.

The 2036 Issue: Bitcoin in a Multipolar World — Lyn Alden

Lyn Alden's long-form piece asks how Bitcoin fares as global monetary order fragments into competing blocs. Quality-Adjusted AI Production Growing at 2,000%+ Per Year

A new paper attempts to measure AI's contribution to GDP on a quality-adjusted basis and finds growth exceeding 2,000% annually in 2024–2025, driven by three compounding forces: data center capacity expansion, hardware efficiency gains, and — the largest contributor — algorithmic improvement.
