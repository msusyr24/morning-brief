Worth your time:

Ken Berry MD

Berry's 30-day carnivore primer covers the practical mechanics he tells beginners: prioritize fatty ruminant meat (beef, lamb, bison — 70/30 fat-to-protein ratio if ground) cooked in butter or bacon fat, eaten to satiety rather than tracked by macro targets. His core argument is that fat + protein are the only macros that meaningfully activate satiety hormones, so hunger self-regulates — he claims even people coming from a standard American diet who "eat till full" consistently see weight loss in the first month. On electrolytes, he's explicit: dropping carbs lowers insulin, which causes the kidneys to excrete sodium and water, so leg cramps and muscle twitches in the first weeks are almost always under-salting, not a protocol failure. He recommends salting to taste as a "sophisticated biochemical instrument" and pushes meal consolidation to 1–2 meals/day (no carnivore snacking) as the structural key that makes the protocol sustainable.

🧬 Health & Science

Hold Still: What Does the Evidence Actually Say About Isometric Training for Strength and Hypertrophy?

Stronger by Science's deep-dive challenges the conventional assumption that isometrics are inferior to dynamic training for both strength and muscle size. The article reviews the actual literature and argues current evidence doesn't support that hierarchy — isometrics produce meaningful strength gains (particularly at the trained joint angle) and may contribute to hypertrophy more than commonly assumed. Worth reading if you're programming around injury or looking for targeted supplemental work, since the practical implication is that isometrics are an underutilized tool rather than a fallback.

There Is No Safe Gamble with High LDL Cholesterol

Attia directly engages the "lean mass hyper-responder" (LMHR) phenomenon — the pattern where some carnivore/low-carb dieters see dramatically elevated LDL alongside lean body composition and good metabolic markers — and argues the lipid energy model used to explain this away does not justify ignoring elevated LDL-C. His core claim: the causal data linking LDL to cardiovascular risk is robust enough that favorable metabolic context doesn't neutralize it, and treating LMHR as a "safe" phenotype is an unjustified gamble. This is a direct rebuttal to a common position in carnivore/keto circles and is directly relevant given Ken Berry's content above.

Talkie: A 13B Language Model Trained Exclusively on Pre-1931 Text

From Alec Radford (GPT, GPT-2, Whisper) and collaborators: a 13B model trained on 260B tokens of historical English text predating 1931, producing a model that writes, reasons, and responds in the idiom of early 20th-century English. The interesting capability question isn't nostalgia — it's what a model with a hard knowledge cutoff at a specific historical moment reveals about how training data shapes model "worldview," and whether historical corpora can surface genuinely different reasoning patterns.
