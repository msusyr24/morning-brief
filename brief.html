Contrast Misreaction

We judge things relative to recent comparisons, not absolute values. A $2,000 suit feels cheap after you've committed to a $30,000 car. Real estate agents show you a worse house first. Munger: 'If you're selling something, show the dud first.' If you're buying, beware of contrast-engineered comparisons.

🧬 Health & Science

Hold Still: What Does the Evidence Actually Say About Isometric Training for Strength and Hypertrophy?

Stronger by Science reviews the actual evidence on isometric training and concludes the conventional wisdom — that isometrics are clearly inferior to dynamic training for muscle growth and strength — is not well-supported by the data. The piece explores the mechanisms (time under tension, motor unit recruitment, joint-angle specificity) and argues isometrics are more effective than their reputation, particularly for strength in specific joint positions and potentially as a complement to dynamic work. Worth reading if you're thinking about programming variety or have injury-related range-of-motion constraints.

Colorectal Cancer Screening: Early Screening, Colonoscopy, and Personalized Strategy

Attia's framing is worth noting: colorectal cancer is one of the rare cancers you can actually intercept before it exists, not just detect early — making it categorically different from most oncology. The episode covers when to start screening (his view: earlier than current guidelines for many people), colonoscopy as both diagnostic and preventive (polyp removal), and how to tailor a strategy based on personal risk factors. If you haven't thought about your own screening timeline, this is the episode that makes the case for doing so now.

Walking Slower? Your Ears, Not Your Knees, Might Be the Problem

A study using iPhone sensor data found that hearing loss correlates with reduced walking speed — an effect independent of age and physical health markers. The proposed mechanism is cognitive load: when the auditory system is degraded, the brain diverts processing resources to compensate, leaving less capacity for gait coordination. Practical implication: hearing health is a physical performance and longevity variable, not just a communication one.

Task Paralysis and AI

This piece explores the phenomenon where AI availability paradoxically makes it harder to start or complete tasks — not because the tools don't work, but because the optionality and delegation they create introduces a new form of decision friction and ownership ambiguity. Relevant to anyone who's noticed that having Claude available doesn't always mean getting things done faster. 225 points on HN suggests it resonated.

Physicalism and Consciousness — Parts I & II

Massimo Pigliucci (Stoic philosopher and biologist) works through what physicalism actually claims about the mind, why the evidence supports it over dualist alternatives, and how to respond to the most serious objections — including the hard problem of consciousness. This is the kind of careful, non-dogmatic philosophy worth engaging with if you think about what a good life means or how Stoic physics connects to modern science. Will AI Kill the Research Paper?

Tyler Cowen's thought experiment: imagine a macro paper with a button that says "update this with the latest data." Suddenly you have multiple versions of the same paper, no canonical one, and authorship becomes ambiguous. The broader question is whether the research paper as a unit of knowledge — fixed, authored, peer-reviewed — survives AI's ability to continuously reprocess and re-analyze. Short but genuinely interesting as a mental model for how AI changes epistemics, not just productivity.

Marginal Revolution

Bear in Mind the Social Consequences of Your Actions — Commentary on Epictetus #36

Donald Robertson's ongoing commentary on the Enchiridion covers Epictetus's argument that every action has a social dimension — we are not atomized agents but nodes in a web of relationships and obligations, and Stoic ethics requires accounting for that. This is the part of Stoicism that gets underemphasized relative to the "control what you can control" framing; Robertson does good work making it concrete.
