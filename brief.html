🎥 From People I Follow

Worth your time:

Huberman Lab

Dr. Paul Eastwick (UC Davis psychologist) presents data that directly challenges the evolutionary "mate market" model most people operate with. Key finding: the traits that make someone universally desirable on dating apps — the things everyone agrees on — predict almost nothing about who ends up in a lasting relationship. What actually matters is idiosyncratic attraction: your personal perception of a partner diverges significantly from the consensus the more time you spend with them. The person everyone rates a 6 might be a 10 to you specifically, and that divergence is the actual engine of successful pairing. His data also punctures the assumption that financial status matters more to women than men — it's roughly equal — and that men exclusively prefer younger partners — women do too, given the choice. Practical implication: time spent together in real contexts (not apps) is the mechanism that generates unique compatibility signals that no profile can surface.

🧬 Health & Science

Is the Correlation Between Hypertrophy and Strength Gains Stronger Than We Realized?

Stronger by Science digs into whether muscle growth actually drives strength gains more than previously thought — and the answer is nuanced. Prior studies underestimated the hypertrophy-strength correlation in newer lifters, likely because they used imprecise muscle size measurements. When better measurement methods are applied, the relationship is stronger — but still far from deterministic, meaning program design, neural adaptations, and skill remain major independent variables. Practically: hypertrophy training is more justified as a strength strategy for beginners than the old literature suggested, but you can't just "get bigger and get stronger" at advanced levels.

Stronger by Science

The Worthlessness of Vitamin D Is Mildly Exaggerated

A careful look at why recent large RCTs (VITAL, D-HEALTH, etc.) found little benefit for Vitamin D supplementation — and why the conclusion "Vitamin D does nothing" may be overcorrected. The core argument: most trial participants weren't actually deficient at baseline, so you'd expect minimal effect. Observational data still show strong associations with deficiency-related outcomes; the trials just didn't test the right population. Worth reading if you've seen the "Vitamin D is useless" takes and wondered whether to stop supplementing in winter.

GLP-1 Drugs and Marriage (Marginal Revolution)

A new working paper using the Understanding America Study finds that single women who start GLP-1 medications for weight loss see their marriage/cohabitation rates rise by 29 percentage points compared to matched women who want to start but haven't. The effect size is large enough to suggest GLP-1s are altering downstream social and economic outcomes, not just metabolic ones — a real data point for anyone thinking about the broader societal implications of widespread GLP-1 adoption.

💡 Worth Reading

Reason Without Virtue Is Just Calculation — Massimo Pigliucci

Pigliucci argues that the Enlightenment project of separating rational inquiry from ethics was always a conceptual error — pure reason is instrumentally neutral and can justify atrocities as readily as it can justify kindness. The Stoic correction: virtue isn't an add-on to reason, it's constitutive of what good reasoning actually is. Short and worth sitting with, especially as a counterweight to the "think more clearly = act better" assumption common in the mental models world.

This Darkness Is Not That Darkness — Donald Robertson

Robertson writes about a forgotten CBT predecessor — likely rational emotive therapy or similar — that helped a WWII veteran stop conflating present-day fear responses with past trauma. The Stoic-adjacent insight: much of emotional suffering comes from misidentifying the source of a reaction, treating an old wound as a current threat. Brief but practically useful framing for anyone thinking about emotional regulation and pattern recognition under pressure.

Should People Avoid Whole-Body Screening Info? — Astral Codex Ten

Scott Alexander examines the genuine tension in whole-body MRI/CT screening: the tests find real things (cancers, aneurysms) but also generate enormous rates of incidental findings that trigger anxiety, follow-up procedures, and sometimes harm without improving outcomes. The policy-level question — should this information be available to individuals who want it? — is a real one with non-obvious answers touching on autonomy, medical epistemology, and the limits of "more data = better."
