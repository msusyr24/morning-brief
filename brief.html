Is There a Recent Growth in Negative-Sum Assets?

Tyler Cowen's Free Press column argues that an increasing share of investment activity is flowing into assets that are negative-sum — meaning aggregate wealth is destroyed rather than created — using a sneaker brand rebranding as "an AI company" as the jumping-off point. As an economist who prefers positive-sum bets (blue-chip stocks, productive capital), he's flagging a potential structural shift in how speculative capital is being allocated. Worth reading if you think about the distinction between productive assets and narratively-inflated ones.

Marginal Revolution

Long-Term Money

Morgan Housel opens with Adam Smith's observation about child mortality in 18th-century Scotland to frame an essay on how radically compressed our sense of "long-term" is versus historical reality. The core argument: durable wealth-building requires a time horizon that feels almost absurd by modern standards, and most people can't hold that frame emotionally even when they intellectually accept it.

🧬 Health & Science

Disappointing Results from the First Rapamycin-Plus-Exercise Trial

The first RCT specifically examining rapamycin combined with exercise showed underwhelming results — rapamycin did not meaningfully enhance the exercise-induced adaptations researchers hoped for. Attia explains why this single negative trial doesn't change his overall view of rapamycin as a potential geroprotector: the trial had design limitations, and the mechanism of action (mTORC1 inhibition, senolytic effects) remains compelling independent of this one data point. This is a good example of updating on evidence without overreacting to a single study — the prior on rapamycin's longevity potential was built on multiple lines of evidence, not just exercise synergy.

Peter Attia MD

Hold Still: What Does the Evidence Actually Say About Isometric Training?

A thorough Stronger by Science deep-dive challenging the conventional wisdom that isometric training is inferior to dynamic resistance training for strength and hypertrophy. The evidence review finds that isometrics can produce meaningful strength and size gains — the inferiority claim is poorly supported by the literature — and explores the specific conditions (joint angles, contraction intensity, duration) under which isometrics are most effective. Useful if you're programming around injury, limited equipment, or looking for ways to target specific positions in a lift.

Stronger by Science

Neanderthals Ran 'Fat Factories' 125,000 Years Ago

Archaeological evidence from 125,000 years ago shows Neanderthals systematically processed large animals specifically to extract and render fat — not just for eating meat — suggesting a far more sophisticated understanding of energy-dense food storage and preparation than previously assumed. This has indirect relevance to the carnivore/ancestral diet debate: our hominid ancestors were deliberately optimizing for fat intake, not treating it as a byproduct of protein consumption.

💡 Worth Reading

The Brand Age

Paul Graham's latest essay argues we're entering a period where brand — reputation, identity, perceived trustworthiness — is becoming the primary competitive moat, displacing product differentiation in an era where AI can commoditize execution. The implications run wide: for individuals, companies, and how we should think about what's actually defensible long-term. Worth sitting with if you think about positioning, trust, or what makes something genuinely hard to replicate.

Paul Graham

Socrates's Pupils — Part I: How the Same Teacher Inspired Hedonists, Ascetics, and Rationalists

Massimo Pigliucci examines how Socrates's students diverged so dramatically — Aristippus founded hedonism, Antisthenes founded Cynicism (asceticism), and Plato founded the rationalist tradition — despite sharing the same teacher. The essay is really about what it means to absorb a philosophical influence: students don't download a system, they extract what resonates with an existing temperament. Part II follows "the paths of pleasure and logic."

Figs in Winter (Pigliucci)

When Reading About Stoicism Isn't Enough

Donald Robertson makes the argument that philosophical reading without deliberate practice — ideally with coaching or structured accountability — rarely produces the behavioral change Stoicism promises. He draws on the ancient distinction between learning *about* a philosophy versus actually training the mind through exercises like journaling, premeditation of adversity, and the view from above. Short but pointed: the gap between intellectual understanding and lived philosophy is the whole game.

Donald Robertson

What Deontological Bars?

Scott Alexander's piece examines the practical coherence (or incoherence) of hard deontological constraints — the idea that some actions are simply off the table regardless of consequences. Worth reading as a rigorous look at where rule-based ethics holds up under pressure and where it quietly collapses into consequentialism anyway. Relevant to anyone thinking seriously about decision-making frameworks or ethics beyond the "rules vs. outcomes" intro-level debate.

Astral Codex Ten

Have Online Worlds Become the Last Free Places for Children?

A MarginalRevolution piece pushing back against the dominant "smartphones are ruining kids" narrative, arguing that as physical public space for children has been steadily eliminated (no more roaming neighborhoods, schools with locked-down environments), digital spaces may now be the only remaining place where children exercise genuine autonomy, navigate social dynamics unsupervised, and develop real-world-adjacent judgment. The argument isn't pro-phone so much as it's a structural critique of the alternatives being offered.
