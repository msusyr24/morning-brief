GTO Wizard

Tom Boss and Joey Ingram walk through the core math unifying pot odds, MDF, and alpha — arguing these aren't separate concepts but two sides of the same coin. The key insight demonstrated via GTO Wizard node-locking: bluffing frequency on the river must match the pot odds you're laying, and the relationship is extremely sensitive — shifting just 3-4 combos between bluffs and value makes all bluff-catchers swing from pure fold to pure call. They show that at equilibrium, bluffs are essentially break-even (EV ≈ 0), and that blocker effects matter primarily because of the scarcity of bluff candidates in a given range, not intrinsic blocking power. Practical upshot: on the river, the solver isn't "choosing" to balance — it's forced to by the math, and any deviation by your opponent creates a clear exploitative response.

🧬 Health & Science

Is the Correlation Between Hypertrophy and Strength Gains Stronger Than We Realized?

Stronger By Science reviews new evidence suggesting hypertrophy contributes more to strength gains in novice lifters than prior studies indicated — previous research likely underestimated the relationship by studying trained populations where neural adaptations dominate. However, the authors are explicit that the relationship is still far from 1:1, meaning training for maximum hypertrophy and training for maximum strength remain distinct goals with meaningfully different programming implications. Key practical caveat: the "more muscle = more strength" connection is strongest early in training and weakens as lifters advance.

Hold Still: What Does the Evidence Actually Say About Isometric Training for Strength and Hypertrophy?

Stronger By Science challenges the conventional assumption that isometric training is inferior to dynamic training for strength and hypertrophy — the current evidence does not actually support that claim. The piece reviews mechanisms and practical applications, suggesting isometrics may be underutilized, particularly for joint-angle-specific strength and in contexts where full range of motion is not possible or advisable. Worth reading for anyone programming around joint issues or looking for complementary modalities.

Peter Attia + Tom Dayspring: Brain Cholesterol Is a Closed System

The key clinical takeaway from their brain lipidology deep dive: the brain is behind the blood-brain barrier and synthesizes its own cholesterol entirely independently of plasma LDL. The brain contains ~20-25g of total body cholesterol vs. the liver's 3-5g, making it by far the most cholesterol-rich organ. The implication directly relevant to longevity medicine: aggressively lowering plasma LDL with statins or PCSK9 inhibitors does NOT deprive the brain of cholesterol — a widespread and incorrect fear. APOE4 risk for Alzheimer's operates through a separate intracranial mechanism, not through plasma cholesterol levels.

CRISPR Tech Selectively Shreds Cancer Cells, Including "Undruggable" Cancers

Researchers have developed a CRISPR-based technique that can selectively destroy cancer cells — including cancers previously considered "undruggable" because they lack targetable protein mutations. Rather than editing genes, the approach uses CRISPR as a precision cutting tool to shred specific RNA sequences present in cancer cells but not healthy ones. If this mechanism generalizes across cancer types, it could represent a fundamentally new therapeutic modality beyond both traditional chemotherapy and existing gene therapy approaches.

Patience on the Out-Breath — Donald Robertson

Robertson's piece reframes anxiety management through Stoic-adjacent practice: rather than trying to eliminate or suppress anxiety, the technique is to relax INTO it on the exhale — accepting the physiological sensation without fighting it. The distinction matters because resistance to anxiety often amplifies it, while acceptance followed by deliberate slow exhalation activates the parasympathetic nervous system and actually reduces the signal. Practical, grounded in both Stoic philosophy and modern physiological understanding of breath regulation.
