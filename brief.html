Incentive-Caused Bias

Munger on FedEx: the company couldn't get packages sorted on time until they stopped paying workers by the hour and started paying by the shift. Workers had been incentivized to drag out the job. Once the incentive flipped, the bottleneck vanished overnight. Whenever a system produces strange behavior, ask: what is it paying for?

Paul Saladino MD

Saladino's protein bar breakdown is worth watching for the specific ingredients he flags: EPG (esterified propoxylated glycerol) is a fat-derived calorie-free additive that causes GI distress at typical serving sizes and impairs absorption of fat-soluble vitamins A and K — but doesn't appear on calorie labels because it's not absorbed. He also walks through how soy lecithin is extracted using petroleum-derived hexane (a neurotoxin), how "natural flavors" can include beetle-derived carmine or beaver anal gland secretion, and how artificial flavors can be synthesized from petroleum byproducts while remaining FDA-approved under GRAS standards. The GLP-1 short raises a more genuinely interesting scientific point: GLP-1 agonists suppress dopaminergic reward circuitry (VTA, nucleus accumbens) — the same circuits involved in addiction, but also in pair bonding and desire — and with 60+ million people now on these drugs, the long-term effects on relationships and motivation remain unstudied. 🧬 Health & Science

Hold Still: What Does the Evidence Actually Say About Isometric Training?

Stronger by Science digs into whether the long-held assumption — that isometrics are inferior to dynamic training for strength and hypertrophy — actually holds up. The piece argues current evidence does not support that claim, and examines what isometrics can and can't do, how they compare to dynamic ROM work, and where they might have a legitimate place in programming. Worth reading if you program for hypertrophy and have dismissed isometrics as outdated.

Stronger by Science

Advanced Language Processing Found in the Anesthetized Human Hippocampus

Baylor researchers found that the human hippocampus continues sophisticated language processing even under general anesthesia — suggesting unconscious neural plasticity is more active than previously understood. This has implications for both our model of consciousness and for how we think about memory consolidation and learning during sleep or sedation.

💡 Worth Reading

AI Slop Is Killing Online Communities

A data engineer documents how AI-generated content is degrading the signal-to-noise ratio in technical forums, Slack communities, and mailing lists — not through obvious spam, but through confident-sounding low-quality answers that waste the time of both askers and the humans who have to correct them. The practical concern isn't that AI answers are wrong, it's that they're wrong in ways that are hard to quickly identify, and they crowd out the friction that used to filter for genuine engagement.

HN Discussion

The Brand Age

Paul Graham argues we're entering an era where brand — meaning trust and reputation — becomes the primary differentiator as AI commoditizes execution. The implication: in a world where anyone can build anything quickly, the question of "who made this and do I trust them" becomes more, not less, important. Short, but the thesis is worth sitting with if you think about how AI affects competitive moats.

Paul Graham

Long-Term Money

Morgan Housel uses the historical example of child mortality in 18th-century Scotland (where a mother commonly buried most of her children) to frame how truly long time horizons change what decisions make sense. The essay is about how psychological adaptation to short-term loss is the actual prerequisite for compounding — in money, relationships, and anything else. Characteristically short and worth the five minutes.

Collaborative Fund

Bear in Mind the Social Consequences of Your Actions (Epictetus #36)

Donald Robertson's ongoing commentary on Epictetus's Handbook reaches the chapter on thinking through how your actions affect others before you act — not from guilt, but from the Stoic view that we are social animals and our role in the community is part of what virtue demands. Robertson connects the ancient text to practical decision-making in a way that's useful rather than academic.

Donald Robertson / Substack

Physicalism and Consciousness (Parts I & II)

Massimo Pigliucci (Stoic philosopher and scientist) makes the case for physicalism — the view that consciousness is fully explicable in physical terms — and in Part II takes on the strongest objections from smart skeptics. Relevant if you're interested in questions about the mind, free will, and what a naturalistic ethics looks like. Clear, rigorous, and not dismissive of the hard problem.

AlphaEvolve: Gemini-Powered Agent That Discovers Novel Algorithms

DeepMind's AlphaEvolve uses Gemini to autonomously write, test, and evolve code — and has produced genuinely novel algorithms across mathematics and computer science, including improvements to matrix multiplication that beat human-discovered methods. This is a meaningful step beyond code completion: the system is doing open-ended discovery, not just executing specified tasks.
