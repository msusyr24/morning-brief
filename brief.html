🎥 From People I Follow

Worth your time:

Nick Norwitz

A new Nature Communications paper engineered an enzyme called CMLase — built via directed evolution (the Nobel Prize-winning technique of forcing rapid protein mutation toward a target function) — that erases a specific aging marker called CML. CML is the result of sugars bonding to structural proteins in the extracellular matrix over time, a process previously considered irreversible. In lab tests, the enzyme removed over 70% of CML from old human arteries and worked in skin and eye tissue too. Norwitz is clear this isn't "we reversed aging," but frames it as a genuine platform advance: a proof-of-concept that aging-associated molecular damage once thought permanent can be enzymatically cleared — with obvious implications for what targets come next.

🧬 Health & Science

Is the Correlation Between Hypertrophy and Strength Gains Stronger Than We Realized?

Stronger by Science digs into new data suggesting muscle growth contributes more to strength gains in novice lifters than prior research indicated — prior studies likely underestimated the relationship due to methodological issues (short study durations, insensitive hypertrophy measures). The key caveat: the relationship still isn't particularly strong even when properly measured, meaning neural adaptations, technique, and specific strength skill remain major independent drivers. Practical implication: for beginners, chasing hypertrophy and strength simultaneously is more synergistic than previously modeled, but experienced lifters still can't rely on size gains alone to drive strength.

Does Heat Training Build a Better Engine?

Peter Attia's team examines emerging evidence on deliberate heat exposure during or after exercise as a training stimulus — the proposed mechanism being plasma volume expansion, improved cardiac output, and enhanced heat shock protein response. The framing is "cautious optimism": real physiological effects exist, but the magnitude of benefit relative to simply doing more aerobic work is still uncertain. Relevant for anyone already training seriously who's looking at sauna or hot-environment sessions as a potential adjunct.

💡 Worth Reading

Stoicism Is Not Unemotional

Donald Robertson pushes back on the popular misconception that Stoicism demands emotional suppression or coldness — the "man made of oak or stone" caricature. The Stoics distinguished between destructive passions (driven by false judgments) and healthy emotions (eupatheiai) like joy, caution, and wishing well — the latter being entirely compatible with Stoic practice. This is a useful corrective for anyone who's encountered Stoicism through pop-culture distillations that flatten it into "just don't feel things."

Donald Robertson / Substack

New In-Depth Interview: Stoicism and the Art of Living

Robertson sat for a wide-ranging conversation covering Stoicism as a practical system, its relationship to modern cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT draws heavily on Stoic ideas), emotional resilience, and what it actually means to pursue wisdom in daily life. Robertson is one of the more rigorous modern interpreters — less aphorism-heavy than most — so this is worth the time if you want the full framework rather than quotes.

Donald Robertson / Substack

Five Allegedly Bad "Isms" — Parts I & II (Pigliucci)

Massimo Pigliucci (philosopher, Stoic practitioner, scientist) examines accusations leveled at him: positivism, scientism, and related charges. Part I sets up the question of when philosophical "-isms" become genuine intellectual vices vs. strawman labels. Part II gets into what he actually believes about the relationship between science and philosophy. Pigliucci is sharp on the boundary questions — where science ends and philosophy must begin — and this two-parter is a good workout for thinking clearly about epistemology without the culture-war framing those debates usually attract.

Figs in Winter / Substack

Book Review: Great and Desperate Cures

Finalist in Scott Alexander's annual book review contest — the book covers the history of lobotomy and the broader story of how a radical, destructive psychiatric intervention spread through medicine before being stopped. ACX book review finalists tend to be long, serious, and genuinely informative; this one is relevant to thinking about epistemic failure in institutions, how consensus forms around bad ideas, and what "evidence-based" actually required. Worth reading slowly.
