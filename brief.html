Dr. Sten Ekberg

Solid mechanistic walkthrough of post-50 visceral fat accumulation. In men, declining testosterone (1%/year after 30) reduces muscle mass and allows more visceral fat, which then upregulates aromatase — converting remaining testosterone to estrogen, worsening the ratio further and reducing the cortisol buffer. In women, menopause causes rapid estrogen loss, shifting fat deposition from gynoid (hips/thighs — benign) to android (belly — inflammatory). Both sexes converge on the same visceral fat → insulin resistance → inflammatory cytokine loop. His five practical interventions: cut refined carbs/sugar (lowers insulin), eat 2-3 meals in a shorter window (no snacking), resistance training for glucose uptake without insulin, zone-2 aerobic work (not "huff and puff" cardio, which raises cortisol), and crucially — the real issue post-50 is that you've lost tolerance for the unnatural lifestyle you got away with younger.

Thomas DeLauer

Heavy week of content with several substantive mechanisms. On resistant starch: the key variable is amylose content (linear, tightly packed) vs. amylopectin (branched, easily digested) — long-grain/basmati rice and regular potatoes are higher in amylose and thus more resistant; cooking and then cooling (retrograding) increases resistant starch further, changing crystalline structure so enzymes can't break it down as efficiently. The colon ferments resistant starch into butyrate, which is the primary fuel for colonocytes and has anti-inflammatory effects downstream. 15-30g/day improved insulin sensitivity in a Journal of Nutrition study in 4 weeks. On FGF-21: this "cellular stress messenger" drives fat burning during fasting/exercise but chronic activation leads to FGF-21 resistance — the metabolic equivalent of the firefighters getting exhausted. Implication: short, high-intensity workouts (15-20 min) with proper recovery outperform long chronic cardio for metabolically unhealthy people. On cortisol activation vs. production: the enzyme 11β-HSD converts inactive cortisone to active cortisol inside fat and liver tissue, independent of blood cortisol levels — explaining why some people have normal cortisol labs but still accumulate visceral fat. Dark chocolate (25g/day, 4 weeks) reduced cortisol-to-cortisone ratio in one study by inhibiting this enzyme via epicatechins; optimal timing is early-to-mid afternoon. On eggs and Alzheimer's: a 7-year study of 1,024 people found just 1 egg/week associated with 47% lower Alzheimer's risk; autopsy analysis showed 49% lower amyloid/tau pathology odds. Choline (precursor to acetylcholine, the memory neurotransmitter) explained 39% of the effect; lutein and zeaxanthin provided antioxidant protection to microglia; and egg consumers also showed improved insulin sensitivity, directly relevant to the "Type 3 diabetes" model of Alzheimer's.

🧬 Health & Science

Resistant Starch & Insulin Sensitivity (Thomas DeLauer)

Detailed mechanism worth internalizing: amylose content (not just "fiber") is the molecular variable that determines whether a starch reaches your colon intact. High-amylose foods (basmati, long-grain rice, regular potatoes) are more resistant than low-amylose ones (jasmine/sushi rice). Cooking and then refrigerating overnight retrograde the starch into a new crystalline structure that resists digestion — same food, meaningfully different metabolic profile. The downstream benefit is butyrate production in the colon, which feeds colonocytes and reduces inflammatory signaling that leaks systemically. The practical error: eating only one type of resistant starch overfeeds specific bacterial populations (RS2 promotes Bacteroidetes but suppresses Bifidobacteria/Lactobacilli) while missing distal colon fermentation needed for colorectal protection — variety across RS1 (legumes), RS2 (green banana, raw potato), and RS3 (cooked/cooled) matters.

FGF-21 Resistance: Why Exercise Backfires When You're Metabolically Unhealthy

FGF-21 is the cellular stress messenger that responds to exercise and fasting — but chronic overstimulation (going from zero to 60 in workouts while already stressed) leads to FGF-21 resistance, where the stress signals amplify but the adaptive response fails. The 2018 Nature study found this mechanism explains why metabolically unhealthy people feel worse the more they exercise, not better. The practical implication: 15-20 minute high-intensity sessions with full recovery outperform 60-minute chronic sessions for people in metabolic dysfunction. The same "pulse not pressure" logic applies to fasting — chronic caloric restriction raises the cortisol activation enzyme (11β-HSD) in fat tissue, causing visceral fat to accumulate independently of caloric intake. The mantra: treat your life like interval training.

Choline, Eggs, and Alzheimer's Risk (Thomas DeLauer)

The Rush Memory and Aging Project followed 1,024 people for 7 years and found even 1 egg/week associated with 47% lower Alzheimer's risk. The autopsy sub-study (578 people) found 49% lower odds of amyloid/tau pathology — not just symptoms, but the actual structural disease. Choline explained 39% of the effect (it's the direct precursor to acetylcholine, the neurotransmitter that degenerates in Alzheimer's, and most adults get less than half the recommended amount). The metabolic angle is also real: egg eaters showed 4.4% reduction in fasting glucose and improved HOMA-IR vs. controls over 12 weeks, directly relevant to the "Type 3 diabetes" model of Alzheimer's. Synergistic pairing: eggs + fatty fish (DHA enhances choline utilization in neuronal membranes) + prebiotic fiber (gut-brain axis, butyrate → brain anti-inflammatory signaling).

Post-50 Visceral Fat: The Hormonal Mechanism (Sten Ekberg)

Men: testosterone decline → less competition with cortisol → cortisol drives visceral fat → visceral fat upregulates aromatase → more testosterone converts to estrogen → ratio worsens further. Women: menopause → rapid estrogen loss → fat redirects from hips/thighs (subcutaneous, benign) to belly (visceral, inflammatory). Both converge on the same loop: visceral fat signals inflammatory cytokines → liver stays insulin resistant → stores more fat. Important nuance: cardio at 140-170 bpm raises cortisol and preferentially deposits fat viscerally — it's the wrong tool. Resistance training works because contracting muscles absorb glucose without insulin, building the glycogen storage capacity that reduces the need to convert carbs to fat. The underlying principle: aging lowers your tolerance for an unnatural lifestyle, not your capacity to be healthy.
