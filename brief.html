GTO Wizard

Tom Boss walks Joey Ingram through a unified framework for river bet sizing, pot odds, and MDF — arguing these aren't separate concepts but two sides of the same coin. The core insight: when you bet 150% pot on the river, you're laying your opponent 37% pot odds, which means you must be bluffing exactly 37% of the time for their bluff-catchers to be indifferent. The nodelock demonstrations are the real value — shifting just 4 combos from bluffs to value causes every bluff-catcher to fold; adding a handful more bluffs makes everything call. The takeaway: river lines are extremely sensitive to small deviations in bluff frequency, and the GTO solution exists precisely because it makes your opponent indifferent and earns you zero EV on your bluffs while maximally extracting from value. Also covered: why the BB should donk-lead on 5-4-3 boards (nut advantage + prevent cutoff from checking back), and how blocker effects at showdown drive the specific selection of bluff hands (hands that unblock villain's folds).

GTO Lab

Kayhan Mokri makes a sharp point about range balance when checking down to the river OOP: if you never include delayed bluffs in your check-check-bet line, your river betting range becomes purely value-heavy, which eliminates fold equity entirely — IP can just never fold. The solution is to include hands with zero showdown value (e.g., 9-2 offsuit on a river blank) as check-check-river bluffs, because they have blocker value and unblock the hands villain will fold (king-high, queen-high type holdings).

Ken Berry MD

The Isabella Cooper interview is legitimately interesting for the bone health angle. The core claim: hyperinsulinemia — not low bone mineral density — is the primary driver of fragility fractures in most people, and the DEXA scan misses this because high insulin actually drives *higher* bone mineral density that is paradoxically brittle (lacks ductility and torsion strength). The mechanism: osteocytes (42 billion of them, half-life ~25 years) require fatty acid delivery via LDL-docked lipoprotein receptors — receptors that are damaged by both high glucose and high insulin. High-fiber diets and seed oils lower LDL, further starving osteocytes of their preferred fuel. Osteocalcin, the 10th most abundant protein in the body, is the key bone peptide here — it should be low in a healthy metabolic state and rises paradoxically with worsening insulin resistance before eventually crashing. The practical implication: fasting insulin under 6-10 µIU/mL and adequate K2 and D are more important for bone strength than a good DEXA score. Berry also recants his prior caution on LDL, now stating high LDL in the context of normal metabolic markers (A1C, fasting insulin, triglycerides, HDL, hsCRP) is a good sign, not a concern.

Nick Norwitz

A well-structured explainer on heart rate variability (HRV) as a marker of vagal (parasympathetic) tone and "neurocardiac agility." Key data: in an 808-patient post-MI study (AJC, 1987), low HRV predicted 5.3× higher mortality over the following years; in a 2,501-person healthy cohort, each 1 SD drop in HRV was associated with 47% higher risk of a first cardiac event. Practical improvement levers: zone 2 cardio is the most reliable baseline booster; HIIT is a double-edged sword — use HRV itself to gate recovery before next hard session (rough ratio: 70% zone 2, 30% HIIT). Omega-3s (EPA+DHA at 3,000–4,000 mg/day, equivalent to ~3 cans of sardines) improve HRV via membrane fluidity, anti-inflammation, and vagal tone through brainstem accumulation — he favors lyso-phosphatidylcholine DHA for brain delivery. Caffeine acutely suppresses HRV but up to 4 cups/day is neutral-to-beneficial; don't measure HRV post-caffeine.

🧬 Health & Science

Bone Strength, Hyperinsulinemia, and Osteocalcin — Dr. Isabella Cooper via Ken Berry MD

The central and counterintuitive claim: hyperinsulinemia causes higher bone mineral density that is paradoxically fragile — brittle like chalk rather than ductile. The mechanism is damage to lipoprotein-related receptor 1 (LRP1) on osteocytes via AGEs from chronic glucose/insulin exposure, which prevents fatty acid delivery to the 42 billion osteocytes that constitute the living matrix of bone. Osteocytes burn fatty acids (not ketones) as their primary fuel; without LDL-delivered triglycerides and fat-soluble vitamins D and K2, they cannot produce structural collagen or osteocalcin properly. The clinical implication: fasting insulin is the key bone health marker, not DEXA — and the lab reference range of "0–24.9 µIU/mL" is functionally useless (0 = type 1 diabetes). Optimal fasting insulin is under 6; under 10 is acceptable. High-fiber diets and seed oils worsen bone health by lowering LDL, starving osteocytes of their delivery vehicle. This is a mechanistically coherent argument that directly contradicts conventional dietary guidance for bone health.

HRV: Landmark Data Points — Nick Norwitz

Two studies worth bookmarking: (1) 808 post-MI patients followed with Holter monitors — low HRV group had 5.3× higher all-cause mortality over subsequent years (American Journal of Cardiology, 1987). (2) 2,501 healthy individuals followed 3.5 years — each 1 SD drop in HRV associated with 47% increased risk of a first cardiac event, independent of standard risk factors. Measurement protocol: measure immediately upon waking, before caffeine or movement, same device daily — consistency matters more than which protocol you use. Zone 2 cardio is the most reliable HRV improver; omega-3s (3,000–4,000 mg EPA+DHA/day) have RCT support via membrane fluidity, inflammation reduction, and brainstem vagal tone effects.
