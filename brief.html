🎥 From People I Follow

Worth your time:

Anthony Chaffee MD

Chaffee's main episode features 18-year-old Danish handball player Jacob Lohman, who reports near-zero DOMS after games, recovery from a chronic knee inflammation (pes anserinus syndrome), and the ability to maintain and build muscle training only once a week in-season — all attributed to ~4 months on a lion diet (beef, butter, eggs). The more striking claim in the short: Chaffee argues that "normal age-related brain atrophy" is not actually normal — citing a 2008 Oxford study showing B12 deficiency within the normal reference range correlates with 0.5–1% annual brain shrinkage, and a study of 99 chimpanzees whose brains showed zero age-related atrophy on MRI. His thesis is that standard lab reference ranges are arbitrary and vary between labs, meaning a "normal" result may still reflect subclinical deficiency that's quietly degrading brain tissue over decades. The episode also revisits the pre-steroid bodybuilding era — Serge Nubret eating 6 lbs of horse meat daily in the 3 months before competition — as historical precedent for high-performance carnivore eating.

🧬 Health & Science

Reducing Cardiovascular Risk: A Playbook for Lipid-Lowering Pharmacotherapy

Attia lays out a risk-stratified, six-step framework for choosing and combining lipid-lowering drugs — not a generic "take a statin" recommendation but a structured escalation protocol based on individual risk tier, LDL-C and Lp(a) targets, and drug tolerability. Practically useful if you're managing your own lipid panel seriously or working with a physician who isn't being aggressive enough.

Peter Attia MD

Hold Still: What Does the Evidence Actually Say About Isometric Training for Strength and Hypertrophy?

Stronger by Science's deep dive challenges the conventional wisdom that isometric training is clearly inferior to dynamic full-ROM training for strength and muscle growth. The piece reviews the actual evidence base and argues current data doesn't support that claim — relevant if you're programming around injury, or looking for underutilized tools in a hypertrophy block.

Stronger by Science

Behavioral Timescale Synaptic Plasticity Rewires the Brain After a Single Experience

Quanta covers a newly characterized form of synaptic plasticity (BTSP) that can durably rewire neural circuits after just one exposure — operating on a timescale of seconds to minutes rather than the repeated-trial timescale of classic Hebbian learning. The implication is that the brain has a faster, more powerful learning mechanism than previously understood, which has downstream relevance for memory, skill acquisition, and potentially why certain single high-intensity experiences (trauma or peak performance moments) leave such strong traces.

When Reading About Stoicism Isn't Enough

Donald Robertson — probably the most rigorous modern interpreter of Stoic practice — argues that philosophical reading without deliberate application is insufficient, and makes the case for active practice and coaching as the missing layer. This is the Stoic version of the gap between knowing and doing, which Marcus Aurelius himself returned to obsessively in the Meditations.

Donald Robertson / Substack

Socrates's Pupils — Part I

Pigliucci traces how the same teacher — Socrates — inspired radically divergent philosophical schools: the hedonists (Aristippus/Cyrenaics), the ascetics (Antisthenes/Cynics), and the rationalists who became the Stoics. The piece is a useful reminder that philosophical lineage is messy and that what we call "Stoicism" was one interpretation of Socratic practice, not the obvious conclusion.

Figs in Winter (Massimo Pigliucci)

Long-Term Money

Morgan Housel opens with Adam Smith's observation that Scottish highland mothers in the 18th century commonly bore twenty children and kept fewer than two alive — using it as a frame for how radically our relationship to time, planning, and wealth has changed when survival itself is no longer the primary constraint. Housel at his best uses historical contrast to destabilize assumptions about what "long-term thinking" even means.
