Mental Model · Economics/Productivity

Pareto Principle (80/20)

Roughly 80% of effects come from 20% of causes, across almost every domain. 80% of sales from 20% of customers, 80% of crime from 20% of criminals, 80% of your happiness from 20% of your activities. The skill: identifying the 20% before wasting effort on the 80%.

Peter Attia (with Layne Norton)

Attia raises the industrial processing objection to seed oils — not just linoleic acid purity, but the hexane extraction, repeated heating, and refinement. Norton responds methodically: hexane is used because it's nonpolar and has a low boiling point (~69°C), allowing rapid evaporation; residual hexane in finished oils is typically 0.05–0.5 parts per million, often below detection thresholds. The toxicity route for hexane is inhalation, not ingestion, and the rodent LD50 data, converted to human equivalent, would require consuming ~11,340 kg of oil to see mild side effects. Norton's position: hexane doesn't bioaccumulate, the body clears it, and the amounts ingested are negligible. The more substantive framing comes at the end: linoleic acid was under 3% of food availability ~100 years ago; today it's closer to 10% — the processing concern may be secondary to the sheer quantity shift.

🧬 Health & Science

Seed Oils: The Processing vs. Molecule Debate (Peter Attia / Layne Norton)

The most substantive health conversation this cycle. The key exchange: even granting that pure linoleic acid may be benign, does industrial extraction (hexane solvent, repeated heating) create a meaningfully different product? Norton's answer is essentially "no" on hexane — residuals are sub-ppm, not bioaccumulative, and toxicologically negligible at any realistic consumption level. But both implicitly agree the more important variable may be epidemiological: linoleic acid went from ~3% to ~10% of the food supply over the last century, and no controlled trial has matched that real-world exposure trajectory. The honest takeaway is that the processing concern is probably overstated, but the quantity-of-consumption shift is underexplored.

ACV, AMPK, and Fat Adaptation (DeLauer)

The cited mechanism: acetic acid → AMPK phosphorylation (40% increase in one in-vitro rat hepatocyte study within 1 hour) → PPARα nuclear activation → upregulation of CPT1/CPT2 (mitochondrial fat transport) and CD36 (lipid transporter). The "fat adaptation" claim is that PPARα needs sustained activation to remodel mitochondrial machinery, and regular ACV use could maintain that signal during low-carb/fasting protocols. Caveat: the 40% figure is in vitro. No human RCT cited for the fat-adaptation endpoint specifically.

Fatty Liver Root Cause: De Novo Lipogenesis, Not Dietary Fat (Ekberg)

Ekberg's central argument — well-supported by the mechanistic literature — is that hepatic fat accumulation is primarily driven by fructose and excess glucose triggering de novo lipogenesis via insulin, not by dietary fat intake (which doesn't raise insulin or contribute to DNL). Fructose is uniquely problematic because only the liver can process it. The implication: standard advice to "cut saturated fat" for fatty liver is directionally wrong, while the actual intervention is reducing refined carbohydrates and improving insulin sensitivity. His assignment of 70% of therapeutic leverage to insulin resistance reversal is a useful framing heuristic.
