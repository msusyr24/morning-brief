Hedgehog vs. Fox

Stronger by Science

A deep dive into isometric training — the kind where you hold a position without moving through a range of motion. The article argues that isometrics are broadly underrated: the common assumption that they're inferior to dynamic training for strength and hypertrophy isn't well-supported by current evidence. Isometrics can drive meaningful gains, particularly for strength at specific joint angles, and may have underappreciated roles in programming for injury resilience and variety. Worth reading if you're thinking about training efficiency or novel stimuli.

🧬 Health & Science

Disappointing Results from the First Rapamycin + Exercise Trial

The first trial testing rapamycin combined with exercise produced negative results — but Attia's takeaway is that this doesn't diminish rapamycin's promise as a geroprotector. His argument is that a single negative trial in this domain is weak evidence against the mechanistic case, particularly given trial design limitations inherent in early longevity research. Worth reading for anyone tracking the rapamycin-for-longevity thesis seriously.

Peter Attia

💡 Worth Reading

What Makes Art Great?

Tyler Cowen flags a new essay by Nabeel Qureshi arguing that great art distinguishes itself through "multiple overlapping layers of echoes" — a structural property harder to see in verbal artifacts than musical or visual ones, but present in the work that lasts. This is the kind of aesthetic theory question that doesn't get asked seriously enough; Qureshi's framing suggests greatness is less about surface quality and more about resonant internal architecture.

Marginal Revolution / Essay by Nabeel Qureshi

Long-Term Money — Morgan Housel

Housel opens with Adam Smith's observation that Scottish highland mothers commonly bore twenty children without two surviving — using it as a lens on how radically short our time horizons are relative to human history, and what that means for how we think about money, risk, and patience. A characteristic Housel move: historical context as a corrective to present-bias. Short but worth sitting with.

Socrates's Pupils, Parts I & II — Massimo Pigliucci

A two-part series examining how the same teacher — Socrates — gave rise to wildly divergent philosophical schools: the hedonists (Aristippus and the Cyrenaics), the ascetics (Antisthenes and the Cynics), and the rationalists (proto-Platonists). Pigliucci uses this to interrogate what we actually take from teachers vs. what we project, and which path leads to a well-lived life. Good companion reading to anyone working through Stoic foundations.

Bad Connection: Global Telecom Exploitation by Covert Surveillance Actors

Citizen Lab documents systematic exploitation of the global telecom signaling infrastructure (SS7/Diameter protocols) by covert surveillance actors to track individuals across borders. This isn't a theoretical vulnerability — it's active, widespread, and affects anyone carrying a mobile phone internationally. The piece has real implications for understanding how easy mass-scale location surveillance is in practice, regardless of device-level security.

🤖 AI Capability Watch

OpenAI's o1 Correctly Diagnosed 67% of ER Patients vs. 50–55% by Triage Doctors

A Harvard trial tested o1 on emergency room triage diagnosis against human triage doctors — o1 hit 67% accuracy versus 50–55% for physicians. This is a capability benchmark with direct real-world stakes: ER triage is a high-volume, time-pressured setting where a 12–17 point accuracy gap translates to meaningful patient outcomes. The result adds to a growing body of evidence that frontier models are crossing clinical utility thresholds in specific diagnostic tasks.
