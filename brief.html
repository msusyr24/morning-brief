🎥 From People I Follow

Worth your time:

Ben Felix

Felix makes a careful, evidence-based case for when owning a home beats renting — and it's more conditional than most people think. His core finding from modeling 12 Canadian cities (2005–2025): disciplined renters who invested the difference came out 14% wealthier on average. But three variables flip the math toward owning: (1) Time horizon — plan to stay 10+ years, because an owned home functions as a hedge against rising rents in your specific neighborhood, and that hedge only compounds with time; (2) Tax situation — high-income earners who've maxed their TFSA/RRSP face a lower opportunity cost on home equity, since their taxable investment returns are eroded by taxes while home appreciation is tax-free in Canada; (3) Asset allocation — conservative investors (e.g., 60/40) have such a low after-tax expected return (as little as 1.4% above real estate appreciation at top Ontario rates) that renting and investing almost never catches up. He also cites research showing that for households with existing investment portfolios, a home can improve risk-adjusted total portfolio returns as a diversifying asset — but only if you already have meaningful non-housing wealth.

🧬 Health & Science

The Evolving Landscape of Alzheimer's Detection and Treatment

Peter Attia's conversation with neurologist Gayatri Devi covers how early diagnosis of Alzheimer's is now meaningfully actionable — not just a way to brace for decline. The core claim: new disease-modifying therapies (likely referring to anti-amyloid agents like lecanemab/donanemab) combined with multimodal interventions (sleep, exercise, metabolic health, cognitive engagement) can actually alter cognitive trajectory if caught early enough. The shift in framing is important — Devi argues early detection is no longer a "why know if you can't do anything" question; it's now a "catch it early enough to intervene" imperative.

Peter Attia MD

Does Losing Sleep Mean Losing Lifespan? What a New Meta-Analysis Can and Can't Tell Us

Attia examines a new meta-analysis on sleep duration and mortality, and characteristically pushes back on the causal inference — observational data on sleep and lifespan can't cleanly distinguish "short sleep causes death" from "sick people sleep less." His practical bottom line: the uncertainty in the data doesn't change the recommendation, because the mechanistic case for adequate sleep (metabolic health, cognitive consolidation, cardiovascular function) remains strong regardless of what the meta-analysis shows.

Is Hypertrophy More Tightly Linked to Strength Gains Than We Thought?

Stronger by Science digs into a new analysis suggesting that muscle growth explains more variance in strength gains for novice lifters than prior research indicated — but the relationship is still far from deterministic. The key nuance: studies that lump experienced and inexperienced lifters together underestimate the hypertrophy-strength link in beginners, who have less room to gain neurologically and thus rely more on added muscle mass. For intermediate and advanced lifters, neural and skill factors still dominate the strength adaptation picture.

Beyond the Inner Citadel — Massimo Pigliucci on the Limits of Stoic Self-Help

Pigliucci (a Stoic philosopher, not a pop interpreter) uses Ludwig Edelstein's scholarship to examine whether the Stoic retreat into the "inner citadel" — controlling only your own judgments and responses — is philosophically sufficient or whether it quietly abandons the social and political dimensions of Stoic ethics. This is a meaningful internal critique worth sitting with: the inner citadel framing is what most modern Stoicism sells, and Pigliucci is asking whether that's a truncation of what the Stoics actually believed about how to live among others.

Figs in Winter (Pigliucci)

Stoicism Is Not Unemotional

Donald Robertson (author of How to Think Like a Roman Emperor) pushes back on the popular misconception that Stoics were emotionless or suppressed feeling — the Stoics distinguished between passions (irrational, destructive reactions) and good emotions (eupatheiai: joy, caution, wishing well) which are the natural result of living in accordance with reason. Marcus Aurelius himself shows grief, tenderness, and frustration throughout the Meditations. This is a short but clarifying read for anyone who has internalized "Stoic = stone-faced."

Donald Robertson

Tyler Cowen's Talk at DeepMind: On Meaning in an Age of AI

Cowen's framing is counterintuitive and worth engaging: he argues the coming challenge won't be finding meaning in a world where AI has devalued human labor, but managing the excess of meaning — lives so stimulating and full of possibility that a kind of cognitive and emotional exhaustion sets in. This inverts the usual "AI takes meaning away" narrative and poses a genuinely different question about what human flourishing looks like when scarcity of interesting problems is no longer the constraint.

Marginal Revolution

The Hard Problem of Consciousness Is a Category Mistake — Pigliucci

Pigliucci argues that David Chalmers' "hard problem" — why there is subjective experience at all — commits the same error as 19th-century vitalism (the belief that life required a special non-physical "vital force"). Just as vitalism dissolved once we understood biochemistry, Pigliucci argues the hard problem will dissolve as neuroscience matures, not because consciousness is explained away but because the question itself is malformed. This is a substantive philosophy-of-mind position, not pop philosophy — worth a careful read if you find the consciousness debate interesting.
