GTO Wizard

A strong case against the "statistician trap" in PLO study: the mistake isn't studying too little, it's memorizing frequencies instead of learning patterns. The video introduces the SIP method — Simplify, Isolate (find the 20% of spots driving 80% of EV), and find Patterns. Concretely: PLO opening ranges across positions share a common skeleton, so you anchor on the cutoff range and add/subtract at the margins rather than learning each position from scratch. On sizings, the entire flop strategy collapses to two rules: locked boards (paired, monotone, straight-heavy) get small bets (1/3 or 1/4 pot); regular boards default to half pot. For hand reading post-flop, the key skill is finding the threshold group — the exact hand strength where strategy shifts from pure fold to pure continue — and ignoring everything else.

Thomas DeLauer

Sodium bicarbonate (baking soda) has an ISSN-reviewed evidence base for high-intensity performance: 0.2–0.5g/kg bodyweight taken 60–180 minutes pre-training raises extracellular bicarbonate, creating a steeper gradient for hydrogen ions to exit muscle cells, buffering the burn in efforts lasting 30 seconds to ~12 minutes. Beta-alanine covers the intracellular side of the same system by loading muscle carnosine (requires 4–6g/day for 4+ weeks; tingling is the main side effect). The key finding: a British Journal of Sports Medicine meta-analysis of 40 studies (1,400+ subjects) found beta-alanine alone had an effect size of 0.18 — barely meaningful — but the combination with sodium bicarb jumped to 0.43, the single largest performance effect in the entire analysis. Applied to a 1500m race, a 2.85% improvement is roughly 6 seconds — podium-shifting at elite level.

🧬 Health & Science

Sodium Bicarbonate + Beta-Alanine: Dual-Buffer Performance Stack

Covered under GTO Wizard above but worth extracting here as a standalone finding. The Thomas DeLauer video summarizes a well-designed meta-analysis (British Journal of Sports Medicine, 40 studies, 1,400+ subjects): beta-alanine alone hits an effect size of 0.18 on exercise performance; combining it with sodium bicarbonate pushes that to 0.43 — more than double, and the largest single effect size in the entire analysis. The mechanism is clean: carnosine (loaded by beta-alanine) buffers hydrogen ions inside the muscle cell; bicarbonate buffers them extracellularly once exported. They hit different compartments and don't compete for absorption. Practical protocol: 0.3g/kg sodium bicarb 60–120 min pre-training; 4–6g beta-alanine daily for 4+ weeks (split doses to reduce tingling).

Thomas DeLauer

Antioxidant Supplements: The Evidence Against (Ekberg summary)

The ORAC measurement framework — the entire scientific basis for marketing antioxidant supplements — was quietly abandoned by the FDA in 2012 after failing to show any meaningful relationship to health outcomes. The clinical damage: a 230,000-person meta-analysis of 68 RCTs found that beta-carotene, vitamin A, and synthetic vitamin E supplements all increased mortality. Two specific trials were halted early after seeing a 17–28% increase in cancer in the treatment arm. The biological reason: free radicals are intracellular signals that activate NRF2, which in turn produces the body's own enzymatic antioxidants (catalase, SOD, glutathione) — each capable of neutralizing millions of free radicals per minute vs. one-for-one for supplements. Flooding the system with exogenous antioxidants suppresses that signaling cascade. Fruits and vegetables benefit health primarily through microbiome-feeding fiber and polyphenols, not antioxidant content.

Dr. Sten Ekberg

Fasting + Chemotherapy: Differential Killing Mechanism

Peter Attia's short clip lays out a clean mechanism: during caloric restriction, healthy cells downregulate growth and shift to maintenance/repair, making them more resistant to chemotherapy's indiscriminate cell damage. Cancer cells cannot do this — mutations in tumor suppressor genes force them to keep growing, making them disproportionately vulnerable to the same treatment. Clinical trials confirm that water-only fasting for 24+ hours before chemo reduces side effects sufficiently to allow higher or more complete dosing — which is itself a major outcome driver since many patients can't complete full chemotherapy regimens due to toxicity.
