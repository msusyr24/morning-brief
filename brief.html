🎥 From People I Follow

Worth your time:

Thomas DeLauer

DeLauer walks through his PSMF (Protein Sparing Modified Fast) protocol — 2 consecutive days per week at ~800–1,000 calories, almost entirely lean protein (chicken breast, egg whites, white fish, fat-free Greek yogurt + whey), near-zero carbs and fat. The mechanism: liver glycogen depletes, insulin drops to baseline, glucagon rises, and the body shifts into deep fat oxidation. He cites a meta-analysis of 24 RCTs in Obesity ranking alternate-day fasting above standard caloric restriction, and a Cleveland Clinic retrospective of 1,400+ patients showing 55% hit 5%+ weight loss in a year vs. 20% with other diets. The muscle-preservation key, backed by an AJCN study, is high protein even on restriction days — the study group eating 2.4g/kg protein actually gained lean mass while in a 40% deficit, versus 1.2g/kg who lost it. The five "normal" days should stay at ~1g/lb protein with clean whole-food carbs; the day after PSMF he deliberately reintroduces carbs (sweet potatoes, berries, Manuka honey) to restore serotonin and sleep quality. Hard training stays on non-PSMF days; PSMF days get only low-intensity maintenance lifts.

🧬 Health & Science

Is the Correlation Between Hypertrophy and Strength Gains Stronger Than We Realized?

Stronger by Science examines new analysis suggesting that prior studies underestimated how much muscle growth contributes to strength gains in novice lifters, largely because those studies used cross-sectional designs and short timeframes that obscured the relationship. The corrected picture: hypertrophy explains more of strength development early in training than the classic "neural adaptation dominates" story implied — but the correlation is still far from deterministic, meaning you can't reliably predict strength from size or vice versa. Practical implication: for newer lifters especially, programming that drives real hypertrophy is also building the structural foundation for strength, not just aesthetics.

Stronger by Science

Building Strength and Muscle Mass for Longevity (AMA #71 rebroadcast)

Attia rebroadcasts his core thesis: skeletal muscle mass and strength are among the strongest predictors of all-cause mortality, and the window to build it is finite — "the more you move, the more you're alive." Key practical emphasis: training for longevity isn't just cardio; progressive resistance training to build and preserve lean mass is the single highest-leverage intervention for people in midlife and beyond.

Peter Attia MD

Metformin and Cancer: Active Surveillance Needed

Attia covers new data showing metformin failed in prostate cancer trials and several others, despite earlier observational enthusiasm. His point isn't to dismiss the drug but to argue for precision thinking: metformin may help in some cancer contexts and hurt in others, and blanket prescriptions for "cancer prevention" aren't warranted by the current evidence. A useful corrective to the trend of repurposing metformin as a longevity/anti-cancer panacea.

💡 Worth Reading

Marcus Aurelius Was Not an Emotionless Stoic

Donald Robertson pushes back on the pop-culture reading of Stoicism as emotional suppression, drawing on his biography of Aurelius to show the Meditations are full of grief, longing, self-doubt, and frustration — the work of a man actively struggling, not a sage who had transcended feeling. The Stoic goal was never to eliminate emotion but to transform destructive passions into reasoned responses; Aurelius is valuable precisely because he's transparently imperfect. Worth sitting with if you've ever found the "just don't feel bad" version of Stoicism hollow.

Donald Robertson / Substack

Beyond the Inner Citadel — Massimo Pigliucci on the limits of Stoic self-help

Pigliucci examines Ludwig Edelstein's critique that the Stoic "inner citadel" framing — the idea that you can always retreat into your own reason as a refuge — is philosophically incomplete and can shade into passivity or social disengagement. The essay asks whether Stoicism as typically practiced is actually a mature philosophy of life or a sophisticated coping mechanism, and where the line between the two lies. A useful challenge for anyone who's found Stoicism genuinely useful but suspects it has edges.

Figs in Winter / Substack

Fable turned a reMarkable tablet into Tom Riddle's diary

A developer used Claude Fable (Anthropic's frontier model) to make a reMarkable e-ink tablet respond in real time as if it were a sentient, manipulative diary — you write to it, it writes back in its own handwriting, in character.
