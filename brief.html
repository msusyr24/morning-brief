🧬 Health & Science

Prostate Cancer Screening: Why Current PSA Guidelines Are Failing Men

Attia's core argument: standard PSA guidelines (often recommending men start screening at 50, or skip it altogether) are leaving cancers undetected until they're no longer curable. The key claim is that timing of detection is arguably the determining factor in life vs. death — early-stage prostate cancer is highly treatable; late-stage is not. He advocates for PSA testing starting younger (40s), combined with PSA velocity tracking over time and newer tools like MRI-guided biopsy and liquid biomarkers (e.g., 4Kscore, PHI) to reduce unnecessary biopsies from false positives. The piece also pushes back on the USPSTF's 2012 recommendation against routine PSA screening, which Attia argues caused a measurable spike in late-stage diagnoses in subsequent years.

Related companion piece · Peter Attia

Air Is Full of DNA

Environmental DNA (eDNA) — shed from skin, breath, and waste — is now detectable in ambient air samples with enough fidelity to identify species, and in some cases individuals. Researchers are finding this opens up ecological monitoring (census wildlife without trapping) but also raises serious privacy flags: human eDNA is present and potentially identifiable from public spaces. A genuinely novel finding with implications that run from conservation biology to surveillance law.
