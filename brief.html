🎥 From People I Follow

Worth your time:

Lex Fridman

A deep conversation with the lead developers of FFmpeg and VLC — two pieces of open source software that quietly underpin essentially all video on the internet (YouTube, Netflix, Chrome, Discord, and billions of other devices). The most striking takeaway is the scale of what volunteers built: FFmpeg contains 100,000+ lines of hand-written assembly because, at the codec level, every CPU cycle matters across 3 billion decoding devices. The compression ratios involved are staggering — modern video codecs achieve 100–1000x compression by exploiting human perceptual limits (we're less sensitive to color than brightness, so chroma resolution is halved almost invisibly). JB's story of tracking down 350+ contributors to relicense VLC's core from GPL to LGPL — including visiting a factory worker whose deceased son had written a few lines of code — is genuinely moving. The core insight worth sitting with: five to fifteen people maintain software that civilization runs on, and they do it because they care about craft, not money or fame.

🧬 Health & Science

Hold Still: What Does the Evidence Actually Say About Isometric Training for Strength and Hypertrophy?

Stronger by Science reviews the actual evidence on isometric (static contraction) training — the long-held assumption being that it's clearly inferior to dynamic full-range training for both strength and hypertrophy. The piece challenges that assumption, arguing current data doesn't cleanly support the inferiority claim, particularly when isometrics are performed at longer muscle lengths (where mechanical tension is high). For practical programming: this isn't a case for replacing dynamic training, but isometrics at stretched positions may deserve a real role in accessory work, especially for tendons and hard-to-load ranges. Worth reading in full if you're building hypertrophy programs.

💡 Worth Reading

Long-Term Money

Morgan Housel's latest uses the jarring historical fact that Scottish highland mothers routinely bore twenty children and watched most die — to reframe what "long-term thinking" actually requires. The core argument: our intuitions about time, patience, and expected outcomes were calibrated in a world of radical uncertainty and short lifespans, and truly long-term financial (and life) thinking requires overriding deeply wired short-term instincts. Housel at his best is when the historical framing makes the abstract visceral.

When Reading about Stoicism isn't Enough

Donald Robertson argues that reading Stoic texts — however deeply — doesn't automatically translate into Stoic practice, and that some form of active coaching or practice structure is necessary to actually change behavior under pressure. This maps onto a real tension: the gap between intellectual understanding of Stoicism (knowing the dichotomy of control) and being able to apply it when emotionally activated. Short piece but the thesis is practically important for anyone who reads a lot of philosophy without doing much with it.
