🧬 Health & Science

Hold Still: What Does the Evidence Actually Say About Isometric Training?

Stronger by Science does a deep evidence review on isometric (static contraction) training for strength and hypertrophy — the kind of training long assumed to be inferior to dynamic, full range-of-motion work. The piece argues the current evidence does NOT support that assumption: isometrics can produce comparable strength and size gains under the right conditions, particularly when matched for effort and volume. Worth reading if you program resistance training and have reflexively dismissed isometrics — the practical implications for injury rehab, tendon health, and supplemental work are real.

Stronger by Science

Colorectal Cancer Screening: When to Start and How to Build a Personal Strategy

Peter Attia's episode on colorectal cancer makes a point worth internalizing: this is one of the few cancers where intervention can prevent the cancer from ever existing (not just catch it early), because colonoscopy removes precancerous polyps before they progress. The discussion covers when to start screening (earlier than the official 45-year guideline if you have family history or symptoms), how to think about colonoscopy vs. alternatives like Cologuard, and how to build a personalized cadence. The actionable threshold: if you have a first-degree relative with colorectal cancer, you should be screened at 40 or 10 years before their diagnosis age — whichever comes first.

💡 Worth Reading

The Brand Age — Paul Graham

Graham argues we've entered an era where brand — trust, reputation, associations — has become the primary competitive moat, displacing earlier advantages like distribution or manufacturing scale. Given how much of his thesis intersects with AI (when outputs become commoditized, brand of the producer matters more), this is worth sitting with. Short and characteristically punchy.

Paul Graham

Bear in Mind the Social Consequences of Your Actions — Epictetus #36

Donald Robertson's commentary on Handbook chapter 36 unpacks Epictetus's point that we are social creatures first, and that our actions reverberate beyond ourselves in ways we must account for — not as a constraint on freedom but as part of what it means to act rationally. This is the Stoic counterweight to purely individualist thinking about virtue: you can't fully evaluate an action without modeling its effect on the people around you. A useful corrective when decision-making under pressure tempts you toward tunnel vision.

Donald Robertson / Modern Stoicism

Eugenics: Bad Science, Bad Ethics, or Both? — Massimo Pigliucci

Pigliucci's piece is less about eugenics per se and more about the epistemics of rejection: are we declining an idea for the right reasons? He argues that if we reject eugenics only because it was "misused," we leave the door open to its return under better conditions — the stronger case is that the science itself was flawed AND the ethics were bad, independently. A good example of first-principles reasoning applied to a culturally charged topic; the method transfers well to other ideas you hold strong intuitions about.

Early Evidence on School Smartphone Bans and Mental Health

A new study using synthetic difference-in-difference models looks at what actually happens when schools ban smartphones — and Cowen flags that the treatment effects are surprisingly weak, consistent with his general skepticism about intervention-based mental health solutions. "Early" is appropriately stressed, but if the effect is small even in controlled conditions, it's a useful prior update against the confident pro-ban narrative. Worth tracking as more data accumulates.
