GTO Wizard (Tom Boss21 & Joey Ingram)

A proper Poker Math 101 lesson covering the deep equivalence between pot odds, bluff frequency, and MDF/alpha — concepts most players treat as separate but are actually two sides of the same coin. The key insight demonstrated via GTO Wizard node-locking: on a polarized river spot, shifting bluff frequency by even a few combos (from ~37% to 35%) causes every bluff-catcher in villain's range to go from mixing to pure fold, because they all cluster near the same equity threshold. The reverse is equally sharp — over-bluffing by a small amount makes every bluff-catcher a mandatory call. The practical takeaway: river decisions are binary and extremely sensitive to small frequency deviations, which is why table-level detection is nearly impossible but exploiting known tendencies (under-bluffers vs. over-bluffers) has massive EV. The blocker discussion — why nines beat tens as a bluff-catcher because the bluffing range contains more 8x and 67x than 9x — is a clean illustration of how scarcity of opponent bluff-combos should shape your calling range.

Nick Norwitz

Norwitz self-experimented with retatrutide (GLP-3, a triple agonist: GLP-1 + GIP + glucagon receptor) at conservative doses (1–2mg/week vs. trial doses up to 12mg), logging 127 biomarkers before and after. Key findings: the glucagon mechanism hits first and hard — his resting heart rate jumped from the low 40s to 65–70 bpm, HRV dropped, and core body temperature increased 1.2°F, while he was simultaneously eating 4,000+ calories/day and still lost 4 lbs in week one. This resolves the "paradox" of people getting hungrier on reta: energy expenditure increases before appetite suppression kicks in. The muscle-sparing claims don't hold up in published data — at 8–12mg, roughly 38% of weight loss is lean mass, comparable to earlier GLP-1 drugs. His hsCRP rose from 0.3 to 1.8 mg/L, which he hypothesizes was indirect (driven by fat loss-induced inflammation) rather than the drug itself. He also doubled his dose from 1mg to 2mg and ended up sick on the bathroom floor at 3am — a visceral signal of how potent the metabolic acceleration is. Bottom line: this is a genuinely different drug, not just a stronger Ozempic, and the cardiovascular implications (elevated resting HR, lower HRV) deserve serious study.

🧬 Health & Science

Hypertrophy–Strength Correlation: Stronger Than Assumed, But Still Weak

Stronger By Science published an analysis of the relationship between hypertrophy and strength gains, finding that muscle growth probably contributes more to strength improvements in novice lifters than prior studies suggested — but the correlation is still nowhere near as strong as the raw data appears at first glance. The implication: early training gains in strength are heavily confounded by neural adaptation, and assuming hypertrophy drives strength proportionally will overestimate the contribution of muscle mass to 1RM performance, particularly in intermediate/advanced trainees. This matters for programming decisions around volume vs. intensity blocks.

Isometric Training: The Evidence Is Better Than You Think

A Stronger By Science deep-dive into isometric training challenges the common assumption that it's inferior to dynamic training for strength and hypertrophy. The current evidence doesn't support that claim — isometrics can drive meaningful muscle and strength adaptations, with angle-specificity being the main limitation for strength transfer. For lifters dealing with joint pain, tendinopathy, or looking for novel training stimuli, this is worth revisiting.

Retatrutide: Triple Mechanism, Real Metabolic Firepower, Muscle-Sparing Claims Don't Hold

See the Norwitz summary above. The key health-science takeaways: (1) glucagon receptor activation creates a genuine thermogenic effect distinct from appetite suppression — these mechanisms are temporally dissociated, which explains the "hungrier but losing weight" paradox; (2) at therapeutic doses (8–12mg), ~38% of weight loss is lean mass — the muscle-sparing narrative circulating in popular media is not supported by published data; (3) resting heart rate elevation and HRV reduction on the drug are biologically meaningful signals that need further study before drawing longevity conclusions; (4) hsCRP elevation may be indirect (from fat mobilization/loss) rather than drug-induced inflammation. The trillion-dollar question of whether to stay on these drugs long-term remains genuinely open.

Brain Cholesterol Is an Entirely Separate System From Serum Cholesterol

From the Attia/Dayspring podcast: the brain synthesizes its own cholesterol (primarily via astrocytes using ApoE as its transport protein) and does not import peripheral LDL across the blood-brain barrier in meaningful quantities. The brain holds 20–25g of cholesterol — 20x more than the liver — and guards it tightly. This has a direct clinical implication: the widespread fear that aggressive LDL lowering (via statins or PCSK9 inhibitors) will harm the brain by depriving it of cholesterol is not mechanistically supported. ApoE genotype (especially APOE4) governs how efficiently the brain recycles and transports cholesterol internally, which is why APOE4 is the largest genetic risk factor for Alzheimer's.
