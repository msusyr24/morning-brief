Mental Model · Psychology

Deprival Super-Reaction

Losing $100 feels worse than finding $100 feels good — by about 2x, per Kahneman's research. Munger calls this 'deprival super-reaction.' It explains stock-market panics, breakup grief, and why people fight harder to keep what they have than to acquire what they don't. Knowing this, you can price losses more rationally.

🧬 Health & Science

Resistant Starch: Visceral Fat Reduction in RCTs (Nick Norwitz deep dive)

The core study (Nature Metabolism, double-blind RCT, n=37): 40g/day of type 2 resistant starch (high-amylose, specifically Hi-Maize 260) for 8 weeks reduced body weight, fat mass, and visceral fat confirmed by MRI — calorie intake controlled and matched to placebo. One participant lost 100 cm² of visceral fat; liver fat dropped 58% in high responders. Mechanistically: resistant starch resists small-intestine digestion, reaches the colon, gets fermented by bacteria (which have far more carbohydrate-active enzymes than humans), restructuring the microbiome toward short-chain fatty acid production, altered hormonal signaling, and increased fecal fat excretion. Causality was confirmed via fecal microbiota transplants into germ-free mice — mice receiving "resistant starch" human microbiomes lost fat on identical diets. A 2025 Cell Metabolism follow-up identified the responder/non-responder split: high baseline Prevotella suppresses benefit (competes out efficient fermenters); high Bifidobacterium pseudocatenulatum predicts strong response. Practical implication: before supplementing, gut microbiome composition may determine whether you're in the 58%-liver-fat-reduction group or the 7% group.

12-HEPE: The Omega-3 Hormone Linking Sardine Consumption to Brown Fat Thermogenesis

Norwitz's sardine self-experiment (1,000 sardines/month) pushed his blood omega-3 index to 16% — triple the average human's ~5%, comparable to marine mammals. He developed measurable cold resistance, which he attributes to omega-3 conversion to 12-HEPE, a hormone that stimulates brown fat thermogenesis and muscle glucose uptake. A recent Cell paper identified the receptor for 12-HEPE as OLFR110, a smell receptor expressed not just in the nose but in liver, brown fat, and brain — repurposed by evolution for metabolic regulation. 12-HEPE is lower in people with obesity and higher in leaner individuals, though causality direction remains open. The biological principle he draws out: biological systems reuse components across contexts (feathers → insulation then flight; ear bones evolved from jaw bones), meaning every intervention has connected trade-offs — statins reduce inflammation but damage muscle, omega-3 megadosing activates thermogenesis but also activates the same olfactory receptor that makes you smell like a fish even after showering.

MOTS-c: Mitochondrial Hormone with Fat Loss and Longevity Signals

MOTS-c is a small peptide encoded in the mitochondrial genome (part of a newly discovered class of "short reading frames" beyond the known 13 mitochondrial genes). It is conserved across mammals, increases with exercise in humans, and in animal models drives fat loss, increased energy expenditure, improved endurance, and extended lifespan. It's species-conserved (mice, lions, dolphins, humans), suggesting evolutionary importance. Norwitz notes people are now self-injecting it and he plans to report on personal experimentation — currently preclinical but the mechanism is biologically plausible. Watch this space.
