Mental Model · Economics/Business

Principle-Agent Problem

When one party (agent) acts on behalf of another (principal), their incentives rarely perfectly align. Lawyers bill by the hour, not by outcome. CEOs optimize for short-term stock price, not long-term company health. The discipline: in any relationship, ask whose incentives actually match yours.

Jared Alderman

Alderman's conversation with high-stakes live player Charlie makes a compelling case that psychological edge — not GTO theory — is the most under-exploited frontier in poker today. Charlie argues that being "one level above" an opponent psychologically (understanding their internal state, not just their range) is more valuable than any solver work, and that live tells fall into two categories: the hard-to-teach intuitive "energy read" à la Phil Ivey, and a highly teachable systematic category where specific physical behaviors at specific moments in a hand meaningfully condense ranges. He believes the high-stakes community will discover these systematic tells within 5 years, and the psychological/mindfulness layer within 15 — and that most players are leaving more EV on the table from mental-game leaks than from theoretical gaps. Alderman adds that he could put 100 hours into solver work or meditate 10 minutes before every session, and the meditation would produce more EV.

🧬 Health & Science

Peter Attia — Prostate Cancer Screening

Attia's podcast argues current PSA screening guidelines are causing preventable deaths by catching cancers too late: "The timing of when you find the cancer is not a minor detail, it's arguably the determining factor in life and death." The episode covers modern tools that go beyond standard PSA thresholds — including PSA density, PSA velocity, MRI-guided biopsy, and genomic risk scores — that allow for earlier, more accurate detection while reducing overtreatment of indolent cancers. Worth listening if you or a close male in your life is approaching 40-45 and relying on standard guidelines.

Episode #388 — Peter Attia MD

Acetaminophen vs. Ibuprofen — A Real Investigation

Asterisk Magazine runs a careful look at what the evidence actually shows about when acetaminophen and ibuprofen work, when they don't, and why the standard advice ("take either, they're equivalent") is probably wrong for many use cases. The piece examines the distinct mechanisms (COX inhibition vs. unclear central mechanism for acetaminophen), the clinical trial data on pain types where each outperforms the other, and the toxicity profiles that most people misunderstand — most people fear ibuprofen's GI effects but underweight acetaminophen's narrow therapeutic window. Well-sourced and genuinely clarifying.

Smart Contact Lens Monitors and Treats Glaucoma

A microfluidic contact lens in development can continuously monitor intraocular pressure and release medication in response — addressing a core problem in glaucoma management where patients miss pressure spikes between clinic visits. The closed-loop design (sense → respond) in a wearable platform is a meaningful step toward real-time therapeutic intervention for a condition that currently relies on patient self-reported adherence and periodic snapshots.

💡 Worth Reading

Five Crucial Stoic Ideas — How Do They Stand Up to Modern Science and Philosophy?

Massimo Pigliucci (Figs in Winter) applies contemporary philosophy of mind and empirical science to stress-test five foundational Stoic commitments: the dichotomy of control, the role of reason, the cosmopolitan brotherhood of humanity, living according to nature, and the unity of virtue. This is the kind of piece that separates Stoicism-as-Instagram-quote from Stoicism-as-serious-philosophy — Pigliucci neither dismisses nor uncritically celebrates, he pressure-tests. Worth reading alongside Marcus or Epictetus rather than instead of them.

Figs in Winter (Pigliucci)

The Complex Evolution of the Stoic "System"

A companion piece tracing how Stoic doctrine shifted across its three periods — Early Stoa (Zeno, Chrysippus), Middle Stoa (Panaetius, Posidonius), and Late Stoa (Seneca, Epictetus, Marcus). The core argument is that Stoicism has always been a living, internally contested tradition, not a fixed canon — which matters if you're trying to understand why your Epictetus contradicts your Chrysippus. Useful historical grounding for serious readers.

Figs in Winter (Pigliucci)

What Marcus Aurelius Can Teach Us About Coping with Stress

Donald Robertson's conversation with Mark Forstater, author of *The Spiritual Teachings of Marcus Aurelius*, focuses on the practical psychological techniques in the *Meditations* — particularly the "view from above" exercise, negative visualization, and the distinction between what disturbs us (our judgments) versus events themselves. Robertson is one of the most rigorous Marcus interpreters working today; even if you know the basics, these conversations usually surface something actionable.

Donald Robertson / Stoicism

Greg Brockman: Inside the 72 Hours That Almost Killed OpenAI

Farnam Street's Knowledge Project interviews Greg Brockman on the Sam Altman firing and reinstatement, the internal culture and decision-making at OpenAI, and his own views on AGI timelines and what it means to build responsibly under uncertainty. Less about AI capabilities, more about decision-making under extreme pressure, organizational resilience, and what it's like to be inside a genuinely high-stakes institutional moment. Relevant to the mental models / inner game thread as much as AI.

Diverse Organic Molecules Found on Mars via SAM TMAH Experiment

The Curiosity rover's Sample Analysis at Mars (SAM) instrument used a wet chemistry technique for the first time on Mars and detected a diverse suite of organic compounds preserved in ancient sedimentary rock — including fatty acids and aromatic compounds. These are not biosignatures per se, but their diversity and preservation over billions of years substantially expands the case that Mars had a chemically complex environment, and possibly the preconditions for life.
