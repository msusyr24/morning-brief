🎥 From People I Follow

Worth your time:

Jared Alderman

Two short but dense mental game clips. The more striking one reframes poker fear: what feels like anxiety about a specific hand is often a memory — a pattern laid down in childhood when you exposed yourself and got rejection or aggression back. The nervous system doesn't distinguish between a vulnerable communication with a parent and running a bluff; it just knows "I did something exposed and got the opposite back." Alderman's practical reframe isn't "how do I escape this feeling" but "how do I learn to relate to it as what it is" — a once-wise protection that no longer fits the context. The second clip makes a simpler but complementary point: the characteristics that produce success (show up regardless of how you feel, don't fixate on output) are domain-general.

Videos: The fear isn't about the hand. It's a memory. · Corey Eyring on Succeeding Across Domains

Andrew Huberman (ft. Dr. Tommy Wood)

A genuinely information-dense episode on exercise and neuroplasticity. Key finding: a 6-month HIIT protocol (Norwegian 4x4 — 4 minutes at 85–95% max HR, 3-minute rest, repeated 4x, three times per week) produced significantly better hippocampal structural preservation and function than Zone 2, despite similar VO2 max gains — and that benefit persisted for 5 years post-intervention in older adults. Wood also draws a useful distinction between neurogenesis (mostly not happening in adults) and neuroplasticity (always happening via synapse remodeling), arguing the field confused these for decades. Practical implication: novelty and cognitively demanding new activities — language learning, new instrument, unfamiliar movement patterns — produce measurable executive function improvements in RCTs, not just observational data. Wood's framework: holding onto existing function requires continuing to use it; extending function requires novel hard inputs.

Does Heat Training Build a Better Engine?

Peter Attia's team covers the emerging data on deliberate heat exposure as a training stimulus. "Cautious optimism" is the framing — mechanistically, heat stress triggers plasma volume expansion and increases cardiac output, which overlaps with aerobic adaptation pathways. No specific protocol numbers in the excerpt, but the piece is positioned as a practical evaluation of summer heat training rather than just sauna research.

Is the Correlation Between Hypertrophy and Strength Gains Stronger Than We Realized?

Stronger by Science revisits the hypertrophy-strength relationship, finding that muscle growth likely contributes more to strength gains in novice lifters than prior studies suggested — but emphasizes the relationship is still far from deterministic (i.e., you can gain muscle without gaining much strength and vice versa). Relevant if you're programming and trying to decide how much size work carries over to performance.

💡 Worth Reading

Human Mathematicians Are Being Outcounterexampled

AI systems are now generating mathematical counterexamples — disproving conjectured theorems — faster and more prolifically than human mathematicians. The Xena project blog (which works on formal proof verification) documents this directly: the Jacobian conjecture was recently disproved by an AI-found counterexample that fits in a tweet. This isn't benchmark performance; it's AI doing original mathematical work that changes what's considered true.

Discussion · Xena Project

Who's Afraid of Chinese Models?

Ben Thompson's argument: the frontier US labs (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google) will be fine because they're in an arms race that open-weight Chinese models can't win at the top. The real problem is that there's no strong open US alternative — and the US government's instinct to restrict AI exports is backfiring by ceding the open-weight ecosystem to China. His proposed fix: allow US labs to distill their frontier models for open release, which would require acknowledging the hypocrisy of prohibiting distillation of their own models while training on unlicensed data. Tyler Cowen adds at Marginal Revolution that China's strategy is textbook "commoditize your complements" — make AI a free commodity so that the physical world (manufacturing, robotics) where China leads becomes the value layer.

Stratechery · Cowen's take

Stoicism Is Not Unemotional

Donald Robertson pushes back on the pop-Stoicism caricature of the emotionless sage. The Stoic goal isn't suppression of feeling but transformation of it — moving from reactive passions (fear, rage, grief driven by false judgments) toward eupatheiai, the rational emotions like joy, caution, and wishing well. Relevant as a corrective if you've been treating "be Stoic" as "feel nothing."

Donald Robertson / Substack

Five Allegedly Bad "Isms" – Parts I & II

Massimo Pigliucci (Stoic philosopher and scientist) defends himself against accusations of positivism, scientism, and related charges — a useful two-part piece on where the legitimate boundaries of science are and where scientism actually goes wrong. Good for sharpening your own thinking about what counts as evidence and what doesn't, particularly relevant if you navigate between empirical health claims and philosophical frameworks.
