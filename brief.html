Mental Model · Problem-solving

Five Whys

Toyota's technique: when a problem surfaces, ask 'why?' five times. Each answer gets you closer to root cause. The first answer is always a symptom, not a cause. Munger uses this in business analysis: 'Why is this company losing money?' → five layers deep, you usually find a cultural or strategic issue, not an operational one.

Bitcoin's Quantum Problem Is Really a Governance Crisis in Disguise

Guillaume Girard of UTXO Management argues that the real quantum threat to Bitcoin isn't technical imminence — a capable quantum computer may never arrive — but the governance reality that Bitcoin's protocol changes move glacially, like a state legislature. The window to prepare is now, precisely because acting later would require rushing through changes under pressure. An interesting inversion: the quantum problem reveals Bitcoin's consensus process as its true bottleneck.

🧬 Health & Science

Hold Still: What Does the Evidence Actually Say About Isometric Training for Strength and Hypertrophy?

Stronger by Science does a deep dive into isometric training — holding a muscle contraction without movement — and challenges the conventional dismissal of it as inferior to dynamic, full-range training. The evidence suggests isometrics can produce comparable strength gains, particularly at the trained joint angle, and may have underappreciated hypertrophy potential depending on contraction intensity and duration. Worth reading if you're thinking about injury-context training, rehab, or adding variation to a stalled program — the evidence is more nuanced than "just do full ROM."

Habitual Coffee Intake Shapes the Microbiome, Modifies Physiology and Cognition

A Nature study finds that regular coffee consumption produces measurable changes in gut microbiome composition, with downstream effects on physiology and cognitive function. This is mechanistic, not just correlational — coffee appears to be actively shaping the microbial environment, not merely correlating with healthier lifestyles. Adds to the growing body of evidence that coffee's effects run deeper than caffeine's direct neurological action.

💡 Worth Reading

Why I Write (1946) — George Orwell

Orwell's classic essay on the four motives behind all serious writing: sheer egoism, aesthetic enthusiasm, historical impulse, and political purpose. He's bracingly honest that vanity is always present, and that pretending otherwise is its own kind of dishonesty. If you've never read it, it's 15 minutes that will change how you think about your own reasons for communicating — in any medium.

Five Crucial Stoic Ideas — Massimo Pigliucci

Pigliucci runs five core Stoic concepts — the dichotomy of control, living according to nature, the four virtues, preferred indifferents, and cosmopolitanism — through the lens of modern philosophy and empirical science to ask which hold up and which need revision. He's honest about where Stoicism is on shaky ground (particularly the physics undergirding "living according to nature") and where it remains practically robust. Good for anyone who wants more than surface-level Stoicism quotes.
