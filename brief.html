🧬 Health & Science

Seed Oil Processing: Hexane Is Probably Not the Problem

Layne Norton (with Peter Attia) walked through the actual chemistry: hexane is used as a nonpolar solvent to extract seed oils, then steam-evaporated off at ~69°C for 60–90 minutes. Residual hexane in finished oils: 0.05–0.5 ppm, often below detection limits. Hexane toxicity is primarily an inhalation risk, not ingestion — no documented human deaths from oral exposure — and the dose required for even mild side effects via food would require consuming ~11,340 kg of oil. Norton's conclusion: hexane isn't the mechanism of harm. The more legitimate open question is the population-level shift from ~3% to ~10% of dietary energy from linoleic acid over the past century, and whether chronic exposure at scale has downstream effects independent of acute toxicity concerns.

Vitamin C, Cellular Iron Accumulation, and Biological Aging

Nick Norwitz summarized new findings (published in a top science journal) showing that aging cells accumulate excess iron, which activates a molecular switch accelerating biological aging. Vitamin C appears to bind and inhibit this switch. In monkey studies, 30 mg/kg of Vitamin C slowed biological aging markers including brain volume loss on MRI. The human equivalent dose is approximately 2.1g/day — achievable with an inexpensive supplement. Mechanism is speculative-but-plausible; the animal data is the headline.

Jason Fung's Three-Type Hunger Framework

Fung's core argument (via Thomas DeLauer's channel): obesity is a broken body-fat thermostat problem, not a willpower or calorie-arithmetic problem. Insulin and cortisol push the thermostat up; GLP-1 pushes it down (hence why semaglutide works hormonally, not mechanically). The three hunger types — homeostatic (physical, hormonal), hedonic (dopamine/pleasure, exploited by ultra-processed foods), and conditioned/social — matter clinically because ~30–40% of people with weight issues meet criteria for food addiction, which requires abstinence strategies, not moderation advice. Telling a food addict "everything in moderation" is, in Fung's framing, as counterproductive as telling an alcoholic to "just have one drink."

SPEAKE(a)R: Turn Speakers to Microphones for Fun and Profit

A 2017 security research paper (resurfacing on HN) demonstrating that headphone/speaker jacks can be repurposed as microphones via software on commodity hardware — no physical modification required — enabling covert audio surveillance even when no microphone is present or the mic is disabled. The underlying mechanism is that speaker drivers are essentially microphones run in reverse, and the signal can be captured and reconstructed. Practically relevant for anyone thinking seriously about physical security of devices in sensitive conversations.
