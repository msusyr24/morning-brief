GTO Wizard

A high-quality PLO study methodology breakdown built around what they call the "SIP method": Simplify, filter (80/20), and find Patterns. The core argument is that memorizing solver frequencies hand-by-hand is the wrong frame — instead, identify the threshold groups where strategy actually changes (e.g., "pair + open-ended always continues, pair + gutshot mixes, bare pair folds") and build heuristics from those. Specific tactical insights include: use cutoff opening range as an anchor for multiple positions; on "locked" boards (paired, monotone, straight-heavy), use small sizing (1/3–1/4); on regular boards, half-pot is the preferred default. The big preflop insight: ace-high hands dominate opening ranges because they reduce opponent 3-bet frequency AND navigate low-SPR spots well post-flop — "bad holding couples" (3x, 4x, 5x side cards) are the primary filter for folding marginal hands from early position.

🧬 Health & Science

Urolithin A: Timing Over Daily Dosing for Mitochondrial Recovery

DeLauer's self-experiment — and the underlying cell biology — makes a compelling mechanistic case for timing urolithin A to the 6–48 hour post-exercise window rather than taking it daily. The key pathway: exercise activates PINK1/Parkin proteins that tag damaged mitochondria for removal (mitophagy peak at ~6 hours post-workout). Urolithin A amplifies this exact pathway. A Cell Reports Medicine RCT showed 4 months of urolithin A produced measurable increases in phospho-Parkin in muscle biopsies, improved electron transport chain proteins, and ~12% functional strength gains. The counterintuitive insight: taking mitochondria-stimulating peptides or performance drugs on top of already-stressed mitochondria from hard/fasted training may push oxidative stress past the hormetic sweet spot into net damage — urolithin A does the opposite by clearing the damaged units.

Artificial Sweeteners and Cognitive Decline: Why the New Study Likely Reflects Reverse Causality

A high-profile 8-year, 12,000-person observational study in the Journal of Neurology found an association between artificial sweetener consumption and cognitive decline — but the high-sweetener group also had significantly higher rates of diabetes, obesity, hypertension, ultra-processed food consumption, and alcohol use, all of which independently cause cognitive decline. Multiple randomized controlled trials (including the 1-year SWEET trial) show no worsening of metabolic or cardiometabolic markers from sweeteners, and slightly better weight outcomes. The mechanistic pathways cited (gut dysbiosis, inflammation) appear in RCTs to produce no measurable downstream harm. The most likely explanation is the same as the old weight-gain confound: sick people trying to improve their health use diet products, so diet products correlate with illness.

Pterostilbene in Cranberries Reduces Fatty Liver via PPAR-Alpha Activation

A 6-month RCT (n=110) found 144mg/day of pure cranberry extract significantly reduced liver fat and improved insulin sensitivity versus placebo. The proposed mechanism is pterostilbene activating PPAR-alpha, a nuclear receptor that shifts hepatic cells toward fatty acid oxidation — meaning cells get better at burning fat rather than storing it. This is distinct from fructose-heavy sweetened cranberries (which can worsen fatty liver); the effect appears specific to the pterostilbene content of unsweetened cranberry. The visceral fat and fatty liver connection is bidirectional, making this potentially meaningful for metabolic health beyond just liver outcomes.
