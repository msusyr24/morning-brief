GTO Wizard

First-ever GTO Wizard PLO livestream with Tombos21 and Joey Ingram, walking through the new PLO solver tool. Key insight: PLO rewards calling far more than 3-betting preflop because equities run much closer and playing in position with 4 cards is extraordinarily valuable — folding on the button is essentially never correct. The 4-bet pot analysis is striking: when facing a 4-bet from the hijack, the solver shows aces make up ~86% of the range, meaning hands like KK are often folds, while low connected hands actually have better equity. Tombos's central takeaway for hold'em players crossing over: "your hand strength at this moment in time is not important — what matters is what your hand strength will be by the river," which reframes everything around nut potential, backdoor draws, and future blockers rather than current pair strength.

Jared Alderman

Interview with Brian Hastings — 6-bracelet winner who famously beat Isildur1 for $4M in a heads-up session. The strategic discussion centers on when to RNG decisions vs. commit to a pure line: Hastings argues RNG makes more sense early in the game tree where variables are unpredictable, but on the river almost always one action is correct. The deeper debate is about balance vs. deliberate imbalance as a strategy — Alderman argues that being genuinely willing to do anything (overbluff or shut down completely depending on the spot) creates the same opponent uncertainty as balance, and may play to personal strengths better than trying to achieve precise theoretical frequencies on the fly. Hastings pushes back that against top players who can identify systematically over/underbluffed spot types, balance is still the safer anchor. The personal side: Black Friday in 2011 (age 23) sent Hastings into depression and he moved back in with his parents — a candid look at the psychological fragility behind even elite poker careers.

Peter Attia

Attia and Layne Norton go deep on seed oil processing — specifically whether industrial extraction (hexane solvents, heat, refining) is the real source of harm independent of linoleic acid itself. Norton's position: hexane residuals in finished oils are well under 1 part per million, the danger from hexane is inhalation not ingestion, and the math on toxic dose is so extreme (would require consuming ~11,000 kg of oil) that it's essentially a non-issue. The more substantive concern they land on: oxidation during cooking and reheating, not extraction. The historical context is worth noting — linoleic acid went from under 3% of food availability ~100 years ago to roughly 10% today, which is the real structural shift worth examining.

Isometric Training: The Evidence Is Better Than You Think

Stronger by Science published a comprehensive review arguing the longstanding belief that isometric training is inferior to dynamic training for strength and hypertrophy is not supported by current evidence. This matters practically for programming around joint pain, injury rehab, or limited equipment — isometrics may be a legitimate primary modality, not just a rehabilitation tool. Worth reading if you've dismissed them.

Nick Norwitz: Liver-Targeted CRISPR Gene Editing Drops LDL 62% in Humans

A new clinical study used CRISPR packaged in lipid nanoparticles programmed to target only liver cells (not germline, not heritable) and achieved a 62% reduction in LDL in a single treatment. Norwitz's framing is correct: the cholesterol headline is secondary — the real breakthrough is tissue-specific gene delivery at human scale, which opens a new class of therapies for neurodegenerative disease, cancer immunotherapy, and sarcopenia. The ethical and long-term safety questions are real, but the precision here is categorically different from earlier gene editing attempts.

Scientists Just Gene-Edited Humans to Lower Cholesterol · Nick Norwitz

Seed Oil Processing: Is Hexane the Problem?

Peter Attia and Layne Norton's detailed walkthrough concludes that hexane residuals (~0.05–0.5 ppm) in finished seed oils are far below any plausible harm threshold — the danger from hexane is inhalation, not ingestion, and it doesn't bioaccumulate. The more defensible concern is oxidation during high-heat cooking and repeated reheating, not the extraction process itself. The structural epidemiological signal — linoleic acid rising from ~3% to ~10% of food availability over 100 years — remains the more interesting datum and is separate from the processing question.

Is Industrial Processing the Real Problem With Seed Oils? | Peter Attia & Layne Norton

Calcium Hydroxyapatite vs. Calcium Carbonate: A Meaningful Distinction

From Ekberg's sardine experiment: sardine bones provide calcium hydroxyapatite — structurally identical to human bone, absorbed at any pH, packaged with phosphorus, magnesium, and collagen. Most calcium supplements use calcium carbonate (essentially chalk), which requires stomach acid to absorb but simultaneously neutralizes that acid — making absorption largely theoretical. This isn't a novel finding but the mechanism is explained clearly and has real implications for supplementation choices.

I Ate Nothing But Sardines For 5 Days · Dr. Sten Ekberg

How to cope with Boethius

Massimo Pigliucci (Figs in Winter) takes on Boethius's Consolation of Philosophy — written while awaiting execution — as a meditation on how ancient wisdom traditions deal with the most extreme adversity. The core Boethian move: Fortune is by nature fickle, so the wise person never truly relied on her in the first place. Pigliucci situates this in his ongoing series bridging Stoic and broader ancient thought for modern readers.

How Anyone Can Think Like a Roman Emperor

Donald Robertson on Stoicism and what he calls "majesty as a state of mind" — the Aurelius practice of acting from your role and your values regardless of external circumstances, rather than from mood or reaction. Robertson links this to modern cognitive approaches: the Stoic emperor's internal monologue is a deliberate practice, not a personality trait. Short but worth the few minutes.

Book Review: The Dialectical Imagination

Scott Alexander reviews a history of the Frankfurt School — the group of Marxist-adjacent German intellectuals (Adorno, Horkheimer, Marcuse) who shaped much of 20th century critical theory. Worth reading as intellectual history: how a small group of thinkers in exile produced ideas that became the default framework in academic humanities decades later, and what those ideas actually were before they became tribal signals.
