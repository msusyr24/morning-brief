🎥 From People I Follow

Worth your time:

GTO Wizard

A high-stakes PLO pro breaks down what he calls the "SIP method" — the core insight being that trying to memorize solver frequencies combo-by-combo is the wrong approach entirely. Instead, study for patterns and thresholds: find the hand strength at which strategy changes (e.g., pair + open-ended always continues, pair + gutshot starts mixing, bare pair mostly folds), then ignore everything between the pure strategy boundaries. He argues 20% of solver knowledge drives most of your win rate, and that simplifying sizing to one bet (half-pot on most boards, one-third on "locked" boards like paired/monotone) costs almost no EV while dramatically improving execution. The preflop section is the most underrated: early position → "when in doubt, fold"; button → "when in doubt, open"; and the BB defending range vs. the button shares surprising overlap with the cutoff open, so you can anchor to one and adjust at the margin rather than learning every spot cold.

Videos: How Pros Actually Study PLO

Jared Alderman

A sharp one-minute clip via Uri Peleg on the gap between solver logic and real-world exploitation: a solver instantly adjusts to over-bluffing, but real opponents may never detect a pattern. The actual question to ask is "how would they even know?" — and Peleg gives a concrete example of a high-stakes player who wanted to over-fold certain spots but needed a way to do it without tipping off observant opponents. The practical takeaway: in live play against non-tracking opponents, population tendencies and concealment matter far more than mixing to exact solver frequencies.

Videos: Poker Solvers vs. Real Players: Uri Peleg on Real World Problems

Peter Attia

A deep episode on endometriosis and adenomyosis with a reproductive specialist. Key data: ~10% of women of reproductive age have endometriosis; 30–50% of infertile women have it; and if you have endometriosis, your probability of infertility is ~40%. The evolutionary framing is striking: a woman 200 years ago had roughly 100 lifetime menstrual cycles (late menarche, many pregnancies, extended breastfeeding); modern women may have 400+. This four-fold increase in retrograde menstruation is likely a primary driver of rising endometriosis prevalence — not just better diagnosis. Mechanistically, endo lesions overexpress aromatase (producing their own estrogen, so ovarian suppression alone doesn't fully control the disease) and exhibit progesterone receptor downregulation, explaining why progestin therapy has limits. Adenomyosis — endometrial tissue growing into the myometrium — affects up to 20–30% of women, and ~70% of endometriosis patients also have it; the two conditions share oncogenic somatic mutations (KRAS, PIK3CA) but are molecularly distinct diseases.

Stronger by Science (RSS)

New analysis revisiting the correlation between hypertrophy and strength gains: prior studies likely underestimated how much muscle growth drives strength increases in newer lifters, but the relationship is still far from deterministic — meaning you can gain substantial size without proportional strength gains and vice versa. The practical implication is that training for one does not automatically produce the other, and programming should explicitly target whichever adaptation you prioritize.

🧬 Health & Science

Type 2 Diabetes as "Chronic Carbohydrate Overdose Syndrome" — Ken Berry & Anthony Chaffee

Ken Berry (with his co-host) claims to have interacted with over 1,000 people who had verified T2D and now have normal A1C — framing the disease as reversible carbohydrate toxicity rather than a permanent progressive condition. This aligns with Virta Health RCT data showing ketogenic diets can normalize blood sugar in T2D patients, data that Chaffee argues receives no mainstream press because it threatens pharmaceutical and processed food industry revenue. Chaffee's broader argument — that nutritional epidemiology using food frequency questionnaires is structurally incapable of isolating dietary variables, and that "healthy user bias" (people who eat more vegetables also smoke less, exercise more, etc.) accounts for most observed vegetable-health correlations — is methodologically legitimate even if his carnivore conclusion is stronger than the evidence supports. His citation of Dr. Richard Horton (longtime Lancet editor-in-chief) saying over half of published literature in top journals is "not only false but fraudulent" is real and worth knowing: Horton did say this in 2015, in the context of a broader replication crisis critique.

Ken Berry MD · Anthony Chaffee MD YouTube channels

Pre-Fast Nutrition Protocol for Fat Loss vs. Longevity Goals — Thomas DeLauer

DeLauer's core claim: what you eat before a fast determines the metabolic trajectory of the fast, not just whether you're comfortable. Specific mechanisms cited — casein protein (slow-digesting, gel-forming) is superior to whey before an evening fast; MCT oil uniquely delays gastric emptying despite rapid liver conversion to ketones, smoothing the energy transition into a fasted state; psyllium husk improves GLP-1 stimulation and insulin sensitivity (meta-analysis in BMC Endocrine Disorders); and Mediterranean spices (oregano, thyme, rosemary) activate PPAR-alpha, a nuclear receptor that shifts cells into fat-oxidation mode with negligible calories. The most actionable: slow eating speed significantly reduces hunger one hour later (not immediately) due to improved gut distension signaling — directly relevant to fasting consistency.

Age verification is just a precursor to automated attribution of speech

The argument: age verification systems require identity authentication to access online content, and once that infrastructure exists, it becomes trivially easy to attach your real identity to every piece of content you consume or produce online. The author frames this not as a privacy concern but as a structural speech-attribution system — the kind of thing authoritarian governments build explicitly, being constructed here under the banner of child protection. A serious argument worth engaging with regardless of where you land on the underlying policy.

Pigliucci traces the evolutionary roots of tribal violence (Part I) and then scales up through sacred violence, divine kingship, and nuclear codes (Part II) — asking why cooperation breaks down at the intergroup level even as it succeeds within groups.
