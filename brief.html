Jared Alderman (feat. Brian Hastings)

A rich, substantive conversation on when to RNG vs. make a pure decision in tournament poker. Hastings argues for RNG'ing early in the game tree (e.g., AK vs. an EP open at 90bb) precisely because solver mixes are close and you lack opponent-specific reads — but becomes increasingly opposed to RNG'ing as you approach the river, where one action is almost always dominant. Jared pushes back with an interesting counter: instead of striving for balance (which is nearly impossible to achieve accurately in-game given compounding errors from prior streets), he prefers to be genuinely capable of doing anything — bluffing or shutting down — so opponents can't exploit a predictable lean. They also discuss the psychology of "known aggression" (e.g., a player who everyone knows over-bluffs still gets folds because execution remains hard), and the mechanics of a K-T river check-jam at low frequency, where being balanced requires you to have both nutted hands and blockers — not just any combo of K-T.

🧬 Health & Science

Light as a Metabolic Signal — Mitochondria and Insulin Resistance

Thomas DeLauer synthesized a meaningful body of research this week: a Journal of Clinical Medicine review confirmed that cytochrome c oxidase (Complex IV) directly absorbs red/near-infrared light, improving electron coupling and ATP output — not just circadian signaling but literal energetic machinery. A PNAS study found that one night of sleeping under room light (~100 lux) increased insulin resistance and heart rate the next morning on an identical diet, with the proposed mechanism being melatonin suppression removing its role as a mitochondrial antioxidant and ROS scavenger. A separate Nutrients analysis found morning bright light exposure in overweight subjects produced measurable fat loss and appetite reduction even before dietary changes — attributed to direct metabolic effects beyond circadian entrainment. Practical upshot: sleep dark, get real morning sunlight, and consider that chronic metabolic dysfunction may be partly a light-environment problem.

Nick Norwitz: Zero Coronary Plaque After 7 Years of LDL >500

Norwitz published a peer-reviewed case report after sustained LDL above 500 mg/dL (peak 574) and obtained an advanced 3D coronary plaque scan showing zero plaque — soft or calcified. The relevant comparison: children with familial hypercholesterolemia at similar LDL levels develop plaque within years of life and have heart attacks by age 8-10. Norwitz is a lean mass hyper-responder on a ketogenic diet; his ApoB may be elevated but his particle context (low triglycerides, high HDL, low insulin, zero inflammation markers) differs from the FH phenotype. The paper doesn't prove LDL is irrelevant, but is serious evidence that context and particle type matter more than the raw number.

Fructose → Gut Microbes → Alcohol Metabolites → Liver Damage

A Cell Metabolism study found that fructose-driven liver disease risk is partially mediated by gut microbiota: when researchers eliminated microbiomes with antibiotics, the fructose-liver disease link disappeared. Stool samples from liver disease patients showed upregulated alcohol metabolism pathways, and follow-up experiments mapped a specific pathway where gut microbes convert fructose to acetaldehyde (a toxic alcohol byproduct), driving hepatic inflammation. This doesn't mean fructose is equivalent to alcohol, but it does suggest the gut microbiome is a meaningful variable in why high-sugar diets damage the liver — and that microbiome composition could modulate individual susceptibility.

Isometric Training: Evidence Better Than Assumed

Stronger by Science published a thorough literature review concluding that isometric training's reputation as inferior for hypertrophy and strength is not well-supported. When equated for effort and volume, isometrics produce comparable results to dynamic training for many outcomes. The real limitation is joint-angle specificity — strength gains are more localized to the trained angle — but this can be managed by training at long muscle lengths (which show the most transfer). Practically relevant for rehab programming, busy travel weeks, or adding variety to a stagnant program.

Peter Attia AMA: Medications and Supplements Framework

The framing principle from this AMA is worth holding: "Poor problem definition almost guarantees some sort of false positive." The practical implication is that most people add supplements or medications without first clearly defining what problem they're trying to solve, what measurable outcome would indicate it's working, and what the counterfactual looks like. Without that rigor, you can't know if anything is actually doing anything — and you're likely accumulating interventions that feel productive but aren't falsifiable.

Scott Alexander (Astral Codex Ten) argues that the appeal of paradigm shifts — the idea that a new framework will dissolve your current problems — is almost always illusory. The insight: paradigms change the vocabulary and framing, but the underlying hard problems tend to survive. Worth sitting with if you find yourself attracted to the next "revolutionary" system.
