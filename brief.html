Nick Norwitz

Norwitz has total cholesterol over 700 and LDL near 574 — medical case-report territory — driven not by genetics but by the "lipid energy model": on a lean, insulin-sensitive, low-carb body, fat transport ramps up dramatically and cholesterol rises as a byproduct. He refused statins for 7 years on the grounds that all evidence for statin benefit comes from metabolically unhealthy patients, and statins impaired his own exercise performance in a self-run crossover trial. Now he's starting two non-statin cholesterol-lowering drugs (ezetimibe and bempedoic acid) — not for heart health but because he carries two copies of APOE4 (10-15x Alzheimer's risk), and new data suggest these molecules have neuroprotective effects independent of cholesterol: ezetimibe appears to reduce amyloid buildup and improve autophagy in the brain (one dataset shows 8x lower Alzheimer's risk in users), while bempedoic acid's prodrug form was just shown in a May 2026 Cell Metabolism paper to activate PPARα and shift amyloid precursor processing toward a neuroprotective pathway. He also covers tesamorelin, a growth-hormone-releasing hormone analog proven in multiple RCTs (including NEJM) to reduce visceral fat by ~15% vs. an increase in the placebo group over 6 months — originally developed for HIV patients accumulating visceral fat from medications.

Renaissance Periodization (Dr. Mike)

Dr. Mike makes a strong programming case against ever de-emphasizing arms: bigger arms are never the wrong answer, and if they're overpowering your shoulders, the fix is to grow your shoulders — not shrink your arms. For shoulder width specifically, he argues the visual "pop" comes almost entirely from side delts, which respond to high volume (5-7 sets per session) at 2-4 sessions/week of lateral raises, upright rows, and face pulls — not shoulder pressing, which is primarily a front-delt and tricep builder. On rows vs. pull-downs for back: rows don't bring the lat anywhere near its stretched position, so they're primarily a thickness exercise (erectors, rhomboids, mid/lower traps), while lengthened-position exercises like pull-downs, pullovers, and straight-arm work drive disproportionate lat hypertrophy per current evidence. He also addresses genetic variation directly: some people are 3-4 standard deviations out on muscle-building potential and will have 20" arms drug-free — assuming enhanced is a failure to appreciate how wide the natural distribution actually is.

Thomas DeLauer

Three metabolic tools with actual mechanism data: (1) Dried ginger powder (1-3g/day) outperforms fresh for blood sugar — heat converts gingerols to shogaols, which are stronger TRPV1 activators and better alpha-glucosidase/alpha-amylase inhibitors; a meta-analysis of 10 RCTs showed ~18.8 mg/dL reduction in fasting glucose, comparable to some pharmaceutical interventions. Fresh ginger is still useful for a different pathway: six-gingerol drives white-to-beige fat conversion (browning) in animal models. (2) Post-meal body weight exercise (2-3 sets of squats or push-ups, taken close to failure) activates AMPK independently of insulin, forcing GLUT4 translocation — a much stronger glucose sink than walking because it depletes ATP rapidly enough to trigger the emergency fuel signal. (3) Tahini (2-3 tbsp) — sesame lignans shift estrogen metabolism toward protective metabolites and partially free the testosterone axis from estrogen-mediated suppression; a 6-week RCT showed 39% reduction in the atherogenic index of plasma and significant triglyceride reduction; a separate study in T2D patients showed tahini prevented post-meal systolic blood pressure rise that occurred in the control (bread + margarine) group.

Rapamycin + Exercise: First RCT Disappoints, But the Story Isn't Over

The first human trial combining rapamycin with exercise returned null results for the longevity/performance hypothesis. Peter Attia's read is that one negative trial is insufficient to dismiss rapamycin as a geroprotector — the trial may have been underpowered or poorly designed — and he continues to view the mechanistic case (mTOR inhibition, senolytic effects, lifespan extension in every model organism tested) as compelling. Worth watching as the first real human data in this space.

Peter Attia

Isometric Training Is Not Inferior to Dynamic Training — The Evidence

Stronger by Science's deep-dive concludes that the conventional dismissal of isometrics for strength and hypertrophy is not evidence-based. The actual literature shows isometrics can produce comparable strength and size gains to dynamic training, with some advantages in joint-angle-specific strength and situations where range of motion is limited. Practically relevant for anyone doing rehab or programming around injuries.

Stronger by Science

Neanderthals Ran "Fat Factories" 125,000 Years Ago

A Leiden University study found evidence that Neanderthals systematically processed large animal carcasses specifically to extract and store fat — not just for immediate caloric needs but as a preserved, energy-dense resource. This reframes Neanderthal cognition and behavior as more strategically sophisticated than often depicted, and is an interesting data point for the evolutionary argument that animal fat has been a central human fuel source for hundreds of thousands of years.

Universiteit Leiden · HN Discussion

Group Brain Averages Obscure Individual Behavioral Control — Stanford Study

A Stanford study finds that averaging fMRI data across groups masks how individual brains actually control behavior — the neural signatures for specific behaviors vary significantly person to person, meaning population-level findings may be poor predictors for any given individual. This has direct implications for personalized medicine, cognitive enhancement, and any time you're applying group-level health data to yourself.
