Renaissance Periodization

Dr. Mike released a practical deep-dive on build-exercise mismatches — the idea that your anatomy (femur length, torso length, arm length) may make standard exercises functionally useless or even harmful for the target muscle. Specific findings: lifters with short torsos and long femurs will inevitably good-morning a squat and feel almost nothing in quads — the fix is ultra-wide stance, toes out 45°, high-bar only, chest vertical. Short lifters on leg press hit the machine's bottom before getting quad stretch — fix with feet lower, toes out, heel elevation or yoga blocks on hack squat. For lat pulldowns, tall lifters lose the stretch entirely — lean back 15-30° or go to pull-ups. The core principle: if you can't feel the target muscle, it's probably not a technique problem, it's a geometry problem, and you need either a technical modification or a replacement exercise.

GTO Lab

Ben Heath makes a short but counter-intuitive point about short-stack spots in tournaments: when the small blind is on 6bb and there's dead money from the big blind, the correct play is to call very wide rather than fold — hands like Q9o or 54s are actually printing money in this spot. The common autopilot error is treating these as easy folds when not paying full attention. He notes there's a future-game argument for folding the absolute weakest of these (you'd be button next with 5.5bb against deeper stacks), but most players are massively over-folding the bulk of this calling range.

Modern Wisdom

The standout full-length episode is a meditation on Shakespeare's line "Thus conscience does make cowards of us all" — reframed not as an attack on ethics but on self-awareness itself. The core argument: the same cognitive capacity that makes you reflective and strategic also makes you hesitate, because you simulate failure so vividly your body responds as if it already happened. The key asymmetry is that commission errors leave scars you can point to, while omission errors (the girl you never approached, the business you never started) leave nothing — so your brain never properly accounts for them. The prescription is front-loading the pain of inaction deliberately, essentially forcing yourself to feel the cost of not acting the same way you'd naturally feel the cost of failing. Separately, a Navy SEAL clip describes the highest-performing operators as people who maintained an unbroken daily routine of training, recovery (cryo, float tanks, e-stim), and professional work — long before it was culturally fashionable — which meant they were always "on" with no warm-up required.

🧬 Health & Science

Stronger by Science: Hypertrophy–Strength Correlation Revisited

A new analysis from Stronger by Science finds that hypertrophy contributes more to strength gains in newer lifters than prior studies suggested — but the relationship is far weaker than it appears at first glance, likely because many studies confound neurological adaptation with structural changes. The practical takeaway is nuanced: early training gains are still primarily neural, but muscle growth plays a larger concurrent role than the standard "first 6 months are all neural" narrative implies. Worth reading if you're programming for both size and strength simultaneously.

💡 Worth Reading

Reason Without Virtue Is Just Calculation

Massimo Pigliucci (Stoic philosopher, CUNY) argues that the Enlightenment project of separating reason from ethics was a category error — that rationality divorced from character produces sophisticated justification for bad ends, not wisdom. Directly relevant to the Stoic tradition (which always treated logic as inseparable from ethics) and to modern debates about AI alignment and technocratic decision-making.
