Thomas DeLauer

Solid deep-dive on Tongkat Ali and why the cortisol angle matters more than the testosterone headline. The core mechanism: cortisol suppresses testosterone at three separate levels (hypothalamic GnRH suppression, reduced LH from the pituitary, and direct impairment of Leydig cell synthesis), so a 16% cortisol drop produces a disproportionately large 37% increase in free testosterone. The study: 63 moderately stressed adults, 200mg/day for 4 weeks, measuring salivary (free) testosterone — not total serum, which makes the number more practically meaningful. Tongkat also reduces SHBG (locking up less testosterone) and inhibits aromatase (less converting to estrogen), so it's working three angles simultaneously. His practical protocol: dose 60–90 minutes after waking to avoid blunting the beneficial morning cortisol spike; add pumpkin seeds for zinc/magnesium to support the testosterone→DHT conversion downstream.

🧬 Health & Science

The US Dietary Guidelines Inversion — What Actually Changed

The new federal nutrition guidelines (summarized by Ekberg) represent the most substantive policy reversal in 50 years: seed oils go from 49 mentions/recommendations to zero, replaced by butter, tallow, and olive oil; protein recommendations nearly double (to 84–112g/day) and now explicitly include red meat and eggs; grains move from the foundation to the "limit" category; and for the first time, low-carb diets are flagged as worth discussing with a doctor for people with chronic disease. The guidelines also now explicitly state that the standard American diet caused the chronic disease epidemic — a remarkable self-indictment. Key caveat Ekberg flags: saturated fat is still capped at 10% of calories (22g at 2,000 calories), which is internally contradictory with recommending animal fats as the dietary foundation — in practice, following the new guidelines would put most people at 30–60g of saturated fat daily.

Tongkat Ali: Cortisol Suppression as the Primary Testosterone Mechanism

The DeLauer breakdown (sourced from an ISSN-published RCT, 63 subjects, 4 weeks, 200mg/day) reframes Tongkat Ali not as a testosterone booster but as a cortisol blocker — and the downstream testosterone effect is what you'd expect once the three-level suppression pathway (hypothalamus → pituitary → Leydig cells) is unblocked. The disproportionate ratio (16% cortisol drop → 37% free testosterone increase) is explained by Tongkat's additional mechanisms: SHBG reduction (more free testosterone in circulation) and aromatase inhibition (less testosterone converting to estrogen). Practical implication: the compound is likely most effective in chronically stressed or sleep-deprived people, less so in those with already-optimized lifestyle; timing 60–90 min post-waking avoids blunting the beneficial morning cortisol spike.

Grip Strength as All-Cause Mortality Predictor

Attia's cited data: each 5kg reduction in grip strength = 16% higher all-cause mortality in the subsequent year. In a separate prospective 7-year study of people in their 80s, survival curves separate clearly and monotonically by strength and muscle mass quartile. These are associational, not causal, but the signal is consistent enough across studies that Attia treats muscle maintenance as the single most important longevity variable in the second half of life — the practical implication being that training for strength (not just cardiovascular health) deserves priority long before old age.

💡 Worth Reading

Progress Against Dementia

Research published in JAMA shows the dementia rate has dropped substantially — 40 years ago, 3 in 10 Americans over a certain age developed dementia; that rate has declined significantly. This is one of the more quietly important public health data points of the decade, running counter to the general aging-population doom narrative, and suggests behavioral or treatment factors are bending the curve even as the population ages.

Mental Health: Rising Diagnosis, Flat Functioning Impairment

Research by Henking and Baumberg Geiger finds that while self-reported mental illness diagnoses among young Britons have risen steeply, the share reporting that a mental health problem actually limits their day-to-day functioning has barely moved. This is a meaningful distinction: it raises the question of whether we're seeing a real increase in suffering, a reclassification of normal human difficulty as diagnosable illness, or a cultural shift in willingness to report — with very different policy and personal implications depending on which it is.

Is the Correlation Between Hypertrophy and Strength Gains Stronger Than We Realized?

Stronger by Science revisits the hypertrophy-strength relationship and finds the correlation is stronger in newer lifters than prior research suggested — but with a critical caveat: the relationship is noisy enough that muscle size and strength should still be treated as largely distinct training outcomes. For programming purposes: you can't reliably predict strength gains from hypertrophy or vice versa, and optimizing specifically for one requires deliberately programming for it.

How Doctors Die (2016)

A widely-circulated essay resurfacing on HN: doctors, when facing terminal illness themselves, overwhelmingly choose comfort care over aggressive intervention — the opposite of what they often recommend or perform for patients. The essay argues this gap reveals what physicians actually believe about the value of heroic end-of-life treatment when they have full information and no financial or liability pressure distorting the decision. Worth sitting with as a lens on incentive structures in medicine and on how to think about end-of-life preferences before you're in the room.

Beyond the Inner Citadel — Pigliucci on the Limits of Stoic Self-Help

Massimo Pigliucci engages with historian Ludwig Edelstein's critique of Stoicism: the "inner citadel" model (your mind is always free, regardless of external circumstances) may be philosophically coherent but is psychologically incomplete — it underweights the degree to which social, relational, and material conditions shape what we can even will. Worth reading as a corrective to the more individualist, bootstrapping readings of Epictetus that dominate popular Stoicism content.

Stoicism Is Not Unemotional — Donald Robertson

Robertson pushes back on the popular caricature of the Stoic as emotionally flat. The Stoics distinguished between passions (irrational, destructive emotions) and eupatheiai (rational, appropriate emotional responses) — joy, caution, and wishing are Stoic-approved; glee, anxiety, and craving are not. Marcus Aurelius in the Meditations displays grief, frustration, affection, and gratitude throughout — he's not suppressing feeling, he's working to ensure his feelings track reality accurately.
