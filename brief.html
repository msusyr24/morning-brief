🧬 Health & Science

Antioxidant Supplements: The Evidence Against

Dr. Ekberg's video (above) is worth treating as a standalone health item because the data is specific and counterintuitive enough to warrant attention. The FDA removed ORAC values from its databases in 2012 after finding no meaningful relationship between ORAC scores and health outcomes. Two RCTs on beta-carotene were stopped early due to a 28% increase in cancer incidence; a 68-study meta-analysis of 230,000 people confirmed that beta-carotene, vitamin A, and vitamin E supplements all increased all-cause mortality. The mechanistic explanation: free radicals are intentional intracellular signals that activate NRF2, which upregulates catalase, superoxide dismutase, and glutathione — flooding the system with exogenous antioxidants suppresses this entire cascade. Isolated synthetic alpha-tocopherol also displaces gamma-tocopherol (one of eight natural vitamin E forms), creating a net deficiency in the displaced fraction. Exception: R-alpha lipoic acid, which recycles rather than replaces endogenous antioxidant enzymes.

Source: Dr. Sten Ekberg

Strength & Hypertrophy: The Correlation Revisited

The Stronger by Science analysis argues that prior studies underestimated how much muscle growth drives early strength gains in novice lifters — likely because short study durations captured neural adaptation as the dominant signal before hypertrophy had time to accrue. However, the relationship is described as "nowhere near as strong as it appears at first glance" once confounders are controlled, meaning the two adaptations remain meaningfully separable in programming terms. Practical implication: volume work that drives hypertrophy in beginners may be doing more for strength than the "neural adaptation first" framing suggests, but this shouldn't collapse the distinction between hypertrophy and strength programming at intermediate and advanced levels.

Source: Stronger by Science

Metformin and Cancer: Precision Over Optimism

Peter Attia's piece on metformin argues that after failing in prostate cancer trials and showing mixed results across other cancers, the drug's reputation as a broad cancer-protective agent deserves scrutiny. The key framing: metformin may have real benefit in specific cancer contexts (particularly those driven by hyperinsulinemia or mTOR overactivation) but the exceptions don't validate the general claim, and "active surveillance" — not enthusiasm — is the right posture for a drug that still lacks convincing RCT evidence for cancer prevention in non-diabetics.

Source: Peter Attia MD

💡 Worth Reading

New AI Tutor Achieves 0.71–1.30 SD Effect Size in Dartmouth Course

An AI tutoring system deployed in an actual Dartmouth course produced learning gains of 0.71 to 1.30 standard deviations — a range that, if it holds up, would represent among the largest effect sizes ever recorded for an educational intervention. For context, Bloom's "2-sigma problem" (1984) showed one-on-one human tutoring produces ~2 SD gains vs. classroom instruction; most ed-tech produces 0.2–0.4 SD. This is a single study and should be read carefully, but the signal is large enough to be worth tracking.

Marcus Aurelius Was Not an Emotionless Stoic

Donald Robertson, the leading popular biographer of Marcus Aurelius, pushes back on the common misreading of Stoicism as emotional suppression. The Meditations contain genuine grief, love, frustration, and self-criticism — none of which Marcus treated as failures. The Stoic goal is appropriate emotional response calibrated to what actually matters, not the elimination of feeling. Short excerpt, but useful corrective to the "Stoic = robot" pop-culture framing.

Source: Donald Robertson / Substack

The Lion-Man and the Birth of the Impossible

Massimo Pigliucci (Stoic philosopher, City University of New York) examines what a 40,000-year-old figurine — a human body with a lion's head — tells us about the cognitive leap that makes Homo sapiens distinct: the ability to conceive of things that do not and cannot exist. This capacity for "impossible thinking" is the root of both mythology and science. Short but philosophically interesting as a piece on what distinguishes human cognition.
