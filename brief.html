Jared Alderman

Alderman makes a sharp psychological point about poker and high-stakes decision-making: when your self-worth feels on the line at the table, your nervous system will always prioritize that threat over strategic thinking. You literally cannot access a rational decision-making mode when the deeper emotional need for self-validation is unresolved. The implication is that "fixing your game" is sometimes a self-work problem first, not a strategy problem — until those deep needs feel settled, the strategic layer stays inaccessible under pressure. This maps cleanly onto the inner-game literature (Gallwey, Jared's own coaching work) and is worth sitting with.

First, a guest argues that AI is creating a "simulacra of productivity" — constant back-and-forth that feels like deep work but is actually atrophying the capacity for sustained, unresolved attention that books build. Epstein also covers the maximizer vs. satisficer research: people who set "good enough" thresholds make decisions faster, experience less regret, and report higher life satisfaction than those who optimize exhaustively — with the counterintuitive finding that maximizers don't actually make better decisions despite the extra effort.

₿ Bitcoin & Macro

Bitcoin's New Debt Machine Is Facing Its First Major Test

June marked the first real stress test for Bitcoin-backed preferred shares (Strategy's STRC and Strive's SATA), which sold off sharply before rebounding — interpreted by BitcoinTreasuries.net as a signal the structure is holding. The broader thesis being tested: whether Bitcoin-collateralized corporate debt instruments can function as a sustainable capital market product through volatility, not just in a bull run. Worth watching as a leading indicator of whether the Saylor-style treasury strategy can scale to other corporate adopters.

JPMorgan: The Real Threat to Bitcoin Is Private Blockchains, Not Strategy

JPMorgan frames Strategy's Bitcoin selling as a short-term market concern but identifies the longer-term structural risk as institutional adoption of private/permissioned blockchains over public Bitcoin networks. The argument is that if banks and large institutions build their settlement infrastructure on private chains, it reduces demand for and relevance of the public Bitcoin network even as speculative interest remains high. A bearish thesis worth understanding even if you disagree with it.

🧬 Health & Science

Is the Correlation Between Hypertrophy and Strength Gains Stronger Than We Realized?

Stronger by Science revisits the relationship between muscle size and strength gains, finding that prior studies likely underestimated how much hypertrophy contributes to strength — particularly in novice lifters, where the muscle cross-sectional area increase appears to explain more of the strength gain than previously credited. The important caveat: the correlation is still far from 1:1, meaning neural adaptations remain a significant and separable contributor, and size doesn't reliably predict strength across individuals. Relevant practically: for beginners, programs that drive hypertrophy are simultaneously driving strength more than the old "neural adaptation first" framing suggested.

💡 Worth Reading

Stoicism Is Not Unemotional — Donald Robertson

Robertson pushes back on the popular misreading of Stoicism as emotional suppression, arguing that Marcus Aurelius in particular was deeply emotional — grieving, frustrated, moved — and that the Stoic goal was never to become "like a man made of oak or stone." The distinction the Stoics actually drew was between passions (irrational emotional disturbances) and good feelings (rational emotional responses consistent with virtue). Robertson has a companion excerpt from his Marcus Aurelius biography making the same case with primary source evidence. Useful corrective for anyone who's absorbed Stoicism through the "don't feel things" pop-philosophy filter.

Donald Robertson / Substack · also: Marcus Aurelius Was Not an Emotionless Stoic

Beyond the Inner Citadel — Massimo Pigliucci

Pigliucci examines Ludwig Edelstein's critique of Stoic self-sufficiency — the argument that the "inner citadel" (the idea that your ruling faculty is always fully within your control) is philosophically overstated and can become a limiting form of self-help ideology when taken too literally. The piece asks whether Stoicism's emphasis on individual rational control adequately accounts for social, political, and biological constraints on human agency. Worth reading as a serious internal critique of the tradition rather than a dismissal of it.

EU Parliament Greenlights Chat Control 1.0

The EU Parliament has approved a measure that would require scanning of private messages for child sexual abuse material — effectively mandating client-side surveillance of encrypted communications. The author (MEP Patrick Breyer, a long-standing opponent) argues this breaks end-to-end encryption in practice and sets a precedent for mass surveillance infrastructure regardless of the stated intent. A significant privacy and civil liberties development with direct implications for anyone using encrypted messaging in Europe, and potentially globally if other jurisdictions follow.
