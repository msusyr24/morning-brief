Worth your time:

Nick Norwitz

Norwitz — who has an LDL of 574 and total cholesterol over 700 on a ketogenic diet — explains why he's finally starting cholesterol-lowering medications after 7 years of refusing them. The twist: it has nothing to do with heart disease. He carries two copies of ApoE4 (the strongest known genetic risk factor for Alzheimer's, conferring 10–15x elevated risk), and two non-statin drugs — ezetimibe and bempedoic acid — have caught his attention specifically for neuroprotective mechanisms independent of their cholesterol-lowering effects. Ezetimibe appears to cross the blood-brain barrier and reduce amyloid buildup and tau aggregation (one human dataset showed 8x lower Alzheimer's risk in users), while a new May 2026 paper in Cell Metabolism shows bempedoic acid's prodrug form activates PPARα in the brain, shifting amyloid precursor protein processing toward a neuroprotective pathway. His framework for the whole dilemma — benefits are uncertain for metabolically healthy people, risks of statins are real (impaired mitochondrial function, worsened insulin resistance, measurable exercise performance drop in his own crossover trial), and "no decision is still a decision" — is a genuinely useful model for navigating medicine when you're an outlier the data wasn't built for.

Peter Attia

Two items worth noting. First, Attia and Layne Norton dig into whether industrial processing — not linoleic acid per se — is the real problem with seed oils. Norton walks through the hexane extraction process in detail: the solvent has a low boiling point (69°C), residual hexane in finished oils is 0.05–0.5 parts per million (often non-detectable), hexane toxicity is primarily from inhalation not ingestion, and the estimated amount you'd need to consume to get mild side effects is 11,340 kg of oil. His conclusion: hexane is probably not the mechanism of harm. The more live question remains the linoleic acid hypothesis itself and the shift from ~3% to ~10% of calories from linoleic acid over the past century. Second, Attia published on the first rapamycin-plus-exercise trial, which yielded disappointing results — but he explicitly notes this single negative trial hasn't changed his view on rapamycin as a potential geroprotector, and explains why.

Renaissance Periodization (Dr. Mike)

A pointed correction on row technique and lat training: rows do NOT allow the lat to reach anywhere near its halfway stretch point, and given that the lengthened portion of a range of motion is likely responsible for a disproportionate amount of hypertrophic stimulus, rows are primarily a back-thickening exercise (spinal erectors, mid-back), not a lat exercise. The effective lat exercises are overhead movements — pulldowns, pull-ups, and straight-arm work. He also endorses intentional spinal flexion and extension during rows (rounding then arching) as the correct way to row for back development.

🧬 Health & Science

Nick Norwitz: The Lipid Energy Model & ApoE4-Driven Treatment Decisions

The core scientific claim worth isolating: in lean, insulin-sensitive individuals on a low-carb diet, the body dramatically upregulates fat-based fuel transport, causing LDL to rise as a byproduct of metabolic adaptation — not pathology. Norwitz and colleagues published this as the "lipid energy model" in 2022. The implication is that cause matters for risk stratification: the same LDL number in a metabolically unhealthy person vs. a lean, active, low-carb person may carry very different cardiovascular risk profiles, and the entire evidence base for statin benefit comes from metabolically unhealthy populations. Separately, his ApoE4 situation surfaces a genuinely underappreciated consideration: ezetimibe's brain-penetrant effects on amyloid and autophagy, and bempedoic acid's PPARα activation in neural tissue, are mechanisms essentially orthogonal to cardiovascular risk — drug labels don't capture this.

Nick Norwitz: I Changed My Mind on Cholesterol Medications

Peter Attia + Layne Norton: Seed Oil Processing — What the Evidence Actually Says

Norton's detailed walkthrough of hexane extraction concludes that hexane residue in finished seed oils (0.05–0.5 ppm, often non-detectable) is implausible as a mechanism of harm — primary hexane toxicity is from inhalation, not ingestion, and bioaccumulation doesn't occur. The more defensible concern is the compositional shift: linoleic acid went from ~3% to ~10% of total caloric availability over the past century. The processing question remains genuinely open for oxidation products generated during high-heat commercial cooking (frying, reheating), which wasn't the focus of this segment but is a real gap in the "processing is fine" argument.

Peter Attia: Is Industrial Processing the Real Problem With Seed Oils?

Rapamycin + Exercise: First Trial Results Disappoint

The first trial combining rapamycin with exercise produced negative results. Attia's commentary: a single negative trial in a complex intervention (where dose, timing, and exercise modality all matter enormously) is insufficient evidence to revise a prior on rapamycin's geroprotective potential. This is a useful example of how to weight a single RCT against a mechanistic prior — the trial result is real information but not necessarily decisive.
