GTO Wizard

A high-stakes PLO player breaks down the "SIP method" (Simplify, Identify 80/20, find Patterns) for actually improving win rate with solvers. The core insight: stop memorizing frequencies combo-by-combo and instead learn the thresholds that change strategy — e.g., in a given spot, "pair + open-ender always continues, bare pair almost always folds," and everything else collapses into that one rule. He argues that mixing is almost never correct in practice — solvers mix because of indifference across subgroups, not because you should randomize with a specific hand. On sizing, he reduces all flops to two buckets: locked boards (paired, monotone, straight-heavy) use 1/4–1/3 pot; regular boards default to half pot. The framework lets you encode an entire postflop strategy in a handful of heuristics you can actually execute under pressure.

Videos: How Pros Actually Study PLO

Nick Norwitz

Norwitz covers a genuinely strange finding: three separate human controlled trials found that reducing protein to ~9% of total calories increased total energy expenditure by roughly 600 calories/day — even on a high-fat, high-carb diet, and with no meaningful muscle loss. He tested it on himself, eating 500+ calories/day above maintenance on low protein, and still lost 6.4 lbs over several weeks. A new Nature paper he discusses proposes the mechanism: low protein shifts the gut microbiome, which alters secondary bile acid signaling, which simultaneously activates two metabolic pathways (FXR + FGF21) that together trigger "beige fat" thermogenesis — white fat transforming into a more metabolically active state. Crucially, fecal transplant experiments confirmed it's the microbiome driving the effect causally; researchers then identified a four-strain bacterial cocktail (HU4) that reproduced the fat-browning effect without protein restriction, pointing toward a future probiotic that could mimic the metabolic benefit without sacrificing muscle.

🧬 Health & Science

Low Protein → +600 cal/day Energy Expenditure: What's the Mechanism?

Nick Norwitz's video (covered above in YouTube) is the most substantive health science item today. The specific mechanism from the Nature paper: protein restriction → microbiome composition shift → altered secondary bile acid signaling → simultaneous activation of FXR and FGF21 pathways (a "dual lock") → white-to-beige fat transformation → increased thermogenesis. The fecal transplant experiments established causality — mice receiving microbiomes from high-brown-fat humans showed increased fat browning; those receiving microbiomes from low-brown-fat humans did not. The HU4 four-strain bacterial cocktail amplified the effect in mice, showing greater fat loss and lower triglycerides than controls. The open question: can this be reproduced in humans via a targeted probiotic without the muscle-risk tradeoffs of low protein intake?

Nick Norwitz

GLP-1s and Muscle Loss: New Data (Peter Attia AMA #86)

Attia's headline claim: when patients on GLP-1 receptor agonists are properly counseled on protein intake and resistance training, lean mass loss is minimal — the horror stories in the literature largely reflect inadequately coached patients. He doesn't cite a specific study number in the excerpt but frames it as "new data" changing the clinical picture. The practical implication: the muscle-loss concern is real but addressable with protocol, not a reason to avoid GLP-1s for weight loss.

💡 Worth Reading

Claude Code Is Steganographically Watermarking Its Requests

A researcher discovered that Claude Code embeds hidden signals in the whitespace/formatting of its API requests — invisible to users but detectable by Anthropic — allowing them to identify when requests originate from their own coding tool vs. direct API calls. This is a form of steganographic watermarking applied not to model outputs but to inputs, raising real questions about transparency: users interacting with Claude Code didn't know this was happening. The practical implication is modest (it's not altering content), but the principle — that AI tools can silently tag your behavior without disclosure — is worth understanding.

Why Social Media Conversations Get More Negative Over Time

A study of over 2 billion Reddit comments finds that online dialogue becomes more negative over time within topic-bound communities, and the mechanism is differentiation — participants compete to stand out by being more critical, contrarian, or extreme than prior commenters, creating a ratchet toward negativity that's structurally incentivized rather than reflecting actual sentiment change. Clean finding with obvious implications for how you should weight what you read online.

The Lion-Man and the Birth of the Impossible (Massimo Pigliucci)

Pigliucci uses the 40,000-year-old Lion-Man figurine — a chimeric sculpture of human and lion, discovered in a German cave — as a lens into what makes human cognition distinctive: the capacity for counterfactual and combinatorial imagination, to represent things that don't exist and couldn't. The philosophical payoff is a richer account of what "reason" actually is, grounded in cognitive archaeology rather than abstract definition. Compact and genuinely interesting.
