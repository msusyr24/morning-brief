Mental Model · Problem-solving

Five Whys

Toyota's technique: when a problem surfaces, ask 'why?' five times. Each answer gets you closer to root cause. The first answer is always a symptom, not a cause. Munger uses this in business analysis: 'Why is this company losing money?' → five layers deep, you usually find a cultural or strategic issue, not an operational one.

🎥 From People I Follow

Worth your time:

GTO Wizard

A structured, practical guide to actually learning from solvers rather than just memorizing outputs. The core argument: most players fail because they try to copy solver frequencies rather than understand why the solver acts — how range advantages change sizing decisions, why an Ace-King-2 board calls for an overbet (button dominates the top of range) while Ace-8-2 doesn't (big blind has sets and two-pair too). The recommended workflow: use aggregated reports for broad heuristics first, then build custom solutions with 4 bet sizes (25/50/75/125% pot), set a specific study goal (not "studying BTN vs BB" but "BTN vs BB on two-broadway-one-low flops"), and use node-locking to model opponent mistakes and find exploits. Key exploit example: against a passive player who won't raise enough on the river, your strongest flushes should shift from small-size (designed to induce raises) to large-size, since you're leaving money on the table when the raise never comes.

Videos: How to Study Effectively With GTO Wizard · Study Effectively With GTO Wizard (Short)

GTO Lab

Alex Kulev illustrates river splitting — using two bet sizes on the same node — with a concrete example involving quads. The reason to split: when your range contains both nut hands (four aces) and strong-but-not-nuts hands (king-x full houses plus bluffs), a single size leaves value on the table. The nuts want maximum size; the strong-but-vulnerable hands want a smaller size that can still fold out weaker holdings without risking a raise they can't call off. A clean, precise illustration of why polarization alone doesn't determine sizing — range composition within the nut tier matters.

Video: Alex Kulev explains SPLITTING on the RIVER

Jared Alderman

A reframe of confidence for poker players: instead of trying to fix leaks during a downswing (which keeps your attention on deficit and erodes confidence), anchor your identity to moments when you were performing at your best — what you were actually doing then, and doing more of it. His coach's prompt was: "When were you most successful, and what were the two or three things you were doing?" The meta-point is that poker culture over-indexes on leak-fixing and almost never encourages players to mark and revisit hands they played well. Doing so builds a more durable confidence than result-by-result feedback loops.

Video: Why Fixing Your Leaks Is Killing Your Confidence

Dr. Sten Ekberg

A methodical critique of standard fatty liver "superfood" lists (oatmeal, green tea, blueberries). His central claim: roughly 70% of the work in reversing fatty liver is reversing insulin resistance, because fat accumulates in the liver via de novo lipogenesis — the liver converting excess carbohydrates (especially fructose, which only the liver can process) into fat, driven by insulin. Dietary fat does not meaningfully raise insulin and therefore cannot produce a fatty liver, making "avoid saturated fat" advice actively misleading. The superfoods on standard lists — oatmeal (raises blood sugar), blueberries, green tea, herbs/spices — have real but marginal benefits (he assigns them roughly 5% of the impact), and do nothing to address the insulin/fructose pipeline. Coffee gets credit for reducing liver enzymes (ALT) and fibrosis risk via chlorogenic acids, which also work in decaf — making it a partial exception.

Renaissance Periodization (Dr. Mike)

Two sharp, evidence-referenced clips. First: women often respond better to lower-rep training (5–10 reps) than high-rep training (20–30) for hypertrophy — not because the physiology differs, but because the psychology does. At rep 22 of a set of 30, the lactic acid pain triggers premature termination before true muscular failure; sets of 5–10 reach mechanical failure without that pain signal. The practical fix is simply to put more weight on the bar. Second: the "lifting makes women bulky" fear is physiologically unfounded — female hormonal and genetic profiles strongly resist mass gain, and women eating at a caloric deficit while lifting will typically lose fat while gaining muscle, resulting in net weight loss with improved body composition.

Thomas DeLauer

A detailed breakdown of autophagy signs and amplification tactics. Key signals: mental clarity (neurons fire with less oxidative interference), vivid dreams with strong recall (increased BDNF and synaptic plasticity), "hunter mode" visual sharpening (elevated catecholamines + consolidated glutamate-glutamine cycling), "good hunger" that feels like a churn rather than hanginess (ghrelin directly drives autophagy), and an energy shift around hour 15–16 when glycogen depletes and mitophagy kicks in at the mitochondrial level. Amplification tactics cited: cold exposure for brain autophagy, 1–2g spermidine (or aged cheese/mushrooms), 200–400mg theanine (suppresses mTOR), 5g glutamine to support brain fuel cycling, loading creatine before a fast to bank energy for cleanup processes, and zone-2 walking at the tail end of a fast to extend it without breaking it. He also flags when to turn autophagy off — specifically around muscle-building windows — since autophagy and mTOR are largely antagonistic.

🧬 Health & Science

Hold Still: What Does the Evidence Actually Say About Isometric Training for Strength and Hypertrophy?

Stronger by Science reviews the evidence on isometric (static contraction) training, pushing back on the conventional wisdom that it's clearly inferior to dynamic training for building strength and muscle. The piece examines the actual mechanisms — joint angle specificity, time under tension, motor unit recruitment — and argues current evidence doesn't cleanly support dismissing isometrics, particularly for injury rehabilitation contexts or as a supplement to dynamic work. Worth reading if you're building or optimizing a training program and have written off isometrics entirely.

Orwell's classic essay on his four motives for writing: sheer egoism, aesthetic enthusiasm, historical impulse, and political purpose — and how he came to see all good prose as a struggle to fuse those motives without one overwhelming the others. 980 HN points suggests it hit a nerve this week; it's one of the best short pieces ever written on what it actually means to want to make something.
