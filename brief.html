Worth your time:

Nick Norwitz

Norwitz opens with a personal stake: he carries two copies of APOE4, the major Alzheimer's risk gene, giving him a 10–15x elevated risk — which he discovered right after starting his neuroscience PhD. His core argument is that DHA isn't just good for the brain, it's structurally constitutive of it (40% of gray matter fatty acids), and that lipid metabolism dysfunction is the first domino to fall in Alzheimer's — preceding amyloid plaques, tau tangles, and cognitive decline. The key mechanistic insight for APOE4 carriers: they burn through DHA 77% faster than non-carriers (shorter whole-body half-life), yet younger APOE4 carriers paradoxically show increased DHA uptake into the brain — a compensatory mechanism that degrades with age. His practical conclusion: high DHA intake throughout life may be the most actionable intervention for APOE4 carriers, and explains why DHA trials in people who already have Alzheimer's tend to fail — the transport systems that incorporate DHA have already degraded. He monitors his omega-3 index with OmegaQuant, avoids industrialized seed oils (citing 4-HNE formation from oxidized omega-6), and doesn't avoid whole-food omega-6 sources like walnuts.

Video: The #1 Nutrient For Brain Health (It's Not Even Close)

GTO Wizard (ft. Tombos21 & Joey Ingram)

This is the launch walkthrough of GTO Wizard's new PLO solver — the first time PLO has been available in the tool. Tombos and Ingram work through several key conceptual shifts for hold'em players: (1) PLO features dramatically more pre-flop calling and less 3-betting because equities run closer and pot inflation out of position is more costly; (2) When facing a 4-bet, 86% of the 4-betting range consists of hands containing pocket aces — meaning KK is a near-pure fold in a 4-bet pot, which violates hold'em intuition; (3) The central skill shift is from "what is my hand strength now?" to "what will my hand strength be by the river?" — back-door draws and future blockers matter enormously. Tombos demonstrates that check-to-aggressor as a heuristic is far less reliable in PLO than in hold'em because leading ranges are much more active. The solver shows the hijack calling more than 3-betting vs. an early position open — counterintuitive to cash game hold'em players but explained by the equity flatness of PLO hands.

Video: PLO in GTO Wizard ft. Tombos21 & Joey Ingram

Jared Alderman (ft. Brian Hastings)

Hastings and Alderman get into a genuinely interesting meta-strategy debate: when is RNG-ing (using a randomizer) appropriate vs. lazy? Hastings argues for randomizing early in the game tree where information is scarce (e.g., AK facing a UTG open at 90bb), but almost never on the river where one action is clearly better. Alderman pushes back with an alternative theory: instead of striving for theoretical balance, he prefers being willing to do anything in a given spot — bluff or not bluff, value-thin or fold — without tipping which direction, which creates a similar opponent uncertainty without requiring precise frequency management. Hastings's counterpoint is that this is harder to execute correctly than chasing balance. The conversation also revisits Hastings's famous session beating Isildur1 for $4M+ over 3-4 hours of 6-tabled heads-up play, and his candid account of the post-Black Friday depression that followed — moving back in with his parents at 23.

Ben Felix

A rigorous, research-dense video arguing that the entire point of personal finance is funding a good life — not maximizing wealth. Felix walks through the PERMA-V model of well-being (positive emotion, engagement, relationships, meaning, accomplishment, vitality) and synthesizes several landmark income-happiness studies: the 2021 study found no satiation point, but a 2023 adversarial collaboration reconciled the conflict by finding that happier people continue gaining well-being with income while less-happy people plateau. The crucial nuance: the income scale in these studies is logarithmic — each "step up" is a doubling — and even then, the correlation between income and happiness is only 0.09, and the difference in hedonic well-being between $15K and $250K households is about 5 points on a 100-point scale. Practically: trading time for money is usually a bad deal, noise and commuting are two things humans don't adapt to, and social comparison is a silent drain that causes affluent households to live beyond their means. The strongest finding: prioritizing time over money leads to better relationships, more enjoyable work, and greater happiness — at the cost of less money.

🧬 Health & Science

Hold Still: What Does the Evidence Actually Say About Isometric Training for Strength and Hypertrophy?

Stronger by Science reviews the evidence on isometric (static contraction) training and finds that the conventional wisdom — that isometrics are inferior to dynamic training — isn't well-supported. The article distinguishes between joint-angle specificity (a real limitation: strength gains from isometrics transfer best at or near the trained angle) and overall magnitude of strength/hypertrophy gain, where well-designed isometric protocols are more competitive than assumed. Key practical implication: isometrics may have underappreciated utility for rehab, tendon loading, and as a supplementary modality, especially for people who can't tolerate dynamic loading.

Stronger by Science

Growth Hormone for Musculoskeletal System Repair — Peter Attia

Attia's summary conclusion: despite a compelling theoretical mechanism (GH drives IGF-1, which stimulates tissue repair), exogenous growth hormone has not delivered on its promise for adult musculoskeletal repair in clinical evidence. The gap between mechanistic plausibility and actual outcome data is the point worth sitting with — a good reminder for evaluating other interventions with strong-sounding mechanisms but weak clinical track records.

The Brand Age — Paul Graham

PG argues we're entering an era where brands matter more, not less — because AI is making it harder to evaluate quality directly (everything looks polished), so people will increasingly rely on trust signals and reputation as substitutes for direct quality assessment. Short and worth sitting with given how counterintuitive it is relative to the "AI commoditizes everything" thesis.

Paul Graham

The Real Cost of Owning a Home

A careful accounting of homeownership costs that most analyses undercount: property taxes, maintenance (historically ~1–2% of value/year), opportunity cost of the down payment, insurance, transaction costs on entry and exit, and the illiquidity premium. The piece doesn't argue against buying but gives an honest total-cost framework that most "rent vs. buy" calculators bury. Well-timed given Ben Felix's related discussion of commute, adaptation, and housing decisions in today's YouTube section.

How to Cope with Boethius — Massimo Pigliucci

Part of Pigliucci's Ancient Wisdom for Modern Readers series. Boethius wrote The Consolation of Philosophy while awaiting execution — arguably the most extreme test of philosophical equanimity imaginable — and the text became one of the most widely read books in medieval Europe. Pigliucci examines what Boethius actually argues (that Fortune's wheel is indifferent, that true goods are internal, that Providence gives the framework for suffering) and how it holds up as a framework for dealing with irreversible loss. Sits in the same tradition as Stoicism but with an interesting neo-Platonic twist.

Figs in Winter / Substack

Seven Ways to Avoid Losing Your Job to AI — Tyler Cowen

Cowen's most interesting principle from the excerpt: "Run experiments." His framing is that AI will generate an enormous volume of hypotheses — for drugs, battery tech, materials science, and far beyond — and the comparative advantage for humans shifts toward being a skilled tester and validator rather than a generator of ideas. The broader argument is about complementarity rather than replacement: find roles where human judgment, physical presence, or regulatory trust is a bottleneck that AI can't substitute.
