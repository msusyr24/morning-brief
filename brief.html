Ken Berry MD & Dr. David Diamond

Dr. David Diamond — a neuroscientist (not a cardiologist) who has had documented LDL of ~200 mg/dL for over 20 years with zero cardiac events — makes the case that the statin-LDL paradigm is built on financial conflicts of interest and relative risk manipulation. His core argument: the American Heart Association takes nearly $1 billion annually from food and pharma industry sponsors, which corrupts guidelines; practicing physicians aren't lying, they're "sheepishly following" standard of care out of liability fear. Diamond's personal data is striking: his triglycerides were 700–1000 on a high-carb diet; cutting carbs to 50–100g/day dropped them to ~150, and his ratio went from 25:1 to ~3:1. He also walks through familial hypercholesterolemia research showing that people with genetically very high LDL typically live to ~80 — data published in the American Heart Association's own journals but ignored by the clinical community. The show also covers the "lean mass hyperresponder" phenomenon and the inadequacy of LDL as an isolated risk marker without metabolic context.

GTO Wizard (Tombos21 & Joey Ingram)

First-ever GTO Wizard PLO livestream, functioning as both a product walkthrough and a genuine theory discussion. Key insight from Tombos: in PLO, a 4-bet range is ~86% pocket aces, which means hands like KK that would go all-in in NLH are correct folds — a fundamental mindset shift from "I have a big pair" to "what are my nut-making odds by the river?" Joey emphasizes that calling is correct far more often than 3-betting in PLO because equities run close pre-flop, you rarely want to inflate pots OOP, and even seemingly weak 4-card hands have enormous post-flop potential in position. Tombos flags that the common NLH heuristic of "check to the pre-flop aggressor" breaks down in PLO because leading ranges exist on many more boards — understanding back-door draws and future blockers is what separates beginner from advanced play, not current hand strength.

Jared Alderman — Brian Hastings Interview

A nuanced pre-flop and river strategy conversation with six-bracelet winner Brian Hastings. The central tension: Hastings argues for RNG-ing mixed pre-flop spots (e.g., AK facing an EP open at 90bb) because he lacks confidence that one action is clearly superior, while Jared pushes back that RNG gets lazier and worse as you go deeper in the decision tree. On the river, Hastings makes an interesting case for intentional imbalance: rather than striving for exact GTO balance (which he believes is practically unachievable given range composition errors accumulated over earlier streets), he prefers being genuinely willing to bluff a lot OR shut down entirely — keeping opponents uncertain about which way you lean in a given spot. His example: a KT check-jam at 10% frequency on the river — if your range of KT arrives at that spot at a higher frequency than the solver expects (because you didn't mix on earlier streets), the theoretically correct 10% should actually be lower, making precise balance impossible on the fly. The discussion of his $4M session against Isildur1 is briefly touched — Hastings won roughly $4.1M over 3–4 hours of six-tabled heads-up.

Peter Attia & Layne Norton — Seed Oils Deep Dive

Attia and Norton tackle the industrial processing objection to seed oils — the argument that it's not linoleic acid itself but hexane extraction, repeated heating, and chemical modification that cause harm. Norton's conclusion on hexane: residual levels in refined oils are typically 0.05–0.5 parts per million, hexane toxicity is primarily via inhalation not ingestion, and to produce even mild side effects from dietary hexane intake would require consuming ~11,340 kg of oil. On oxidation, most seed oils require sustained temperatures well above 200°C for hours to oxidize meaningfully — the steam stripping used to remove hexane is brief (~60–90 min) and at much lower temps. The conversation then pivots to the more substantive concern: linoleic acid's share of total food availability has risen from under 3% a century ago to ~10% today — this population-level shift in the omega-6 to omega-3 ratio, rather than any single processing step, may be the actual mechanism worth investigating.

🧬 Health & Science

LDL, Statins, and the Relative Risk Problem — Dr. David Diamond

Diamond's core methodological critique: statin trials report relative risk reductions (e.g., "30% fewer heart attacks") while absolute risk reductions are typically 1–2 percentage points — a distinction that makes drugs appear dramatically more effective than they are. His clinical observation: patients with high LDL but no other metabolic risk factors (normal weight, normal blood pressure, low inflammation, low triglycerides) show no excess cardiovascular mortality in the peer-reviewed literature, including studies published in AHA journals. The implication for low-carb/carnivore dieters specifically: the lean mass hyperresponder pattern (high LDL, high HDL, low triglycerides) appears to be mechanistically distinct from the atherogenic dyslipidemia pattern (high LDL, low HDL, high triglycerides) that drives actual risk — and conflating them in clinical guidelines is, in Diamond's view, the operative error.

CRISPR Gene Editing Achieves 62% LDL Reduction via Liver-Targeted Lipid Nanoparticles

A clinical trial using CRISPR packaged inside lipid nanoparticles programmed to target only hepatic cells achieved a one-time 62% LDL reduction. The significance, per Nick Norwitz, is tissue-specificity: the gene editor reaches the liver but not germline cells, brain, or muscle — meaning it's not heritable and can in principle be adapted to deliver editing tools to specific target tissues. This is the delivery mechanism breakthrough; once you can reliably target an organ, you can begin engineering therapies for Alzheimer's (brain), cancer (immune cells), and sarcopenia (muscle).

Nick Norwitz

Isometric Training — What Does the Evidence Actually Say?

Stronger by Science reviews the evidence on isometric (static contraction) training for strength and hypertrophy and argues the common assumption that it's inferior to dynamic training is not well-supported by the current literature. The piece distinguishes between joint-angle-specific strength gains (real limitation of isometrics) and overall hypertrophy stimulus (more comparable to dynamic training than commonly assumed), and covers practical applications for injury management and supplementary strength work.

Stronger by Science

Testosterone Decline and Plastics — Paul Saladino

Saladino cites data suggesting average testosterone in men aged 30–40 has fallen from roughly 800 ng/dL in the 1920s to ~400–450 ng/dL today — a ~45% population-level collapse over a century. His proposed mechanisms beyond diet: endocrine-disrupting chemicals (EDCs) from plastics in food containers, paper cups lined with plastic, and personal care products. Specific actionable claim: zinc deficiency (serum zinc below 80) can suppress testosterone significantly, and supplementation alone may double levels in deficient individuals — a lower-hanging intervention than TRT that most physicians skip. The broader framing is that erectile dysfunction in men in their 30s and 40s is a metabolic and environmental disease, not a natural aging phenomenon.

💡 Worth Reading

The Dead Economy Theory

One of the highest-voted pieces on HN today. The essay argues that large swaths of the Western economy have become "dead" — captured by incumbent incumbents, regulatory moats, and credential gatekeeping in ways that prevent new entrants from competing on merit. The core claim is that productivity stagnation isn't a technology problem, it's a competition problem: we've built economic structures that preserve existing players rather than reward better ones. Worth reading as a framework for thinking about why growth feels hollow despite apparent innovation.

Using Your Money To Be Happier — Ben Felix

Felix surveys the empirical literature on income and well-being with more rigor than the usual "money can't buy happiness" takes. Key findings worth sitting with: the correlation between log income and happiness is 0.09 in experience-sampling data; the happiness difference between $15K and $250K household income is about 5 points on a 100-point scale; people who prioritize time over money are measurably happier and have better relationships even though it means less money. The 2023 adversarial collaboration between the conflicting 2010 and 2021 studies reconciles the discrepancy: happy people's well-being keeps rising with income, unhappy people's plateaus. The practical application is designing your financial life around how you'll spend your time, not around achieving a static future wealth number your future self may not even want.

Are We Undermeasuring Inflation for Lower Earners?

New research documented by Tyler Cowen finds a structural bias in how inflation is measured across income groups: when upstream input costs rise, absolute price increases tend to be similar across product quality tiers — but the same absolute increase is a larger percentage change on cheaper goods. This means low-price products (which lower-income households disproportionately buy) face systematically higher inflation rates, and standard CPI measures that focus on percentage changes understate this. A clean data finding with real distributional policy implications.
