Thomas DeLauer

A Japanese rice variety called RD43 was crossbred (not GMO) to have a higher amylose content and a V-type crystalline starch structure where amylose wraps around fatty acid chains — making it physically resistant to digestive enzymes. The key distinction: this isn't just slowing glucose absorption (lower GI), it's converting rapidly digestible starch into starch that doesn't get absorbed in the small intestine at all. Human trial data: 34 pre-diabetic subjects who swapped to RD43 for 12 weeks showed drops in fasting glucose, fasting insulin, HbA1c, HOMA-IR, and visceral fat. A second RCT in gestational diabetes found only 6% of the RD43 group needed insulin therapy vs. 23% in the jasmine rice group — a 73% reduction. Practical hack: you can approximate this at home by cooking rice with a small amount of oil (forms the same V-type structure), then refrigerating overnight and reheating — which raises resistant starch ~2.5x. Adding vinegar or lemon juice further slows enzymatic breakdown. Second video flags a real selenium danger in Brazil nuts: a single nut can contain anywhere from 1–500 micrograms of selenium depending on soil, with no way to tell from appearance. Selenium incorporates into selenoproteins in tissue (not urine-cleared), so daily dosing from a high-selenium batch can push you well past the 400mcg tolerable upper limit within days. Recommendation: 1–2 nuts per week maximum, and check your multivitamin's selenium content first.

Dr. Sten Ekberg

Ekberg traces the origin of the food pyramid to a 1974 Swedish affordability crisis — it was explicitly designed to help people eat cheaply, not healthfully — and notes that the US and most of the world adopted it wholesale, then spent 50 years reverse-engineering scientific justification for it. The new RFK-era inverted pyramid represents the most radical federal nutrition shift ever: protein and animal fats as the foundation, grains reduced to "avoid," seed oils completely removed (49 prior mentions → zero), full-fat dairy preferred over low-fat, saturated fat no longer demonized, and low-carb diets mentioned as a legitimate option for the first time. Ekberg's one critique: the new guidelines cap saturated fat at 10% of calories (~22g), which is internally contradictory given they also recommend animal fats as a foundation — in practice, following the new guidelines will put most people at 30–60g of saturated fat, which he argues is fine.

GPT-5.6 Sol Produces a Proof of the Cycle Double Cover Conjecture

The Cycle Double Cover Conjecture is a long-standing open problem in graph theory — it states that every bridgeless graph has a collection of cycles that cover every edge exactly twice. OpenAI is claiming GPT-5.6 Sol has produced a proof. If verified by mathematicians, this would be a landmark: not a benchmark, not a contest problem, but a genuine open conjecture in combinatorics that has resisted proof for decades. Treat with appropriate skepticism until peer review, but this is worth watching closely.

Beyond the Inner Citadel — Massimo Pigliucci on the Limits of Stoic Self-Help

Pigliucci (one of the best modern Stoicism interpreters) engages with Ludwig Edelstein's critique of Stoicism: that the philosophy's emphasis on the "inner citadel" — radical self-sufficiency and withdrawal from outcomes — can tip into a kind of self-absorption that misunderstands what the good life actually requires. Worth reading as a corrective to the way Stoicism is often packaged in pop culture as pure detachment, when the ancient Stoics were deeply committed to civic engagement and relationship.

Stoicism Is Not Unemotional — Donald Robertson

Robertson pushes back on the most common misconception about Stoicism: that the goal is emotional flatness or suppression. The Stoics distinguished between destructive passions (which stem from false value judgments) and what they called "good emotions" (eupatheiai) — joy, caution, well-wishing — which are the natural result of correct reasoning. Marcus Aurelius in the Meditations repeatedly expresses grief, love, and admiration; the goal was never to become, in Epictetus's phrase, "a man made of oak or stone."
