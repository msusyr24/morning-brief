Nick Norwitz

Norwitz self-experimented with Retatrutide (informally called "GLP-3"), the next-gen weight loss drug hitting three hormonal pathways: GLP-1 (appetite suppression), GIP (fuel partitioning), and glucagon (energy expenditure increase). At just 1-2mg/week — far below trial doses of 12mg — he lost 12 lbs in a month while eating 3,000+ calories/day, and his resting heart rate jumped from the low 40s to 65-70 bpm. Key finding: the glucagon-driven metabolic boost hit before appetite suppression for him, causing him to be hungrier initially while still losing fat. He also found his C-reactive protein rose (possibly from accelerated fat mobilization causing mild inflammation, not the drug directly), and notes the published human data show ~38% of weight loss comes from lean mass at higher doses — directly contradicting Andrew Huberman's on-air claim that retatrutide is "muscle sparing."

Peter Attia (The Drive)

Deep dive on brain lipidology with Dr. Tom Dayspring. The central and underappreciated fact: the brain contains 20-25g of cholesterol — 20x more than the liver — and it operates as a completely closed system with zero exchange with plasma lipoproteins. This directly refutes the common fear that lowering LDL will "deprive the brain of cholesterol." The brain synthesizes all its own cholesterol via astrocytes and manages its own lipoprotein recycling system (using ApoE as the carrier, not ApoB), entirely independent of what circulates in your blood. This has major implications for understanding APOE4 risk in Alzheimer's — the brain's cholesterol homeostasis machinery, not plasma LDL levels, is the relevant system.

Stronger by Science published a thorough review challenging the conventional wisdom that isometrics are inferior to dynamic training. The piece examines why joint-angle specificity (isometrics build strength mainly at the trained angle) has historically been seen as a fatal limitation, but argues the evidence doesn't actually support the sweeping conclusion that isometrics are less effective for hypertrophy. Worth reading if you program around injury or have range-of-motion constraints — the practical implications may be more favorable than the dogma suggests.

Brain Cholesterol Is a Closed System — Implications for Alzheimer's and Statin Use

From the Peter Attia / Tom Dayspring episode: the blood-brain barrier completely excludes plasma lipoproteins; the brain runs its own cholesterol synthesis and recycling via astrocytes and ApoE (not ApoB). This means lowering plasma LDL has no direct effect on brain cholesterol supply — the "statins will starve your brain" concern is mechanistically unfounded. The relevant question for Alzheimer's risk is how well ApoE4 manages cholesterol recycling within the brain's closed system, which is a separate and poorly understood problem from cardiovascular lipidology.

The Drive Podcast, Episode 395

Sleep Deprivation Increases Caloric Intake ~300 cal/day via Sex-Specific Hormonal Pathways

Dr. St-Onge's controlled study (9hr vs. ~4hr sleep for 5 nights): men showed elevated ghrelin (hunger hormone up); women showed reduced GLP-1 (satiety hormone down). Both pathways led to the same behavioral outcome — roughly 300 extra calories consumed per day in the sleep-deprived condition. The sex difference had been invisible in prior research because most studies used male-only samples. Extrapolated over weeks: this is a plausible mechanism for 3500+ calorie surplus accumulation, i.e., roughly 1 lb of fat gain per 10-12 days of sleep deprivation without any conscious dietary change.

Donald Robertson on a Stoic-adjacent technique: rather than trying to relax away anxiety, the practice is learning to relax into it — using the out-breath as the moment of acceptance rather than suppression. The distinction matters because fighting anxiety tends to amplify it (the control paradox), while leaning into the physiological signal with equanimity is both more effective and more philosophically consistent with Stoic indifference to externals. Short piece, useful framing.

DiffusionGemma

Google has released DiffusionGemma, a diffusion-based language model (as opposed to the standard autoregressive architecture) that Simon Willison previewed running at 857 tokens/second. Diffusion LMs generate text by denoising rather than predicting left-to-right, which may unlock different speed/quality tradeoffs and novel generation patterns — this is a genuinely different architecture going public, not a variant on existing Gemini models.
