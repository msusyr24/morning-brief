₿ Bitcoin & Macro

VanEck Flags Dual Bullish Signals for Bitcoin

VanEck's analysts are flagging a setup they historically associate with strong forward returns: deeply negative funding rates (indicating bearish positioning in perpetual futures) combined with a clustering of hash rate declines (typically marking miner capitulation). Both signals together have historically preceded significant price recoveries. Worth noting as a data point rather than a forecast.

Bitcoin Magazine

Will AI Save the U.S. Fiscal Situation?

A serious macro framing worth sitting with: even a modest 0.1 percentage point increase in productivity growth from AI raises the fundamental value of U.S. government debt by ~$1.3 trillion and would imply Treasury yields roughly 70 basis points lower than current pricing. At 0.5 pp of extra growth, the fiscal gap essentially closes. The piece is careful to note this is about fundamental value, not market pricing — but it's a concrete quantitative argument for why AI productivity gains have direct implications for monetary policy and sovereign debt.

🧬 Health & Science

There Is No Safe Gamble with High LDL Cholesterol

Attia directly takes on the "lean mass hyper-responder" (LMHR) phenotype argument — the claim that some people on carnivore/low-carb diets can have very high LDL without elevated cardiovascular risk because their particle behavior is different. His position: the lipid energy model lacks sufficient causal evidence to override the cumulative data on LDL's role in atherosclerosis, and the LMHR framing is being used to rationalize ignoring a known risk factor. Worth reading if you follow the carnivore/low-carb debate closely, as this is a direct rebuttal to a popular counternarrative in that community.

Peter Attia

Hold Still: What Does the Evidence Actually Say About Isometric Training?

Stronger by Science's deep dive on isometric training challenges the conventional wisdom that it's inferior for strength and hypertrophy. The piece reviews the mechanistic and empirical literature and argues the evidence doesn't support the assumption — isometrics may be more comparable to dynamic training than commonly believed, particularly for certain force angles and injury rehab contexts. Worth reading if you're programming seriously for hypertrophy or strength and have dismissed isometrics out of hand.

Thinking Scientifically: Why It's Hard, Why It Matters

Attia's framework episode on scientific reasoning — the core framing: "The goal of thinking scientifically is not to be right. It's to be less wrong over time." Less about specific findings and more about epistemics and how to evaluate health claims — useful if you're trying to navigate the carnivore/seed oil/LDL debates without getting anchored to any camp.

💡 Worth Reading

The Brand Age

Paul Graham's latest essay — his argument is that we're entering an era where brand (trust, identity, reputation) matters more than it did in the commodity internet era, because AI makes it trivially easy to produce competent output, so differentiation shifts to who you trust to be right rather than who produces the thing. Worth reading as a mental model for how value accrual shifts in an AI-saturated information environment.

Long-Term Money

Morgan Housel opens with Adam Smith's observation that Scottish highland mothers routinely lost most of their children, using it as a lens on how short human time horizons are compared to the timescales that compound returns require. A short but well-constructed piece on patience, survivorship, and what "long-term" actually demands psychologically — more substance than the title suggests.
