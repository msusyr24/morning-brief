🎥 From People I Follow

Worth your time:

Nick Norwitz

Norwitz covered a new primate study (40-month interventional trial in middle-aged monkeys) showing that Vitamin C at ~30mg/kg/day reduced iron accumulation in aging cells, suppressed the ACSL4 enzyme he calls the "aging catalyst switch," and measurably prevented brain shrinkage on MRI — at a cost of ~$0.06/day for a human equivalent dose of ~2.1g. The mechanism: iron accumulates in cells with age, triggering free radical damage; Vitamin C blocks the upstream enzyme and boosts antioxidant defenses simultaneously. He recommends split-dosing across 3–4 meals to improve absorption and avoid GI distress, or ~700mg liposomal Vitamin C as an alternative. Separately, he self-experimented with retatrutide (GLP-3, a triple-agonist hitting GLP-1, GIP, and glucagon receptors), noting it raised his resting heart rate, body temperature, and hunger — yet he lost weight — suggesting his primary effect was metabolic rate elevation via the glucagon axis rather than appetite suppression. He also ran a blinded crossover statin experiment (atorvastatin 80mg vs. placebo) and found clear performance impairment and leg pain during the statin phase, theorizing mitochondrial Complex 4 inhibition and CoQ10 depletion as mechanisms, and arguing the burden of proof in pharmacology should not be "innocent until proven guilty."

Thomas DeLauer

DeLauer put out a cluster of content around cortisol, fasting, and fat storage. Key claims: (1) A Physiology Reports study found that high-intensity interval training performed 14+ hours into a fast is a "cortisol bomb" — researchers found increased myostatin (muscle growth inhibitor), increased apoptosis, and reduced muscle fiber quality; his rule is no high-intensity work past 14 hours fasted, and if you must, terminate with 20–30g of simple carbs to blunt the cortisol response via the insulin-hypothalamus-adrenal axis. (2) A 40,000-scan MRI Nature Communications study confirmed visceral fat (not subcutaneous) drives cardiometabolic risk — and hip fat may be protective — with specific villains being trans fats, ultra-processed foods, soda (fructose-driven visceral partitioning via cortisol/glucocorticoid receptor activation), fruit juice (often higher fructose % than soda), and alcohol (beer worst; women store more visceral fat from alcohol than men). (3) On fat type and longevity, a rodent study in Journals of Gerontology found lard (saturated + monounsaturated fats) outperformed fish oil for lifespan extension under 40% caloric restriction — his take: polyunsaturated fats oxidize easily in the body, while stable fats (olive oil, avocado oil, tallow) preserve antioxidant capacity for actual aging defense rather than cleaning up oxidized-fat damage.

🧬 Health & Science

Hold Still: What Does the Evidence Actually Say About Isometric Training for Strength and Hypertrophy?

Stronger by Science reviews the evidence on isometric training (holding a fixed position under load) and argues the conventional wisdom — that it's clearly inferior to dynamic training for strength and hypertrophy — isn't well-supported by the data. The piece explores the specific conditions under which isometrics are competitive or potentially superior, and what the evidence actually shows about joint-angle specificity, motor unit recruitment, and practical programming applications.

Stronger by Science

Reducing Cardiovascular Risk: A Playbook for Lipid-Lowering Pharmacotherapy

Peter Attia's team lays out a risk-stratified, six-step decision framework for choosing, combining, and escalating lipid-lowering drugs — statins, ezetimibe, PCSK9 inhibitors, bempedoic acid, and others. The piece is practical and hierarchy-driven: it tells you which drug class to reach for first at different risk levels, when to combine, and what targets to aim for in LDL-C and ApoB. Useful reference if you're managing your own lipid panel or evaluating a doctor's recommendations.

Peter Attia MD

Retatrutide's Differential Effects by Metabolic State (Nick Norwitz)

Norwitz makes a mechanistically interesting argument for why retatrutide (GLP-3) clinical trial data — showing ~38% of weight loss comes from lean mass in obese subjects — may not apply to lean, metabolically healthy people. The hypothesis: retatrutide's glucagon axis upregulates FGF-21 from the liver, which is muscle-sparing and fat-burning; but obesity is an FGF-21 resistant state. Therefore lean individuals may experience a qualitatively different response (more fat loss, more muscle preservation) than what clinical trials in obese populations show. Not proven, but mechanistically plausible and worth tracking as the drug moves through development.
