🧬 Health & Science

Hold Still: What Does the Evidence Actually Say About Isometric Training for Strength and Hypertrophy?

Stronger by Science does a deep dive into isometric training — the long-held assumption that it's inferior to dynamic training for strength and hypertrophy doesn't hold up under scrutiny. The current evidence suggests isometrics can produce comparable gains to dynamic training when volume and effort are matched, and they may have underexplored advantages for tendon health and injury rehab. Worth reading if you do any barbell or resistance training and haven't thought carefully about where isometrics fit in programming.

It's Possible to Learn in Our Sleep. Should We?

New research covered by The New Yorker suggests people can not only communicate during lucid dreams but potentially practice skills while dreaming — with measurable real-world transfer. The piece raises the obvious follow-on question about whether we should deliberately engineer sleep for learning, and what the tradeoffs are for sleep quality and natural recovery. Sits at a genuinely interesting intersection of neuroscience, performance, and ethics.

💡 Worth Reading

The Brand Age — Paul Graham

Graham argues we're entering an era where brand — trust, reputation, and perceived values — matters more than ever as product quality becomes harder to differentiate in a world of AI-generated abundance. His thesis is that when anyone can produce competent work, the signal shifts entirely to who you are and whether people trust you. Short, clean, worth sitting with if you're thinking about how to position yourself or your work.

Paul Graham

Long-Term Money — Morgan Housel

Housel uses the historical observation that Scottish Highland mothers commonly bore 20 children and watched most die as a launching point for thinking about how radically short human time horizons are — and how truly long-term thinking requires internalizing compounding across timescales we're psychologically bad at feeling. Classic Housel: a historical detail that opens into a bigger idea about money, patience, and human nature.

What Deontological Bars? — Astral Codex Ten

Scott Alexander examines the question of whether AI models should have hard ethical limits ("deontological bars") that can't be overridden by consequences — and whether those bars are coherent or secretly just utilitarian calculus in disguise. Relevant both as philosophy and as a practical question about how AI systems get trained to refuse things. Good for anyone interested in ethics or how AI companies think about alignment.

Astral Codex Ten

Socrates's Pupils (Parts I & II) — Massimo Pigliucci

Pigliucci traces how the same teacher — Socrates — inspired wildly divergent philosophical schools: the hedonists (Aristippus and the Cyrenaics), the ascetics (Antisthenes and the Cynics), and the rationalists who led to Plato and eventually Stoicism. Part I covers the divergence; Part II focuses on how pleasure and logic pulled students in opposite directions. If you're interested in where Stoicism came from and how it differentiated itself from its siblings, this is the intellectual genealogy.

When Reading About Stoicism Isn't Enough — Donald Robertson

Robertson makes a distinction worth sitting with: intellectual knowledge of Stoic philosophy and actually practicing it under pressure are completely different skills, and reading more books doesn't close that gap. The argument is that philosophy requires something more like coaching or deliberate behavioral rehearsal — the Stoics called it askesis — to actually change how you respond to adversity. Short but pointed.

New Results on AI Mental Health Therapists — Marginal Revolution

A randomized controlled trial of 1,964 Mexican women with mild-to-severe depression tested an AI-powered mental health app against a control group. Tyler Cowen flags the results — the piece is worth reading for the actual effect sizes and what it implies about AI therapy's real-world viability (and risks of crowding out human care). One of the first large-scale RCTs in this space.

Eka's Robotic Claw: A ChatGPT Moment for Robotics?

Wired profiles Eka's robotic gripper and argues we may be approaching a threshold moment for physical AI — where robotic manipulation suddenly becomes "good enough" to generalize across unstructured real-world tasks, similar to how GPT-3 unlocked language applications.
