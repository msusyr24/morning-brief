Five Whys

Toyota's technique: when a problem surfaces, ask 'why?' five times. Each answer gets you closer to root cause. The first answer is always a symptom, not a cause. Munger uses this in business analysis: 'Why is this company losing money?' → five layers deep, you usually find a cultural or strategic issue, not an operational one.

🎥 From People I Follow

Worth your time:

GTO Wizard

Solid PLO study methodology video built around what they call the SIP method: Simplify, Isolate (the 80/20 spots), and find Patterns that transfer across situations. The core insight is that memorizing solver outputs combo-by-combo is a losing approach — you need heuristics that work at the table. Concrete examples include: EP/CO/BTN opening ranges share enough overlap that CO is a useful anchor; "when in doubt open" for BTN, "when in doubt fold" for EP. For flop sizing, they split boards into "locked" (paired, monotone, straight) → use 1/3 or 1/4, and "regular" → default half-pot. The most useful tactical point: instead of writing down every group's frequency, look only for where strategy *changes* — those threshold spots (e.g., pair + open-ended always continues; pair + gutshot is the threshold; bare pair folds) are the only things worth memorizing.

Jared Alderman (feat. Kevin Rabichow)

Short but genuinely useful exchange on a subtle concept: when, if ever, does it make sense to deviate from GTO to "gather information"? Rabichow's position is that information-seeking deviations belong in the checking range, not the betting range — you don't bet thinner to see what villain calls with. His specific example: J6cc on 865r, check-back, turn 5 pairs the board — he thinks checking is default but a thin bet could outperform against a specific aggressive archetype. Alderman's pushback: if the bet works, villain just folds, so what did you actually learn? The implicit lesson is that exploit-seeking bets are often just thinly-rationalized spew dressed up as "information."

Paul Saladino MD

Saladino systematically attacks the observational epidemiology basis for the "red meat kills you" claim, centering on the 2012 Harvard Nurses Health Study. His key methodological critique: the red-meat-eating cohort in that study smoked more, drank more, exercised less, and had more baseline chronic disease — classic unhealthy user bias — making it impossible to isolate the meat signal. He cites several counterweights: the UK Shopper Study (similar mortality when healthy behaviors are controlled), the Australian 45-and-Up Study (267k people, no difference across meat-eaters, vegans, vegetarians), the 2019 NutriRECS guidelines (no reason to stop eating red meat), the PURE Study (134k people across 21 countries, no correlation for unprocessed red meat), and a 2025 Chinese case-control study finding vegans/vegetarians less likely to reach age 100. His mechanistic case for red meat as a longevity food rests on six compounds largely absent from plants: taurine, anserine, creatine, carnosine, B12, and 4-hydroxyproline — all of which appear in high-end longevity supplement stacks.

Stronger by Science: Hypertrophy–Strength Correlation Revisited

A new analysis suggests hypertrophy contributes more to strength gains in novice lifters than prior studies indicated — but the apparent correlation is inflated by methodological artifacts, so the relationship is still nowhere near deterministic. The practical implication is that beginners probably shouldn't optimize hypertrophy and strength as entirely separate goals, but experienced lifters still need to treat them as largely distinct adaptations requiring different programming emphasis. No link in the RSS but worth reading the full piece for the specific study critiques.

Is the Correlation Between Hypertrophy and Strength Gains Stronger Than We Realized? — Stronger by Science

Peter Attia: Lung Cancer Screening in Never-Smokers

Attia argues that low-dose CT lung cancer screening is a rational personal choice for never-smokers even though it's not guideline-endorsed, based on the evidence — particularly relevant given that lung cancer in never-smokers is the 5th or 6th most common cancer death in the U.S. The core argument is that the screening benefit-to-harm ratio still holds outside the high-risk smoking population when baseline prevalence is sufficient. Worth reading if you're thinking about your own screening protocol.

Lung cancer screening in never-smokers: the data — Peter Attia MD

Thomas DeLauer: Omega-3/6 Ratio and Skin Inflammation

Covers a study in Nutrients in which psoriasis patients who increased omega-3 intake and eliminated seed oils saw significant reductions in lesion severity alongside drops in CRP and TNF. The mechanism: omega-6-rich diets load cell membranes with arachidonic acid (precursor to pro-inflammatory eicosanoids), while EPA/DHA displacement produces resolvins, protectins, and maresins — which don't just block inflammation but actively resolve it. The gut-skin axis piece cites a Frontiers in Microbiology review linking gut dysbiosis to impaired skin immunity and sebaceous gland dysfunction, with probiotic interventions (kefir, fermented dairy) improving skin barrier proteins in animal models. The insulin resistance → acne pathway is also covered with a large RCT showing low-glycemic diets reduce acne lesions.

EFF piece on a California proposal to require 3D printers to embed serialized watermarks in everything they print, creating a surveillance infrastructure for physical manufacturing analogous to what printer steganography already does for documents. Ultrasound Imaging of the Brain

A substantive overview of functional ultrasound neuroimaging (fUS), which offers temporal and spatial resolution between fMRI and EEG at a fraction of the cost and with portability advantages. The technology has been used in awake, behaving animals and is beginning to reach human clinical contexts. If this scales, it could democratize brain imaging research significantly — the kind of foundational capability shift worth tracking.

This Darkness Is Not That Darkness

Donald Robertson on a forgotten precursor to CBT — rational emotive approaches used with WWII veterans — that helped soldiers distinguish present fears from past trauma. The title is the therapeutic insight itself: the fear you feel now is not the same as what happened then. Stoically resonant and practically useful for anyone working on emotional regulation or trauma-informed thinking.

Strangers, Gods, and Bombs: Why We Keep Killing Each Other (Part II)
