Principle-Agent Problem

When one party (agent) acts on behalf of another (principal), their incentives rarely perfectly align. Lawyers bill by the hour, not by outcome. CEOs optimize for short-term stock price, not long-term company health. The discipline: in any relationship, ask whose incentives actually match yours.

Worth your time:

Jared Alderman

Alderman interviews Charlie (a high-stakes pro) on what he believes is poker's most underexploited edge: live tells and psychological self-knowledge. Charlie argues that GTO theory has democratized strategic knowledge so thoroughly that the real remaining edge is understanding your opponent's inner state — and your own. His specific thesis: within 5 years, high-stakes players will master categorized live tells (observable, teachable patterns around timing and eye movement that compress opponent ranges); within 15, the psychological/spiritual layer (recognizing emotional triggers, staying balanced after a bad beat or personal distraction) will be the frontier. Both agree that the ability to "feel someone's feelings" at the table — knowing not just what they hold but what emotional state is driving their decisions — is currently rare, severely undervalued, and very hard to hide. Charlie also notes a perverse incentive: he charges tens of thousands privately for his best live-tell knowledge precisely because sharing it publicly would dilute the edge.

Technological Unemployment in Victorian Britain

A new paper attempts to quantify — using 170 million individual records from the full-count British census — how many jobs were actually destroyed vs. created by the Industrial Revolution's "creative destruction." The methodology is novel and the question is directly relevant to AI displacement debates: what actually happened last time we went through a comparable technological disruption?

The Average Is Over Generation?

New data from both the UK and US shows that today's young graduates are simultaneously over-represented at both the top and the bottom of the earnings distribution relative to expectations — more likely to be in the top quartile AND more likely to be at the bottom than historical cohorts. The "hourglass economy" thesis in empirical form. Relevant to thinking about AI's distributional effects on labor.
