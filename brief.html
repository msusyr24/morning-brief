Contrast Misreaction

We judge things relative to recent comparisons, not absolute values. A $2,000 suit feels cheap after you've committed to a $30,000 car. Real estate agents show you a worse house first. Munger: 'If you're selling something, show the dud first.' If you're buying, beware of contrast-engineered comparisons.

Renaissance Periodization (Dr. Mike)

Dr. Mike made a rare and genuinely useful case against teen steroid use — not the usual sanitized warnings, but three specific mechanisms worth knowing: (1) high estrogen from steroid conversion causes early growth plate closure, potentially costing inches of adult height; (2) steroids during adolescence can permanently impair prefrontal cortex development, reducing impulse control and executive function for life; (3) androgen/estrogen imbalance causes erectile dysfunction both on-cycle and during post-cycle transitions. His actual recommendation: wait until ~25, build as much natural muscle as possible first (which makes steroids more effective later), and note that drugs currently in the pipeline may offer 2x the muscle-building effect with 1/10 the side effects within that timeframe. Separately, he made a clean, evidence-based case that diet soda is simply better than regular soda for weight management — artificial sweeteners are well-studied and safe, while liquid calories from sugar produce no satiety signal, making it easy to drink 800 calories/day without feeling full.

Peter Attia

Attia's conversation with neurologist/psychiatrist Dr. Gayatri Devi covers a genuinely striking shift in Alzheimer's thinking: Devi now believes Alzheimer's patients can actually get better — something she never believed 10 years ago, and which contradicts the conventional teaching that decline is inextricable. She argues the disease is a spectrum disorder (like autism), not binary, and that neuroinflammation may precede amyloid deposition by years or decades, making anti-inflammatory interventions in your 30s and 40s potentially disease-modifying. Practically, she highlighted the shingles vaccine and suppressive HSV treatment as meaningfully reducing dementia risk through inflammatory pathways, and noted that gum disease (via proximity-driven inflammation) is an underappreciated risk factor. She also emphasized that high-functioning individuals can score 28-30 on standard cognitive tests (MMSE/MoCA) even 10-15 years into the disease due to cognitive reserve, making rigorous specialized testing essential for early detection.

🧬 Health & Science

Alzheimer's: Neuroinflammation, Early Intervention, and the Possibility of Reversal

Covered above in the Attia YouTube summary, but the podcast episode goes deeper. Key clinical takeaways beyond the video: Devi uses electrophysiology (EEG for early brain slowing), transcranial Doppler (regional blood flow), and advanced MRI techniques alongside hours-long cognitive testing — standard screening tools miss high-functioning patients for over a decade. She also presented two pairs of siblings where one sibling (including one APOE4 homozygote — the highest genetic risk) shows zero amyloid pathology in their 70s, hypothesized to be related to decades of immune-modulating treatment. Her overall framing: we're at a "penicillin moment" for dementia, with personalized drug cocktails now available that can actually alter disease course.

Is the Correlation Between Hypertrophy and Strength Gains Stronger Than We Realized?

Stronger by Science examines new research suggesting muscle growth contributes more to strength gains in novice lifters than prior studies indicated — but with an important caveat: the relationship looks stronger than it is because both variables are simultaneously driven by training volume and intensity, inflating the apparent correlation. The practical upshot is that hypertrophy and strength training are more complementary for beginners than previously thought, but the underlying mechanism isn't simply "bigger muscle = stronger" in a clean linear way.

Does Losing Sleep Mean Losing Lifespan?

Attia's team reviews a new sleep meta-analysis and correctly notes the limits of what observational data can tell us — confounders (illness causing poor sleep vs. poor sleep causing illness) make causal inference nearly impossible. The practical conclusion: the uncertainty doesn't change the recommendation, since sleep hygiene interventions carry minimal downside and plausible upside across multiple mechanisms.

💡 Worth Reading

Stoicism Is Not Unemotional

Donald Robertson pushes back on the popular misconception that Stoicism demands emotional suppression — the Stoics distinguished between unhealthy passions (driven by false judgments about what's truly good or bad) and healthy, appropriate emotions (eupatheiai) that a wise person naturally feels. Marcus Aurelius himself grieved, expressed affection, and experienced real frustration; the goal was never to become, as Robertson puts it, a man made of oak or stone. This is practically important: if you're reading Stoicism as permission to go numb, you're misreading it.

Donald Robertson / Substack

Beyond the Inner Citadel — Ludwig Edelstein and the Limits of Self-Help Philosophy

Massimo Pigliucci examines classicist Ludwig Edelstein's critique of Stoicism's inward focus: that the "inner citadel" framing (your judgments are always within your control) can slide into a kind of philosophical quietism that ignores the social and political dimensions of a good life. Pigliucci takes this seriously rather than dismissing it, arguing that Stoicism's full picture — including the cosmopolitan and civic duties — is richer than the self-help distillation most people encounter. Worth sitting with if you're engaging seriously with Marcus or Epictetus.

Figs in Winter / Substack

The Hard Problem Is a Category Mistake

Pigliucci argues that the "hard problem of consciousness" — why there is subjective experience at all — is not a genuine explanatory gap but a category mistake, similar to the 19th-century "vital force" debate in biology (which dissolved once we understood biochemistry, not by explaining the force but by showing the question was malformed). If he's right, the hard problem isn't waiting for a scientific solution; it's waiting to be dissolved by better conceptual framing. Genuinely interesting philosophy of mind, not just academic wordplay.

Figs in Winter / Substack

Creating Your Own Religion in an AI-Drenched World

Tyler Cowen flags the observation (from a Fable 5 discussion) that AI will soon make it trivially easy to construct personalized religious frameworks — not necessarily replacing existing religions, but enabling highly individualized "accretions" onto them. The deeper question being gestured at: what happens to shared meaning-making institutions when belief systems become as customizable as playlists? Short post, but the implication is worth chewing on.
