Mental Model · Physics/Systems

Breakpoints

Systems often behave smoothly until a threshold, then change discontinuously. Water at 99°C looks like water at 99°C looks like water at 99°C. Then at 100°C, it's steam. Business analogue: a company can look fine until a cash flow breakpoint is crossed, then collapse in weeks. Watch for thresholds, not trends.

🧬 Health & Science

Hold Still: What Does the Evidence Actually Say About Isometric Training for Strength and Hypertrophy?

Stronger by Science challenges the conventional wisdom that isometric (static hold) training is inferior to dynamic, full-range-of-motion work for building strength and muscle. The piece digs into the actual evidence and argues it doesn't support that dismissal — isometrics can meaningfully contribute to both strength and hypertrophy when programmed appropriately. Worth reading if you're looking for underutilized tools in a training block or working around joint issues.

Stronger by Science

Peter Attia AMA #85: A Guide to Medications and Supplements — What to Take, Skip, and How to Know If It's Working

Attia's framing here is the real value: "Poor problem definition almost guarantees some sort of false positive." The episode is structured around how to actually evaluate whether something is working — including how to set up personal N=1 experiments, how to think about base rates and placebo, and why most people take supplements without ever defining what outcome they're measuring. Practical and rigorous in a way most supplement content isn't.

💡 Worth Reading

What We Lost When We Stopped Letting Kids Leave the Front Yard

Steve Magness — who writes seriously about performance, resilience, and the psychology of pressure — argues that the modern impulse to eliminate all risk from childhood is producing adults with fragile stress-response systems and an inability to self-regulate under adversity. The case isn't nostalgic; it's developmental: unsupervised play is where kids actually build the internal experience of handling fear, failure, and uncertainty without a safety net. Directly relevant to anyone thinking about how resilience is built (or stunted) from the ground up.

Taking a Walk May Lead to More Creativity Than Sitting (2014)

A Stanford study resurfaces on HN: walking — even on a treadmill facing a blank wall — produced significantly more divergent thinking than sitting. The effect was consistent and immediate. Old study, but practically useful as a reminder that your environment and physical state aren't neutral when you're trying to think through a hard problem.

How Anyone Can Think Like a Roman Emperor

Donald Robertson — the cognitive therapist and author of How to Think Like a Roman Emperor — explores Stoicism and what he calls "Majesty as a State of Mind": the idea that Marcus Aurelius didn't cultivate authority through power but through an internalized sense of dignity and equanimity that was independent of circumstances. The practical angle is how this translates to modern emotional regulation — carrying yourself under pressure not through suppression but through a trained internal orientation.

The Evolution of Consciousness

Massimo Pigliucci (philosopher of science, Stoic) takes on whether consciousness is a biological phenomenon and, if so, how and why it evolved — one of the genuinely hard questions at the intersection of philosophy and neuroscience. Not a pop-science summary; Pigliucci engages with the explanatory gap and the competing frameworks seriously. Good thinking-about-thinking territory.
