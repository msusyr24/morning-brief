🎥 From People I Follow

Worth your time:

Nick Norwitz

Norwitz self-experimented with retatrutide ("GLP-3"), the next-generation weight loss drug that hits three hormonal pathways simultaneously: GLP-1 (appetite suppression), GIP (fuel partitioning), and glucagon (energy expenditure). That third mechanism is the key differentiator — it causes your body to burn more, not just eat less. His resting heart rate jumped from the low 40s to 65-70 bpm, his core temperature rose 1.2°F, and he lost 12 lbs in a month while eating 3,000+ calories/day. The widely circulated claim that retatrutide spares muscle is not supported by published human data — about 38% of weight loss on reta appears to be lean mass at higher doses, consistent with earlier GLP-1 drugs. The paradox of some users reporting increased hunger is explained by the glucagon-driven metabolic boost hitting before the appetite suppression, creating a window of elevated hunger while still losing fat.

GTO Wizard

Tom Boss 21 walks Joey Ingram through the unified math behind pot odds, MDF, and alpha — arguing these are all expressions of the same underlying principle: on the river, the frequency you bluff must equal the pot odds you're laying your opponent, or your strategy is exploitable in one direction. The key demonstration via node-locking: shifting your bluffing frequency by even a few combos moves all bluff-catchers from indifferent to always-call or always-fold — the river is extremely sensitive. The practical implication is that most players can't detect whether an opponent is bluffing 35% vs. 41% in real time, which is why studying the conditions under which specific hands become calls or folds matters more than trying to count combos at the table.

Video: The Math Behind Every Profitable Poker Decision

Jared Alderman

Alderman frames mental performance through a "causes and conditions" lens borrowed from contemplative practice: concentration, calm, and clarity aren't character traits you either have or don't — they're outputs of conditions you can learn to create. The practical application to poker (and life) is that studying your own mind the way you study a solver — observing what inputs produce what outputs — is a skill that compounds. He credits this framework, not willpower, for transforming what he describes as a chaotic, ADHD-affected life into one with consistent discipline.

Video: Unlock Your Mind: Master Causes, Conditions, and Inner Physics

Peter Attia

Attia's breast cancer screening episode makes a specific, actionable argument: population guidelines (especially USPSTF's biennial mammography starting at 40) are optimized for societal cost, not individual outcomes. His framework: every woman should have a formal risk assessment by age 25, annual mammography should start at 40 for average-risk women, and MRI should be added for high-risk women — yet despite 9% of women meeting published MRI criteria, actual MRI utilization is only 0.4%, which he calls a pure execution failure. Stage at detection is everything: stage 1 breast cancer has 96% 10-year survival vs. ~30% 5-year survival at stage 4. Dense breasts matter both as a risk factor and as a reason mammography underperforms — density is ~65% heritable, so family history is a partial proxy before you've had imaging.

Video: 396 – Breast cancer screening: understanding risk, deciding when to start, and more

Thomas DeLauer

DeLauer covers a dietary protocol showing 92% improvement in fatty liver outcomes, framing the root cause as two interlocking loops: insulin resistance drives hepatic fat accumulation, and that fat accumulation drives inflammation, which worsens insulin signaling. Phase 1 interventions: eliminate high-fructose corn syrup (which bypasses normal fat metabolism via direct portal vein delivery to the liver), introduce omega-3s (a meta-analysis of 577 NAFLD patients across 10 RCTs showed significant reductions in liver fat and GGT), and add 135 minutes/week of light aerobic exercise (clinically significant liver fat reduction). Phase 2 adds high protein intake, resistance training, and time-restricted eating to accelerate liver fat clearance through GLP-1 activation and improved mitochondrial function.

ModernWisdom (Arthur Brooks)

Brooks argues phone addiction follows the same behavioral recovery structure as other addictions: (1) you have to get genuinely angry at being subjugated by the behavior — without the spirit of rebellion, you won't quit; (2) you need a concrete algorithm to stop, not just willpower; (3) hardest of all, you have to learn to be comfortable alone with yourself again, since the addiction was a flight from your own company. His practical protocols: no phone for the first and last hour of the day, never eat with a device (oxytocin neuropeptide flow during meals is how humans evolved kin bonds), phone never in the bedroom, and 96-hour annual phone fasts which research suggests can actually break the dependency relationship.

Peter Attia: Breast Cancer Screening Framework

Covered in YouTube section above, but the written companion piece is worth reading separately for the full risk factor breakdown and comparison table of organizational guidelines. Key data: women who screen regularly are up to 40% less likely to die from breast cancer; at least 9% of women qualify for breast MRI under existing guidelines but only 0.4% actually receive it. The framework: risk assessment at 25, annual mammography at 40, MRI for anyone with lifetime risk above 20% (calculable via tools like Tyrer-Cuzick). Dense breast tissue (present in ~50% of screening-age women) both increases risk modestly and reduces mammography sensitivity — the two-for-one problem that makes supplemental imaging most valuable for that group.

Beyond the mammogram: a framework for smarter breast cancer screening — Peter Attia
