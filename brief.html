Lollapalooza Effect

Munger's own coinage for when multiple biases converge on the same outcome. Example: auction fever combines commitment bias, social proof, contrast bias, and deprival super-reaction — all pushing the bidder the same way. When you're making a decision and several biases happen to agree, be most suspicious, not least.

The Sigmoids Won't Save You

Scott Alexander's Astral Codex Ten tackles the argument that AI capability growth will naturally slow via sigmoid curves before anything dangerous happens — the "don't worry, it'll plateau" position. The piece appears to challenge the epistemic comfort people take from historical S-curves, examining whether there are principled reasons to expect a safety-giving plateau at the right time or whether that's wishful pattern-matching. Where Did the Stoic God Come From?

Massimo Pigliucci (Figs in Winter) traces the philosophical genealogy of the Stoic conception of God — not a personal deity but a rational, immanent principle (the Logos) running through all of nature. Understanding this is actually important for reading Stoic ethics clearly: their prescriptions about "living according to nature" and the cosmopolitan brotherhood of all rational beings are grounded in this specific theology, which came from a synthesis of Heraclitus and Socratic thought.

Figs in Winter (Pigliucci)

We've Made the World Too Complicated

A short essay arguing that complexity has become self-perpetuating across institutions, software, and daily life — not because complexity solves problems but because it signals effort, expertise, and defensibility. The core claim is that simplicity requires more courage than complexity because it exposes your thinking directly. Worth a read as a mental model check: how much of the complexity in your own work is load-bearing vs. defensive?
