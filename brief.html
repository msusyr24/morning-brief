GTO Wizard

GTO Wizard launched PLO (Pot-Limit Omaha) solver support, and Tombos21 walked through the key mindset shifts a Texas Hold'em player needs. The central insight: in PLO, hand strength right now is almost irrelevant — what matters is nut potential by the river, blockers on later streets, and reverse blockers for bluffs. Tombos showed concretely that in a 4-bet pot, 86% of the range is aces, which means KK is often a fold — a result that would be unthinkable in NLHE. Joey Ingram added that calling pre-flop is correct far more often than 3-betting in PLO because equities run close and you'd rather play in position than inflate pots OOP. The PLO trainer and drill tools are now live inside GTO Wizard.

Jared Alderman

Alderman's guest (Melanie, an experienced tournament pro) makes a sharp observation: most players treat a poker hand like a performance — where study is practice and the hand is the test — when in reality the hand is the practice. This is the root of the chasm between who people think they are as players and who they actually show up as under pressure. Alderman connects this to his coaching work: the delta between a player's best possible performance and their actual output is enormous, and most people never even measure it because their results are "good enough." He extends this to trading: he worked informally with trader friends who had zero process around tilt and emotion, and estimated one saved ~$500K over six months from applying basic poker mental-game discipline. The episode is really about how poker's decision-making frameworks — probabilistic thinking, process over results, emotional regulation — are undervalued and underexported to other high-stakes domains.

🧬 Health & Science

New drug 'functionally cures' many hepatitis B virus infections

A new drug achieves functional cure of hepatitis B in a meaningful proportion of patients — a disease that chronically infects ~300 million people worldwide and that existing antivirals suppress but cannot clear. "Functional cure" means sustained loss of surface antigen (HBsAg) after stopping treatment, which is the clinical benchmark for clearing infection. Notable given that HBV is a leading cause of hepatocellular carcinoma.

Science.org · HN Discussion

Peter Attia + Layne Norton: Is Industrial Processing the Real Problem With Seed Oils?

Norton walks through the hexane extraction chemistry in detail: residual hexane in finished seed oils is typically 0.05–0.5 parts per million, hexane toxicity risk is from inhalation not ingestion, and the amount of oil you'd need to consume to approach mild side effects from hexane alone is ~11,000 kg. He separately notes that to get meaningful linoleic acid oxidation from heating soybean oil, you'd need 240°C for 3+ hours — not the brief steam evaporation step in processing. The conversation doesn't resolve the broader seed oil question but effectively deflates the hexane-as-mechanism argument specifically.

Gene therapy targeting PCSK9: a potential one-time lipid intervention

An early-phase trial tests a gene therapy designed to permanently silence PCSK9, the protein that normally degrades LDL receptors — effectively producing a durable, drug-free version of what PCSK9 inhibitor injections do every two weeks. If the safety profile holds, the implication is a single intervention that could provide lifetime LDL reduction, potentially transforming atherosclerosis from a decades-long management problem to something addressable at the root.

Dopamine Fracking

The essay coins a useful phrase: "dopamine fracking" — the way modern products extract dopamine from increasingly marginal or synthetic sources, the way fracking extracts oil from rock that was previously uneconomical to drill. The argument is that attention platforms have essentially industrialized the exploitation of our reward circuitry in ways that deplete the "reserves" needed for genuine motivation and satisfaction. Worth the read for the framing alone.

Might AI hurt corporate profits?

Clifford Sosin's argument via Tyler Cowen: a large portion of corporate margins exist because customers can't be bothered to monitor, compare, or insource — AI removes that friction. If AI makes it cheap for customers to audit, renegotiate, or replicate what vendors provide, competitive moats erode and margins compress across many industries. A counterintuitive macro thesis worth sitting with: AI's productivity gains may accrue to consumers rather than corporations.
