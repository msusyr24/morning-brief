Thomas DeLauer

Two videos worth noting. First, a deep dive on sodium bicarbonate (baking soda) citing a 740-patient RCT in Nefrology: CKD patients supplementing with oral bicarb saw kidney failure progression drop from 17% to 6.6% over 30 months — a ~60% relative reduction — with dialysis rates and mortality also lower. Mechanism: supplemental bicarb restores acid-base balance, reducing the metabolic stress (muscle breakdown, bone mineral loss, inflammation) that accelerates kidney decline. The ISSN has an official position stand on bicarb for exercise performance: 0.3g/kg bodyweight 60–180 minutes pre-workout buffers hydrogen ion accumulation during high-intensity efforts (30 seconds to 12 minutes), with a multi-day loading option (0.4–0.5g/kg/day split over 3–7 days) to avoid GI issues. Second video covers zinc's two distinct mechanisms for testosterone: (1) enabling the enzymatic pathway that synthesizes testosterone — a 20-week zinc restriction study showed dramatic T decline; (2) increasing androgen receptor sensitivity, so the testosterone you already have binds more effectively. A Frontiers in Physiology study found muscle growth correlated more strongly with androgen receptor content than testosterone levels. Practical guidance: food-first (oysters, beef, shellfish), 10–30mg/day supplemental if needed, always pair with copper at a 10:1 ratio.

Thomas DeLauer: Metabolic Jet Lag and Monday Recovery

DeLauer's "metabolic jet lag" video cites real circadian biology worth knowing: a study in Diabetes found that shifting sleep timing (same duration, different timing) doubled insulin resistance and inflammation compared to circadian-aligned sleep. A PNAS study on forced circadian misalignment showed glucose up 6%, insulin up 22%, cortisol rhythm reversal, and some participants reaching pre-diabetic glucose responses — all from timing shifts alone, not calories. His practical Monday protocol: eat breakfast Monday (don't fast — you need to re-anchor circadian cues), do your 18–20 hour fast on Tuesday instead, separate fat intake to morning and carbs to evening on Monday specifically, get morning sunlight immediately, and avoid the common mistake of combining carbs + fats simultaneously when mitochondrial clock genes are already disrupted (he calls this "metabolic gridlock" that accelerates visceral fat storage).

Three Inverse Laws of AI

A short, pointed essay riffing on Asimov's three laws — argued in reverse: the more capable AI becomes, the more it tends to harm humans (through displacement, manipulation, or concentration of power), override human judgment (automation bias, deskilling), and harm itself (alignment problems, reward hacking). The framing is deliberately provocative rather than rigorous, but the piece is a useful counterweight to uncritical AI optimism and surfaces real tensions worth sitting with.

When Everyone Has AI and the Company Still Learns Nothing

The core argument: AI tools accelerate individual output but can actively suppress organizational learning — when everyone uses AI to produce faster, there's less friction, less iteration, and less shared struggle that normally drives institutional knowledge accumulation. The author distinguishes between "AI as productivity tool" and "AI as substitute for thinking," arguing most corporate deployments default to the latter. A sharp essay on second-order effects of AI adoption that most AI coverage ignores.

Physicalism and Consciousness — Part I (Massimo Pigliucci)

Pigliucci (Stoic philosopher, former biologist) opens a series on consciousness arguing that physicalism — the view that everything, including mind, is physical — is the best-supported position, and that the main alternatives (dualism, idealism, panpsychism) fail empirically or explanatorily. He's not dismissing the hard problem but arguing it doesn't require non-physical solutions. Worth reading alongside whatever you're doing on philosophy of mind; Pigliucci writes clearly and doesn't pretend the questions are easy.
