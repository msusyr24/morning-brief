GTO Wizard

A high-stakes PLO reg breaks down the "SIP method" for solver study: Simplify, Isolate signal, find Patterns. The core argument is that memorizing combo-by-combo frequencies is the wrong approach — instead, find threshold groups (the hand strength where strategy changes from pure fold to pure call) and heuristics that transfer across spots. Concrete examples: in early position, "when in doubt, fold"; on the button, "when in doubt, open." For flop sizing, divide all boards into two types — locked boards (monotone, paired, straight-heavy) where nuts don't change much → use small size (1/3 or 1/4 pot); regular boards → default to half pot. The key practical insight: solver mixing is almost never worth agonizing over; only big EV mistakes (20 BB/100+) are worth deep study time.

Jared Alderman

Short but sharp: Uri Peleg on the right question to ask when studying solvers — not "what does the solver do here?" but "why does this need to be here, and what would my opponent exploit if it weren't?" Alderman adds a useful frame: your opponent in any given hand has a fixed strategy in that moment — they can adjust across sessions but not within a hand — so you're not trying to outmaneuver a fully adaptive agent, you're trying to exploit a static response function.

Nick Norwitz

Norwitz covers three separate studies showing low-protein diets (~9% of calories) caused subjects to burn roughly 600 extra calories per day — a ~20% increase in total energy expenditure — with no meaningful muscle loss, replicated across three controlled trials. He then ran a self-experiment: ate 500+ cal/day in surplus on a low-protein diet and lost 6.4 lbs over a few weeks. The mechanistic explanation comes from a new Nature paper: protein restriction reshapes the gut microbiome, which alters secondary bile acid signaling, which activates two simultaneous metabolic pathways (FXR and FGF21 — a "two-key lock") that convert white fat into metabolically active beige fat, increasing thermogenesis. The causal proof: fecal transplants from high-brown-fat humans into mice reproduced the effect; a defined 4-strain bacterial cocktail (HU4) amplified it further. He's explicit he's not advocating low-protein diets — the point is identifying the microbiome mechanism that could theoretically be replicated without sacrificing protein.

Renaissance Periodization

Dr. Mike makes the case for three chronically undertrained muscles: side delts, biceps, and triceps. For side delts: most people default to front-delt-dominant pressing and treat laterals as an afterthought; his fix is train side delts first in the session, 2 exercises × 3–6 sets, 2–4x/week. For biceps: add one stretch-position exercise per session (low incline curls, lying curls) alongside a standard curl, 2–3x/week. For triceps: need three stimulus types — lateral/medial head extensions (skull crushers, pushdowns), long-head overhead extensions (only movement that fully stretches the long head), and close-grip pressing; overhead work and pressing work should alternate across the two weekly sessions. Separately, a short clip confirms the biomechanical case for knees-over-toes in squats: maximum knee flexion = maximum quad stretch under load = maximum hypertrophy stimulus.

ModernWisdom (Alex Hormozi)

Hormozi's core argument: doing hard things physically does not generalize to doing hard things decisively or emotionally — the transfer only happens if you deliberately create an identity label around it ("I am someone who does hard things") that becomes a global behavioral reinforcer. Without that explicit identity bridge, a guy who's been in combat can still be unable to have a hard conversation with his wife. The practical implication: stop chasing the publicly laudable hard thing (marathon, ice bath) as a proxy for the privately important one (the 3-month-delayed decision, the honest conversation). He also flags the "optionality trap" — never making trades because all paths feel like settling — as the place where most people stay stuck longest. The fix: commitment is the elimination of alternatives, and nothing worth doing has ever avoided that cost.

🧬 Health & Science

Peter Attia: GLP-1 Drugs and Muscle Loss — New Data

Attia's AMA addresses the growing concern that GLP-1 agonists (Ozempic/Wegovy class) cause excessive lean mass loss. His updated position: when patients are counseled specifically on protein targets AND resistance training protocol, lean mass loss is minimal. The implication is that the muscle-loss risk is largely a behavioral/protocol failure, not an inherent pharmacological effect — but that the default clinical deployment (prescribe drug, minimal nutritional guidance) is almost certainly producing the bad outcome in most real-world patients. This is a meaningful update to the GLP-1 narrative.

Peter Attia: HDL-C Is Not "Good Cholesterol"

Attia argues that high HDL-C is a poor proxy for cardiovascular protection — the metric conflates HDL particle function (reverse cholesterol transport) with HDL particle count. High HDL-C can coexist with dysfunctional HDL that doesn't actually clear atherogenic particles. The clinical takeaway: HDL-C as a standalone number is misleading; it matters less than the LDL-P/ApoB burden and triglyceride levels.

The trouble with "good cholesterol" · Peter Attia MD

Stronger by Science: Does Hypertrophy Drive Strength Gains More Than We Thought?

A new analysis suggests muscle growth contributes more to strength gains in novice lifters than prior studies indicated — but the relationship is still far from linear or universal. The key caveat: the correlation looks stronger than it is because both variables respond to the same training stimulus, not necessarily because size causes strength. Practical implication: for new lifters, training for hypertrophy and training for strength are more aligned than previously modeled, but experienced lifters still need to distinguish the two.

US Supreme Court Rules Geofence Warrants Require Constitutional Protections

The Supreme Court ruled that geofence warrants — which compel tech companies (primarily Google) to hand over location data for all devices in a geographic area during a time window, then narrow from there — require Fourth Amendment protections. This closes a major surveillance loophole law enforcement has used widely; the practical implication is that the "reverse warrant" technique used in hundreds of criminal investigations becomes significantly harder to execute going forward.
