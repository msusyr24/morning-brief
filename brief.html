Nick Norwitz

Norwitz has LDL near 600 and total cholesterol over 700 — numbers typically associated with a fatal genetic condition — but argues his case is mechanistically different: the "lipid energy model" holds that lean, insulin-sensitive people on ketogenic diets see cholesterol spike as a byproduct of ramped-up fat-based fuel transport, not pathology. He declined statins for 7 years partly because all the evidence for statin benefit comes from metabolically unhealthy populations, and he personally experienced severe exercise impairment on them. The twist: he's now starting two cholesterol-lowering drugs (ezetimibe and bempedoic acid) — not for cardiovascular reasons, but because he carries two APOE4 alleles (10–15x Alzheimer's risk) and new research suggests both drugs may have neuroprotective effects independent of cholesterol lowering. Ezetimibe appears to cross the blood-brain barrier and reduce amyloid buildup and improve autophagy; a human dataset found 8x lower Alzheimer's risk in ezetimibe users. A May 2026 Cell Metabolism paper shows bempedoic acid activates PPARα, shifting amyloid precursor protein processing toward a neuroprotective pathway. His core framing: these are cholesterol drugs by name only — that's just where the spotlight was pointed.

Peter Attia / Layne Norton

Attia and Norton dig into whether industrial processing — not linoleic acid itself — is the real problem with seed oils. Norton walks through the hexane extraction process in detail: seeds are washed with hexane (a nonpolar solvent), then steam is bubbled through to evaporate it off at low temperatures and short durations (~60–90 min), with residual hexane in final oils typically 0.05–0.5 parts per million. Norton's calculation: you'd need to consume 11,340 kg of oil at once to get even mild hexane side effects, hexane doesn't bioaccumulate, and toxicity from hexane is primarily an inhalation issue, not ingestion. He acknowledges the more legitimate concern is the 3x increase in linoleic acid's share of total food availability over the past century (from ~3% to ~10%), which is a consumption-volume argument rather than a processing-harm argument.

Modern Wisdom — Jefferson Fisher

Trial lawyer Jefferson Fisher on why communication breaks down under conflict: the nervous system cannot distinguish social threat from physical danger, so disagreement triggers the same fight-or-flight response as a bear — pupils dilate, jaw clenches, breath stops, and the prefrontal cortex goes offline. His practical points: lead with the conclusion first ("bottom-line up front") to close the anxiety-producing open loop; label difficult conversations explicitly ("this is going to be hard, and I know we can handle it") to emotionally prepare the other person rather than ambushing them; and recognize that facts and evidence rarely change minds — emotional safety is the prerequisite. He highlights Theo Von's moment with Shawn Strickland as a masterclass in "holding space": just saying "we don't have to talk, I can just sit here with you" disarms the need for performance. Connor Beaton's line — "Your emotions aren't too big for me" — as a relationship tool for defusing the fear of being "too much."

Ben Felix

A thorough evidence-based case that personal finance is about funding a good life, not maximizing wealth. Key findings: the correlation between log income and experienced happiness is only 0.09; going from $15K to $250K household income moves the happiness needle about 5 points on a 100-point scale; an approximately 4x income difference produces roughly the same happiness effect as having a headache (yes, less). The 2023 adversarial collaboration between the 2010 (Kahneman) and 2021 (Killingsworth) teams found that happiness does keep rising with income for already-happy people, but plateaus for less-happy people — meaning money amplifies your baseline more than it transforms it. People who explicitly prioritize time over money report higher happiness, stronger marriages, and more meaningful work — despite lower income. He frames good life ingredients through the PERMA-V model: positive emotion, engagement (flow), relationships, meaning, accomplishment, vitality. The "end of history illusion" is a key caution: we chronically underestimate how much our preferences will change, so optimizing hard for a distant future goal risks arriving somewhere your future self doesn't want to be.

Attia covered disappointing results from the first rapamycin-plus-exercise trial, while maintaining his prior conviction in rapamycin as a potential geroprotector — his argument being that a single negative trial in a specific exercise context doesn't overturn the mechanistic and longevity data from other models. NBER working paper using natural labor market experiments finds evidence that employment causally slows cognitive decline — consistent with the "cognitive reserve" literature but now with better identification than cross-sectional comparisons. Pairs interestingly with the HN-trending piece on "cognitive debt" from AI-assisted work.

The Brand Age — Paul Graham

Graham argues we're entering a "Brand Age" — a period where, due to AI-generated content flooding every surface, brand (i.e., trusted identity and reputation) becomes the dominant scarce resource. The implication is that the economics of attention and trust are inverting: low-cost content production makes content worthless, and the bottleneck shifts entirely to who you trust to have produced it. Compact and worth the 5 minutes.

Microsoft Edge Stores All Passwords in Memory in Clear Text, Even When Unused

A security researcher found that Microsoft Edge keeps passwords decrypted in process memory even when the browser is idle and the user isn't actively using saved credentials — meaning any process with memory-read access to Edge can exfiltrate your passwords without triggering any unlock prompt. Not theoretical: practical extraction demonstrated. If you use Edge as a password manager, you should stop.
