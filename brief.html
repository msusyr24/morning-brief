Is the Correlation Between Hypertrophy and Strength Gains Stronger Than We Realized?

Stronger by Science digs into whether muscle growth drives strength gains more than prior meta-analyses suggested. Their finding: for novice and early-intermediate lifters, hypertrophy likely contributes more to strength than older studies indicated — but the apparent correlation is partly a statistical artifact, and the relationship is nowhere near deterministic. The practical implication is nuanced: you can't simply equate muscle size with strength output, especially in trained athletes, where neural and technical factors dominate. Worth reading if you're thinking about programming priorities or interpreting hypertrophy research.

💡 Worth Reading

Stoicism Is Not Unemotional

Donald Robertson pushes back on the popular misconception that Stoicism demands emotional suppression or impassivity — the "man made of oak or stone" caricature. The Stoics distinguished between destructive passions (which cloud judgment) and what they called eupatheiai — good emotional responses like joy, caution, and wishing well — which the sage cultivates rather than eliminates. This matters practically: the goal isn't flatness, it's that your emotions track reality accurately rather than distorting it.

Donald Robertson / Substack

Marcus Aurelius Was Not an Emotionless Stoic

A companion piece from Robertson's biography of Marcus Aurelius, making the specific historical case that the Meditations are not the product of cold detachment — they're a working journal of a man actively struggling with grief, frustration, anxiety, and self-doubt. The Stoic framework was Marcus's tool for processing those emotions, not for eliminating them. A useful corrective if you've been treating Stoicism as a performance of indifference.

Donald Robertson / Substack

Beyond the Inner Citadel — Pigliucci on the Limits of Stoic Self-Help

Massimo Pigliucci draws on Ludwig Edelstein's scholarship to interrogate whether the Stoic "inner citadel" — the idea that your rational faculty is always a refuge — is actually as robust as pop-Stoicism claims. The critique: circumstances (poverty, trauma, neurological condition) can genuinely compromise your capacity for rational self-governance, which means a purely individualist reading of Stoicism may be philosophically incomplete. Worth sitting with if you find the "it's all in your control" framing occasionally too convenient.

Figs in Winter / Substack

Local, CPU-Friendly, High-Quality Text-to-Speech with Kokoro

Kokoro is a text-to-speech model that runs locally on a standard CPU — no GPU, no cloud, no API key — and produces quality comparable to commercial services. The practical upshot: you can now convert any text to natural-sounding audio entirely offline, which has obvious applications for turning articles, notes, or documents into audio without sending data anywhere.
