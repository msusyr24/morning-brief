Nick Norwitz

Norwitz released a substantial deep-dive on mitochondrial health and aging, anchored by a striking Nature Aging paper (July 3, 2026): in trained, fit older adults, 55% of the gene-expression changes normally associated with aging simply didn't occur — and those protected pathways were enriched in mitochondrial and energy-metabolism genes. His takeaway: exercise doesn't just build muscle, it changes the operating system of your mitochondria to preserve youth. He also covers the mitochondrial fusion/fission cycle (regulated by circadian light exposure — morning sunlight acts as the "orchestra conductor"), the ketogenic diet's effect on mitochondrial size and fat-tissue metabolic rate (doubling it, per Ben Bikman's data), and two peptides worth knowing: SS-31 (FDA-approved, stabilizes the inner mitochondrial membrane) and tesamorelin (stimulates pituitary growth hormone release, shown in RCTs to reduce visceral fat ~20%, improve sleep depth, and potentially rejuvenate the thymus — an immune organ increasingly linked to cancer protection and longevity).

Jared Alderman

Two strong pieces this period. The short on session management raises a genuinely useful self-knowledge question: when a decision is close, does getting it wrong in one direction affect you more than the other? Donald Robertson

Robertson pushes back on the popular misconception that Stoicism demands emotional suppression. The Stoics didn't advocate becoming emotionally flat — they distinguished between destructive passions (arising from false judgments) and "good emotions" (eupatheiai) that are appropriate responses to reality. The goal is emotional accuracy, not emotional absence. He also released an interview covering Stoicism, modern psychology, and emotional resilience in practice.

🧬 Health & Science

Mitochondrial Health: Specific Mechanisms and Interventions (Nick Norwitz)

Norwitz synthesized a strong body of recent research into actionable mitochondrial health levers. Key findings worth noting: (1) A Nature Aging paper (July 2026) found that 55% of age-associated muscle gene-expression changes were absent in trained older adults, specifically in mitochondrial pathways — exercise preserves mitochondrial youth at the gene-expression level. (2) Morning sunlight exposure synchronizes mitochondrial fusion/fission cycles via circadian biology; an Oxford study found this dynamic directly regulates sleep drive. (3) Ketogenic diets cause measurable mitochondrial enlargement in fat tissue and more-than-double the metabolic rate of fat tissue in humans in ketosis (Bikman data). (4) Tesamorelin — a peptide that stimulates endogenous growth hormone — reduced visceral fat by ~20% in RCTs, improves sleep architecture, and is being explored for thymic rejuvenation (the thymus regulates immune surveillance for cancer). (5) SS-31, an FDA-approved mitochondrial peptide, stabilizes the inner mitochondrial membrane where ATP is produced. The leaky mitochondria hypothesis — that mitochondrial contents leaking into the cytoplasm trigger autoimmune/inflammatory cascades — is also gaining serious mechanistic backing in Nature.

Hypertrophy-Strength Correlation Revisited (Stronger by Science)

Updated meta-analytic work suggests that muscle growth contributes more to strength gains in novice lifters than prior studies indicated — but the relationship is still far from deterministic, and the visual correlation is stronger than the actual effect size. Practically: early training gains involve both hypertrophy and neural adaptation, with hypertrophy playing a larger share than previously credited, but programming for hypertrophy alone won't fully transfer to strength expression.
